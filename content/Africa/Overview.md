# Africa Overview

## Scope
This section covers religious and spiritual traditions rooted in Africa, along with later transformations through Islam, Christianity, empire, trade, slavery, colonial rule, local continuity, and syncretism.

## Why Africa matters religiously
Africa is not a residual category or a prelude to diaspora religion.
It is one of the foundational religious continents.
This is where you find:
- ancient Egyptian religion and Nile sacred kingship
- diverse indigenous African religious systems
- major West African divine, ancestral, and divinatory worlds
- early and enduring African Christianity
- early and regionally rooted African Islam
- some of the strongest cases of religious continuity through colonial violence and diaspora transformation

A serious library has to treat Africa as primary, not supplementary.

## Major regional clusters
### Nile and Northeast Africa
- ancient Egyptian religion
- sacred kingship, temple systems, funerary religion
- later Christian Ethiopia and Horn of Africa Islam

### West Africa
- divine plurality, ancestor systems, shrine traditions, and powerful divination systems
- Yoruba, Vodun-related, Akan, and many other religious formations
- major source traditions for Afro-diasporic religion in the Americas

### Horn of Africa
- ancient and medieval Christian traditions with deep historical continuity
- Islam as an early, regionally rooted religious force tied to Red Sea exchange and local societies

### African diaspora connections
African traditions do not end at the coast.
Atlantic slavery, colonialism, and displacement transmit and transform African religion into Afro-diasporic forms in the Americas.
That means this shelf links directly with [[../Americas/Overview|Americas]].

## Core themes worth tracking
- ancestors, spirits, and layered sacred presence
- sacred kingship and political legitimacy
- divination as an epistemic system, not decorative superstition
- shrine practice, sacred geography, and ritual specialists
- continuity under suppression
- syncretism under coercive historical conditions
- Christian and Islamic adaptation in African contexts rather than simple import models

## Research lenses that help here
- never flatten "African religion" into one system
- distinguish local tradition, regional formation, and diaspora transformation
- watch how divination, ancestors, land, and political order interlock
- treat Christianity and Islam in Africa as African histories, not just foreign overlays
- take colonial suppression and reinterpretation seriously

## Recommended resources
- John Mbiti — *African Religions and Philosophy*
- Jacob K. Olupona — *African Religions: A Very Short Introduction*
- J. Lorand Matory — Afro-Atlantic and Yoruba-related work
- Stephan Palmié — Afro-diasporic and Atlantic religious scholarship
- Taddesse Tamrat — Ethiopian church and monarchy
- serious regional ethnography and history over one-size-fits-all summaries

## Good working summary
Africa is a primary civilizational zone of religion, not a footnote. Its traditions are diverse, historically deep, materially grounded, and globally important through both local continuity and diaspora transformation.
