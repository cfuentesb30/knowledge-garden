# Indigenous African Religions

## Scope
This note is a high-level entry point into indigenous African religious systems before collapsing them into one generic category.

## Why it matters
African religions are often treated as either background folklore or raw material later overwritten by Christianity and Islam.
That is wrong.
These traditions are historically deep, conceptually structured, socially embedded, and often highly resilient under pressure.

## Important caution
There is no single "African religion."
Africa contains many religious worlds with different cosmologies, ritual structures, deities, ancestors, divination systems, sacred specialists, political relationships, and local sacred geographies.
A serious researcher starts with plurality, not essence.

## Common patterns often found
- strong ancestor veneration
- layered spirit worlds
- ritual specialists and diviners
- religion embedded in community, kinship, and land
- less separation between "religious" and "social" life than in many modern categories
- shrine and sacred-place importance
- strong relation between healing, protection, conflict resolution, and sacred mediation

## Why divination matters
Many African religious systems do not center authority only in fixed scripture.
They may center it in:
- divination
- elders and ritual specialists
- oral tradition
- embodied performance
- sacred objects and local shrines

That does not make them vague. It makes them structured differently.

## Politics and sacred order
In many indigenous African systems, political authority, lineage order, and sacred order overlap.
Kingship, chieftaincy, ancestor legitimacy, shrine prestige, and ritual authority can be mutually reinforcing.

## Historical pressures
- Islamization in many regions
- Christian missionization and empire
- colonial suppression, reframing, and pathologizing of local traditions
- survival through adaptation and syncretism
- modern revival, reinterpretation, and contested identity politics

## Why the category is limited
"Indigenous African religions" is useful as a shelf label, but it can hide real differences.
Where possible, study should move from this broad entry note into regional notes such as [[West African Religious Worlds]] and later other regional clusters.

## Research lenses that help
- do not confuse oral tradition with weak tradition
- distinguish ancestor systems, deity systems, and spirit mediation without separating them artificially
- watch how religion, healing, land, and political order interact
- take colonial categories critically; many of them distort what they describe

## Recommended resources
- John Mbiti — *African Religions and Philosophy*
- Jacob Olupona — African religion studies
- regional anthropology and historical studies instead of one-size-fits-all summaries
- scholarship on colonial religious classification and African continuities

## Good working summary
The right starting point for indigenous African religions is diversity, not generalization. These are complex systems of ancestors, spirits, land, ritual specialists, sacred power, and social order with long continuity and major global influence.
