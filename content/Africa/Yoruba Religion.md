# Yoruba Religion

## Purpose
The religious tradition of the Yoruba people of southwestern Nigeria, Benin, and Togo — one of the most historically significant and globally influential African religious traditions. The source tradition behind Candomblé, Lucumí/Santería, Trinidad Orisha, and Cuban Ifá. For the diaspora traditions, see [[../Americas/Afro-Diasporic Religions Overview|Afro-Diasporic Religions Overview]] and [[../Americas/Vodou|Vodou]].

## Why this tradition matters globally
The Yoruba were one of the largest populations taken in the Atlantic slave trade — millions transported to Brazil, Cuba, Trinidad, and elsewhere from the 18th–19th centuries. They brought their religious system with them. Yoruba religion survives in the Americas in forms so coherent that scholars can trace specific theological and ritual elements back to specific Yoruba sources. Understanding Yoruba religion in its homeland is essential for understanding the Afro-diasporic traditions built from it.

## The Yoruba people
The Yoruba are one of Africa's largest ethnic groups — approximately 40–50 million people in Nigeria (primarily Ogun, Oyo, Ondo, Osun, Ekiti, Lagos states), Benin, and Togo. Historically centered in the Oyo Empire (c. 1300–1836 CE), one of the major pre-colonial West African states. The Oyo Empire's collapse in the early 19th century, combined with Fulani jihad pressure and inter-Yoruba wars, produced massive displacement and a peak in the slave trade — the period when most Yoruba religious knowledge reached the Americas.

## Theological framework

### Olodumare — the supreme deity
*Olodumare* (also *Olorun* — Lord of Heaven) is the supreme deity — omniscient, omnipotent, the source of all existence. Like *Bondye* in Vodou, Olodumare is too exalted for direct human approach; the *orisha* are the intermediaries.

Olodumare has three primary aspects:
- **Olodumare**: The eternal, unchanging ultimate
- **Olorun**: The owner of heaven, source of the sun's life-giving power
- **Olofi**: The aspect through which Olodumare communicates with the orisha and the world

### The orisha
The *orisha* (singular and plural) are the divine forces — aspects of Olodumare's power differentiated and personified as distinct spiritual beings. They are:
- Forces of nature, human experience, and cosmic reality simultaneously
- Each with a specific domain, symbol system, ritual requirements, and personality
- In relationship with human devotees through worship, divination, and possession
- Numbering traditionally 401 (a symbolic number meaning "innumerable")

The orisha are not separate gods in a polytheistic sense — they are understood as differentiated expressions of the one divine power, each revealing a different face of ultimate reality.

### Ase — divine power
*Àṣẹ* (Ashé in diaspora traditions) is the fundamental concept: the divine power, energy, or authority that all things possess to some degree. It is the force that makes things happen; it is what the orisha embody in concentrated form; it is what ritual activates and transmits. Human beings accumulate or deplete *ase* through their actions. Initiated priests and priestesses carry concentrated *ase* — they are *orisha vessels*.

The word has become a liturgical affirmation in African diasporic traditions — "Ashé!" as "Amen!" or "So be it!"

## The major orisha

**Eshu/Elegba/Elegbara** (corresponding to Legba in Vodou): The divine trickster and messenger, guardian of crossroads and thresholds, the conduit between human and divine. No ritual can proceed without first honoring Eshu — he opens the path. He is not evil; he is the principle of communication and change. He can be mischievous but is fundamentally necessary.

**Ogun**: The orisha of iron, war, hunting, and all work with metal. He guards the road and the bush. Invoked by blacksmiths, surgeons, drivers, warriors, and anyone working with metal tools. Associated with the color black and red; his symbols are iron implements. In the diaspora: in Cuba (Santería), he corresponds to St. Peter; in Brazil (Candomblé), St. Anthony or St. George.

**Shango/Sango**: The fourth Alafin (king) of Oyo, deified after death. Orisha of thunder, lightning, justice, and power. He is royal, fierce, and passionate. Associated with the double-headed axe (*oshe*), the color red and white, and drumming. One of the most widely venerated orisha — his presence in the Americas (as Xangô in Candomblé, Changó in Lucumí) is the strongest of any orisha.

**Oya**: The orisha of wind, storms, change, and the gates of death. Shango's most powerful wife. She is associated with the marketplace, the cemetery, change and transformation, and the warrior spirit. In the diaspora: she guards the cemetery entrance, second only to Oya. Associated with the colors dark red/purple/rainbow; her symbol is the flywhisk.

**Oshun**: The orisha of fresh water, love, fertility, beauty, and prosperity. She is sweet, nurturing, but also powerful and dangerous when crossed. She saved humanity by flying to Olodumare as a vulture when all other orisha had failed — the origin of her bird symbol. In the diaspora: Ochún in Cuba (corresponds to Our Lady of Charity of El Cobre, Cuba's patron); Oxum in Candomblé. Associated with yellow and gold; sweet foods (honey, oranges).

**Yemoja/Yemanjá**: Mother of waters (literally "mother whose children are like fish"). Orisha of the ocean and all waters; mother of many orisha; associated with fertility and protection. In the diaspora: *Iemanjá* in Brazil is the most popular orisha — February 2 in Rio de Janeiro sees millions at the beach offering flowers and boats to her. One of the most globally recognizable Afro-diasporic figures.

**Obatala**: The orisha of creation, purity, wisdom, and the human form. Olodumare gave Obatala the task of molding human bodies (Olodumare breathes life into them). He is associated with white — the color of purity and of the divine. He is the eldest orisha, revered for patience and wisdom. Particularly venerated by those with disabilities, the elderly, and people seeking clarity and peace.

**Orula/Orunmila**: The orisha of divination and wisdom, the witness of human fate. He was present at the creation of each person and knows their destiny. His oracle is Ifá — the most complex divination system in the tradition. He does not possess devotees; he works through the divination process.

## Ifá divination — the intellectual core

### What it is
*Ifá* is the Yoruba divination system attributed to Orunmila. It is among the most complex divination systems in the world — a corpus of oral literature (*odù*) organized around 256 configurations, each associated with hundreds of poems, stories, proverbs, and prescriptions.

### How it works
The Ifá priest (*babalawo* — "father of secrets") manipulates either *ikin* (palm nuts) or an *opele* (a divination chain) to produce one of the 256 *odù* configurations. He then recites relevant verses from the *odù* corpus, which the client helps identify through their resonance with the situation. The verse contains: the problem's nature, relevant precedents and stories, and a prescription (sacrifice, taboo, ritual action).

A fully trained *babalawo* must memorize thousands of verses — training takes years, often decades.

### UNESCO recognition
Ifá was inscribed in 2005 on the UNESCO Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity — an unusual recognition of the system's intellectual and cultural significance.

### Ifá in the diaspora
In Cuba, *Lucumí Ifá* survives with extraordinary fidelity to Yoruba sources — scholars have demonstrated near-identical *odù* verses across the Atlantic. This is one of the most remarkable cases of oral tradition preservation under extreme duress.

## Initiation and priesthood

### Becoming an orisha priest/priestess
Initiation into the priesthood of an orisha (*kariosha* in Cuba, *candomblé initiation* in Brazil, *isese* initiation in Nigeria) involves:
- Determining one's *orí* (personal head/destiny) and the orisha who "owns" the person's head
- A period of seclusion, preparation, and instruction
- The placement of the orisha's *ashe* into the initiate's body
- The visible marking on the initiate (ritual dress, head shaving, specific colors)

After initiation, the priest/priestess carries concentrated *ashe* and can serve as a vehicle for their orisha's possession.

### The *orí* — personal destiny
Each person has an *orí* (personal head/inner head) — their personal destiny agreed upon before birth, the individual dimension of their soul. Honoring one's *orí* through ritual and ethical living is fundamental. One cannot succeed against an aligned *orí*; conversely, proper cultivation allows the *orí* to fulfill its potential.

## Egungun — ancestor masquerade
The *Egungun* are the masked embodiments of the ancestors — costumed dancers whose elaborate, all-covering costumes with long cloth trains literally embody the collective ancestral dead. When the Egungun appears and dances, the ancestors are present. The identity of the dancer is secret and irrelevant — the Egungun is the ancestor, not a representation.

Egungun festivals maintain the living-dead community relationship; they transmit blessings, resolve disputes, and warn against dangerous behavior.

## Yoruba religion today
- Active as a living tradition in Nigeria, Benin, and diaspora communities
- *Isese* (traditional Yoruba religion) is experiencing a significant revival in Nigeria, partly as a response to both Christian and Muslim dominance
- Scholars estimate 10–50 million active practitioners globally when diaspora forms are included
- The *Oodua* identity movement is partly religious — connecting Yoruba cultural nationalism with traditional religious practice

## Research notes
- The scholarly literature on Yoruba religion is extensive; Bolaji Idowu's *Olodumare: God in Yoruba Belief* (1962) established the academic framework
- The diaspora transmission of Yoruba religion to Cuba, Brazil, and Trinidad is one of the best-documented cases of religious transmission in history
- Gender in orisha religion: orisha have gendered identities but male priests can be possessed by female orisha and vice versa; gender transgression in initiation contexts is documented
- The relationship between Yoruba traditional religion, Islam (present in Yorubaland since c. 14th century), and Christianity (major since the 19th century) is complex and ongoing
