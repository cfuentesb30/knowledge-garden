# North American Indigenous Religions

## Purpose
Overview of Indigenous religious traditions of North America — their diversity, common structural features, the colonial disruption of those traditions, and analytical principles for studying them responsibly. This is a broad starting note for a vast and diverse field that warrants regional expansion. For the broader Americas context, see [[../Americas/Overview|Americas Overview]].

## The fundamental problem of generalization
North America north of Mexico contains hundreds of distinct Indigenous nations with distinct languages, cosmologies, ceremonial systems, and relationships to land. There is no "North American Indigenous religion" in the singular — there are Haudenosaunee religion, Lakota religion, Diné religion, Anishinaabe religion, Pueblo religion, Tlingit religion, and hundreds of others, each with its own logic, geography, and depth.

The single biggest analytical error is treating "Native American religion" as a uniform category and extracting decontextualized fragments (medicine wheels, vision quests, spirit animals) as if they were freely accessible generic spiritual content.

This note maps broad patterns and structural commonalities while insisting those patterns must be filled in with tradition-specific content.

## Why this shelf has been underdeveloped
Several reasons — all worth being clear-eyed about:
- Most academic religious studies scholarship centered European and Asian traditions for most of the 20th century
- Indigenous oral traditions were deliberately suppressed (residential school systems, missionary prohibitions, forced assimilation)
- Many sacred practices and knowledge systems are intentionally restricted — not all traditional knowledge is meant for public circulation
- Post-colonial academic ethics around who has the right to study, document, and publish Indigenous religious content is actively contested

Studying North American Indigenous religion responsibly requires different methodological commitments than studying ancient Greek religion from text-based archives.

## Common structural features

### Land-based cosmology
Perhaps the single most consistent feature: the land itself is sacred, not as metaphor but as the actual medium of religious life. Sacred geography is not backdrop to religion — it *is* religion. Specific mountains, rivers, rock formations, springs, and animal habitats are places of power, covenant, and ongoing communication between humans and other-than-human beings.

This has direct political implications: the forced removal of peoples from their land was also a religious dismemberment. This is part of why land return and rights are understood by many Indigenous communities as spiritual obligations, not merely political ones.

### Relational ontology — the "all my relations" frame
Most North American Indigenous traditions operate within what scholars now call a *relational ontology*: humans are not the pinnacle of creation or the only beings that matter. All beings — animals, plants, rocks, weather, celestial bodies — are persons in the relevant sense: they have agency, awareness, and standing within a web of obligations and relationships.

*Mitákuye Oyásʼiŋ* ("All my relations" — Lakota): a prayer and a worldview. The ceremony and the cosmos are populated with relatives, not objects.

This is structurally different from Western nature-as-resource frameworks and requires genuine reorientation to understand.

### Ceremony as cosmic maintenance
Ceremonies are not merely commemorative or expressive. They are performative in a cosmological sense: the ceremony *does* something necessary to maintain right relationship between humans and the rest of the cosmos. If the ceremony is not performed correctly, something goes wrong in the broader relational web.

Sun dances, potlatches, Green Corn ceremonies, kachina dances — these are not cultural performances or "rituals" in a dismissive sense. They are obligations.

### Oral tradition as primary scripture
Most Indigenous traditions preserved knowledge through oral transmission — songs, stories, ceremonial practice, specialized vocabularies. The person-to-person transmission is itself part of the sacred form; the content cannot be fully separated from the relationship in which it is passed down.

This means: written accounts of Indigenous traditions — including academic ethnographies — are always partial, always filtered through the relationship between recorder and community, and always structurally different from the living tradition.

### Specialist roles
Most traditions include specialist religious practitioners:
- **Healers/medicine people**: Working with plant knowledge, spiritual diagnosis, and healing
- **Shamanic practitioners** (various terminologies): Traveling to spirit realms, mediating between human and other-than-human worlds
- **Ceremonial specialists**: Knowing and leading specific ceremonies
- **Singers**: Carrying songs as sacred knowledge
- **Dream specialists**: Interpreting and working with dreams

The role is not separable from extensive apprenticeship, personal calling (often through illness or vision), and community recognition.

## Regional traditions — sketch map

### Eastern Woodlands
Haudenosaunee (Iroquois Confederacy), Anishinaabe (Ojibwe), Lenape, and others.
- Haudenosaunee: The *Longhouse* tradition; Midwinter ceremony; False Face Society (sacred masked healing society — the masks themselves are considered living beings; their commercialization is a major point of conflict); Sky Woman creation narrative
- Anishinaabe: *Midewiwin* (Grand Medicine Society) — graduated initiation system for healing and sacred knowledge; the Ojibwe birchbark scrolls encode cosmological knowledge in visual form
- Wampum belts as sacred treaty records — not decorative but mnemonic and ceremonial documents

### Plains
Lakota (Sioux), Cheyenne, Blackfoot, and others.
- Lakota: *Wakan Tanka* (Great Mystery/Spirit — often translated as God, which is too narrow); *Tunkashila* (Grandfather Spirit); *Inipi* (sweat lodge); *Hanblecheyapi* (vision quest); *Wiwanyag Wachipi* (Sun Dance — involves fasting and, in traditional form, piercing)
- The vision quest (*Hanblecheyapi*): Alone on a hill or in the wild, fasting, praying for a vision — a direct encounter with the spiritual world that orients a person's life and calling
- *Chanunpa* (sacred pipe): The pipe embodies and mediates the connection between the human world and the spirit world; pipe ceremonies are among the most sacred Lakota practices

### Southwest
Diné (Navajo), Pueblo peoples (Hopi, Zuñi, Acoma, etc.), Apache.
- Hopi: Kachinas (*Katsinas*) — spirit beings who visit the human world during certain seasons, embodied in elaborate masked dances; their dolls are sacred objects for children's religious education, not decorative art
- Diné: *Hózhó* (beauty, balance, harmony) — the central value and soteriological goal; ceremonial healing (Sings/Chants) restore balance when it is disrupted; the *Nightway* ceremony involves elaborate sand paintings constructed over days and then destroyed
- Pueblo architecture and kiva (ceremonial chamber) as a cosmological map

### Pacific Northwest Coast
Tlingit, Haida, Kwakwaka'wakw, and others.
- *Potlatch*: Ceremonial distribution of wealth — the inverse of accumulation; prestige comes from giving, not keeping; the Canadian government banned the potlatch 1885–1951 as part of cultural suppression; its revival is an act of both cultural and spiritual reclamation
- *Totem poles*: Not "idols" (a colonial misreading) but narrative-genealogical records encoding clan histories, relationships with other-than-human beings, and sacred encounters
- Formline art as sacred visual language encoding cosmological knowledge

### Arctic and Subarctic
Inuit, Yupik, and others.
- *Angakkuq* (angakok): Shaman — the specialist who travels to other realms to negotiate with spirits for the community's hunting success, healing, and weather
- Inuit religion has no separation between the spirit and material world — the same world, perceived at different registers
- *Inua*: The person/spirit inside — every animal, natural feature, and object has an *inua* (inner being) that must be respected

## The colonial disruption

### Residential schools
In the United States (Indian boarding schools, late 19th–20th century) and Canada (residential schools, 1870s–1996), Indigenous children were forcibly removed from their families and communities and prohibited from speaking their languages or practicing their religions. The explicit goal: "Kill the Indian, save the man." This was a systematic attempt at cultural and religious genocide.

The consequences for the transmission of religious knowledge — songs, languages, ceremonial practice, oral tradition — were severe and are still being reckoned with.

### The Indian Religious Crimes Code (US, 1883–1978)
US government policy actively prohibited Indigenous religious ceremonies (Sun Dance, potlatch, kachina dances, vision quests) until the *American Indian Religious Freedom Act* (1978) nominally protected them. Many ceremonies were practiced in secret during the prohibition period.

### Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA, 1990)
Required US museums and federal agencies to return human remains and sacred objects to Indigenous communities. Implementation has been slow and contested. The return of sacred objects (including False Face masks, wampum belts, and medicine bundles) is an ongoing religious — not merely cultural — priority.

## The appropriation problem
Certain Indigenous practices — vision quests, sweat lodges, "shamanism," dreamcatchers, "spirit animals" — have been extracted from their contexts and sold by New Age teachers, wellness retreats, and popular culture. This is:
- Often harmful to practitioners (fake sweat lodges have caused deaths)
- Disrespectful to communities who have been actively prevented from practicing these traditions
- Epistemologically distorting — the context is not separable from the practice

This is not an argument against studying these traditions. It is an argument for doing so with appropriate relationship, attribution, and deference to living Indigenous voices.

## Analytical principles for study
1. **Tradition-specific before comparative**: Learn Lakota religion as Lakota religion before comparing it to other traditions
2. **Living tradition, not just historical artifact**: Most of these traditions are alive and evolving
3. **Indigenous scholarship first**: Prioritize Indigenous scholars and community voices (Vine Deloria Jr., Tink Tinker, Kim TallBear, Shawn Wilson, Robin Wall Kimmerer)
4. **Know what is not for public circulation**: Some knowledge is restricted; that restriction is itself a form of religious practice
5. **Land cannot be separated out**: Understand the political-territorial context as inseparable from the religious content

## Recommended resources
- Vine Deloria Jr. — *God Is Red: A Native View of Religion* (foundational, written from inside)
- Robin Wall Kimmerer — *Braiding Sweetgrass* (Potawatomi botanical knowledge/worldview — not strictly religious studies but essential)
- Joseph Bruchac — various works on Abenaki tradition
- Black Elk — *Black Elk Speaks* (Lakota; with John Neihardt; note the editorial mediation)
- Raymond Bucko — *The Lakota Ritual Sweat Lodge* (careful ethnographic work)
- *American Indian Culture and Research Journal* — academic journal with significant Indigenous authorship
