# Rastafarianism

## Purpose
Map Rastafari as a living religion: its historical origins in 1930s Jamaica, the theology of Haile Selassie as the returned Messiah, the Babylon/Zion symbolic framework, ritual practice including ganja, the role of reggae in its global spread, and the major mansions.

---

## The core corrective

Rastafari is commonly reduced in popular culture to dreadlocks, reggae, and marijuana. That is the surface. The actual movement is a millenarian, anti-colonial, theologically sophisticated African-diasporic religion that emerged from Jamaican working-class engagement with biblical text, pan-Africanism, and a specific reading of Ethiopian history.

It is also not a unified church but a decentralized movement with multiple "mansions" (organized branches) and many independent practitioners. Rastafari resists institutional consolidation by design.

The proper term: **Rastafari** or **Rasta**. The form "Rastafarianism" is used by outsiders and resisted by many practitioners because the "-ism" suffix implies an ideology or "ism" the movement rejects — Rasta is understood as a way of life and a relationship with Jah, not a system.

## Historical origins

### Marcus Garvey and the prophecy

**Marcus Garvey** (1887–1940), the Jamaican pan-African leader, founder of the Universal Negro Improvement Association (UNIA), is often cited as having prophesied: "Look to Africa, where a Black king shall be crowned, for the day of deliverance is at hand."

Whether Garvey actually said this in those exact terms is contested. But Garveyite pan-Africanism — the call for Black self-rule, the dignity of African heritage, return to Africa — is the immediate intellectual context for Rastafari.

### The coronation of Haile Selassie I (1930)

On November 2, 1930, **Ras Tafari Makonnen** was crowned Emperor of Ethiopia, taking the throne name **Haile Selassie I** ("Power of the Trinity"). His full Ethiopian titles included "King of Kings, Lord of Lords, Conquering Lion of the Tribe of Judah, Elect of God."

For some Jamaicans reading the coronation in the context of Garvey's prophecy and the King James Bible (Revelation 5:5 — "the Lion of the tribe of Judah... hath prevailed"), this was the fulfillment of biblical prophecy. The Black king crowned in Africa with biblical titles was the returned Messiah.

### Early Rasta preachers

Several independent street preachers in Jamaica during the early 1930s began proclaiming Selassie as the returned Christ:
- **Leonard Howell** — often called the "first Rasta"; preached this gospel from 1932; established the Pinnacle commune; charged with sedition and persecuted by Jamaican authorities
- **Joseph Hibbert** — separately developed the same teaching from study with the Ancient Mystic Order of Ethiopia
- **Archibald Dunkley**
- **Robert Hinds**

These independent figures arrived at the same conclusion (Selassie = Christ returned) through similar biblical reading; this convergence is part of how Rastas understand the truth of the message — multiple witnesses to a revelation.

The movement spread rapidly among Jamaica's poor working class and rural population during the 1930s–50s under conditions of colonial oppression and economic marginalization.

## Theology

### Jah

**Jah** is the Rasta name for God — a shortening of **Jehovah** from the King James Bible. Jah is the one God of the Old Testament and the New Testament. Importantly, Jah is **inside** as well as outside — the relationship between Jah and the believer is one of indwelling (the "I" in Rasta speech, see below).

### Haile Selassie I as Jah's manifestation

Different Rasta traditions take different positions on exactly who Selassie was:
- **Most strictly**: Haile Selassie I is the literal incarnation of Jah, the returned Christ, the visible God on earth
- **Less strictly**: He is the manifestation of Jah's divine power for this age, but Jah is greater than any single person
- **Among some**: He is the Elect of God and a divine figure, but distinct from Jah

Selassie himself, a devout Ethiopian Orthodox Christian, never claimed to be God. When a 1966 visit to Jamaica brought him face to face with thousands of weeping Rastas at the airport, he was reportedly bewildered. The Ethiopian Orthodox Church's response to Rastafari is ambivalent — it welcomes Rasta interest in Ethiopian Christianity but rejects the claim of Selassie's divinity.

When Selassie was deposed by the Derg in 1974 and died (officially in 1975, but probably murdered), Rastas have responded in various ways: some hold that he is not literally dead, some that his physical death is irrelevant to his spiritual identity, some that his body's death simply marks a new phase. The crisis of Selassie's death was significant but did not destroy the movement.

### Babylon and Zion

Two foundational symbols structure Rasta worldview:

**Babylon** — the corrupt, oppressive, materialistic system of Western/colonial society. The British Empire, the United States, capitalism, the police, the church (when complicit), Western consumer culture — all manifestations of Babylon. The mental enslavement that continues after physical emancipation. Babylon is both an external political reality and an internal mental state to be overcome.

**Zion** — the true home; literally Ethiopia and Africa (the place); also the spiritual reality of communion with Jah; the redeemed condition of liberated Africans. "Zion train is coming" — the eventual repatriation, redemption, and overcoming of Babylon.

### Repatriation

Repatriation to Africa (specifically Ethiopia) is a core Rasta commitment. **Shashemene**, in Ethiopia, was granted by Haile Selassie as a settlement for Africans in the diaspora; a small Rasta community has lived there for decades.

For most Rastas, repatriation is partly literal (some return) and partly symbolic (a spiritual orientation that resists Babylon and affirms African identity). Few Jamaican Rastas have actually moved to Africa; many continue Africa-oriented spirituality from the diaspora.

### Anti-colonialism and Black liberation

Rastafari is inseparable from anti-colonial politics. The movement frames the African diasporic condition as exile imposed by Babylonian (European/colonial) violence. Liberation is spiritual, mental, and material. The dignity of Africa, of the Black body, of African religious heritage, is part of the theology.

## Practice

### Reasoning sessions

The central social-religious practice is the **reasoning** — a gathering of brethren to study scripture, discuss politics and theology, share food, smoke ganja sacramentally, and "reason out" spiritual and worldly issues. Reasoning is the closest Rastafari has to a congregational worship form.

There is no central hierarchy, no priesthood in the conventional sense, no fixed liturgy. The community gathers, talks, learns, and is together.

### Nyabinghi

**Nyabinghi** is the main ceremonial form: drumming, chanting, dancing, and praise that may continue for days. The Nyabinghi drum (a set of three drums — bass, fundeh, and akete/repeater) provides the rhythmic foundation. Nyabinghi celebrations mark significant dates: Selassie's coronation (Nov 2), his birthday (Jul 23), Ethiopian Christmas (Jan 7), Marcus Garvey's birthday (Aug 17), and others.

The term Nyabinghi originally referred to an East African anti-colonial spiritual/political movement; Rastafari adopted the name and the spiritual militancy it carries.

### Ganja as sacrament

**Cannabis (ganja)** is used sacramentally and reasoning-companionally. Scriptural justification includes Psalm 104:14 ("He causeth the grass to grow for the cattle, and herb for the service of man") and Genesis 1:29 ("I have given you every herb bearing seed").

Ganja is held to:
- Facilitate reasoning and spiritual insight
- Connect the user with Jah's natural creation
- Open the mind to divine perception

It is **not** treated as a recreational drug; respectful sacramental use is the ideal. Practical reality varies among practitioners.

The persecution of Rastas under Jamaican (and later many Western) anti-marijuana laws has been a major source of conflict with Babylon. Legal cannabis reform in recent decades has changed this landscape, but Rasta sacramental use was a key driver of the religious liberty arguments.

### Ital food

**Ital** (from "vital") is the Rasta dietary ethic:
- Vegetarian or vegan in many traditions
- No pork (consistent with biblical Leviticus)
- No shellfish (biblical)
- No processed foods, additives, salt in some traditions
- Foods cooked in clay pots, eaten from natural vessels
- The goal: living food that supports vitality and connection with Jah's creation

Strict ital varies among mansions and individuals. The principle: eat what supports life and is closest to Jah's natural creation.

### Dreadlocks

The dreadlocks (locs) are a Nazarite vow practice based on Numbers 6:5 — "no razor shall come upon his head." More than fashion: a religious commitment, a public marker of identification with Jah, and a refusal of Babylon's grooming norms. The locs also symbolize the lion's mane — connecting to the Lion of Judah.

Not all Rastas wear dreadlocks, and not all who wear dreadlocks are Rastas. The reduction of Rasta identity to locs is part of the cultural appropriation problem.

### Iyaric / I-and-I speech

Rastas developed a distinctive English usage called **Iyaric** or **Dread Talk**:
- **I-and-I** (or "Iyahna I") instead of "we" — emphasizes the unity of the speaker with Jah and with other Rastas; the divine "I" present in each person
- **Words rebuilt for positive sound/meaning**: "overstand" instead of "understand" (you stand over the truth, not under it); "downpression" instead of "oppression"; "livity" instead of "life"
- **Removal of objectifying or hierarchical words**: "Babylon system" is named and rejected
- The reformed language is itself a spiritual practice — words have power, and reshaping speech reshapes consciousness

## The mansions

Rastafari is decentralized but several recognizable "mansions" (organized branches) exist:

**Nyahbinghi (House of Nyahbinghi)** — the oldest mansion; emphasizes Selassie's divinity; theocratic Africa orientation; nyabinghi ceremonies as central

**Bobo Shanti (Ethiopia Africa Black International Congress)** — founded by Prince Emmanuel Charles Edwards in the 1950s; wear turbans; live in disciplined communities (Bobo Hill in St. Thomas, Jamaica); priestly orientation; strict ital; Emmanuel held as a divine figure in addition to Selassie

**Twelve Tribes of Israel** — founded by Vernon Carrington (Prophet Gad) in 1968; organized around the twelve biblical tribes; each member belongs to a tribe based on birth month; more middle-class and less ascetic in some respects; Bob Marley was a member of this mansion

**School of Vision** — smaller mansion with mystical/visionary orientation

Many Rastas don't belong to any formal mansion and practice independently or in small reasoning groups.

## Reggae and global spread

The global spread of Rastafari is inseparable from the rise of reggae music in the 1960s–70s. Reggae was the cultural vehicle through which Rasta theology, Babylon/Zion vocabulary, and Africa-centered identity reached worldwide audiences.

**Bob Marley** (1945–1981) — the most influential single figure. A Twelve Tribes Rasta whose music ("Redemption Song," "Exodus," "War," "Get Up Stand Up," "One Love") carried Rasta themes globally. His funeral in 1981 in Jamaica was a state event. Marley's commercial success spread Rasta visibility but also created the commodification problem.

**Other key musicians**: Peter Tosh, Bunny Wailer, Burning Spear, Dennis Brown, Black Uhuru, Steel Pulse — reggae musicians who explicitly carried Rasta theology in their music.

The downside: international audiences often consumed reggae aesthetics — locs, ganja, "irie" — without engaging the political-theological substance. The Bob Marley brand became a tourist commodity in Jamaica; Rastafari was simultaneously globalized and superficialized.

## Current status

Rastafari is recognized as a religion in multiple jurisdictions. There are:
- An estimated 700,000 to over a million Rastas globally
- Significant communities in Jamaica, the Caribbean broadly, the UK, US, continental Africa (especially Ethiopia and South Africa), Brazil, and elsewhere
- Continuing legal struggles around religious cannabis use, hair (locs in workplace and school contexts), and other expressions of practice
- Diverse internal debates about gender (the Rasta tradition is historically patriarchal — Empress vs. Kingman dynamics, menstrual taboos in some mansions — and is being reshaped by women's voices), LGBTQ+ inclusion, and Selassie's status
