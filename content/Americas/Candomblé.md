# Candomblé

## Purpose
Map the Brazilian Afro-diasporic religion of Candomblé: its African sources, the terreiro as institutional center, the orixá system, initiation, and how it differs from Cuban Santería despite shared Yoruba roots.

---

## What Candomblé is

Candomblé is an Afro-Brazilian religion formed primarily from Yoruba, Fon/Ewe, and Bantu traditions transported to Brazil during the Atlantic slave trade, concentrated in Bahia (Salvador) and spreading throughout Brazil. Like Santería, it is a living tradition with millions of practitioners.

The name likely derives from a Bantu word; the tradition itself is more commonly called simply "the religion" (a religião) among practitioners. Communities are organized around **terreiros** — the ritual house and community space.

---

## African source nations: the "nações"

Unlike Cuba, which developed a relatively unified Lucumí tradition, Brazilian Candomblé retained clearer distinctions between African source traditions, organized as "nations" (nações):

**Candomblé Ketu** (or Nagô) — the most prestigious and studied; Yoruba (Ketu was a Yoruba kingdom) origins; uses Yoruba language in ritual; the Bahian terreiros (especially Ilê Axé Opô Afonjá and Casa Branca/Ilê Iyá Nassô) are the central institutions. When scholars talk about "Candomblé" without qualification, they usually mean Ketu.

**Candomblé Jeje** — Fon/Ewe origins from Dahomey (present-day Benin); similar to Vodou source tradition; divine beings called Voduns rather than orixás; Sakpata (= Babalú-Ayé, smallpox god) particularly important.

**Candomblé Angola** — Bantu origins (Kongo/Angola region); divine beings called Nkisi (same term as Kongo power objects); more syncretic, incorporates indigenous Brazilian and Catholic elements more freely; less prestige in the hierarchy of purity claims.

**Candomblé de Caboclo** — incorporates indigenous Brazilian spirits (caboclos, spirits of the land and the indigenous dead) alongside African orixás; considered less "pure" by Ketu orthodoxy.

**The purity hierarchy**: Ketu terreiros historically claimed superior African authenticity and looked down on more syncretic houses. This is itself a complex politics of legitimacy — Ruth Landes, Roger Bastide, and others have analyzed how the "purity" claim functioned as social capital.

## Theology: Orixás and Axé

**Olodumare / Olorun** — supreme deity, remote and not directly worshipped; the same theological structure as Yoruba religion and Santería.

**Orixás** — the divine beings who actually interact with humans. In Candomblé Ketu, the core pantheon is largely the same as Santería/Lucumí, though names, domains, and protocols differ somewhat:

| Orixá (Brazil) | Orisha (Cuba) | Domain |
|---|---|---|
| Exu | Elegba/Eshu | Crossroads, communication, trickster |
| Ogum | Ogún | Iron, war, roads |
| Oxóssi | Ochosi | Hunt, forest, abundance |
| Ossain | Ossain | Plants, herbal medicine |
| Xangô | Shangó | Thunder, justice, fire |
| Iemanjá | Yemayá | Ocean, motherhood |
| Oxum | Oshún | Rivers, love, gold |
| Iansã/Oyá | Oyá | Wind, storms, death |
| Obatalá/Oxalá | Obatalá | White purity, creation, peace |
| Omolu/Obaluaê | Babalú-Ayé | Disease, healing |
| Oxumarê | — | Rainbow, serpent, cyclical time |
| Nanã | — | Primordial mud, ancient motherhood, death |
| Ibeji | Ibeji | Twins, childhood, luck |

Some orixás are more developed in Brazil than Cuba (Oxumarê, Nanã) and vice versa.

**Exu vs. Elegba**: Exu in Brazil is more consistently associated with a dark/tricky energy and is more often confused with the devil by outsiders and some Catholics. In Cuban practice, Elegba tends to be more clearly separated from "dark" associations. Neither is the devil — but the misidentification is particularly strong in Brazil.

**Axé** — the divine force/power that animates everything; the key concept in Candomblé. The terreiro accumulates and circulates axé. Sacred objects, certain trees (especially the iroko/ficus), the ritual blood of sacrifices, and initiated bodies all carry and transmit axé. "Giving axé" is the fundamental religious act.

## The terreiro

The terreiro is much more than a temple. It is:
- a religious community (a familia-de-santo, "family of the saint")
- the residence of the orixás, housed in their individual shrines (peji/quartos de santo)
- the performative space for ceremonies, drumming, and possession
- a social welfare institution in many poor communities — providing food, shelter, mutual aid
- a keeper of African cultural and linguistic knowledge

**Leadership:**
- **Ialorixá** (Mãe-de-santo, "Mother of the saint") — female ritual head; the Bahian terreiros historically had female leadership as a norm, which became a central claim in academic literature (though its interpretation is contested)
- **Babalorixá** (Pai-de-santo, "Father of the saint") — male ritual head
- **Babalawo** — Ifá divination specialist; separate role, less central in Brazil than in Cuba (Candomblé uses the merindilogun/cowrie shell system more than the opele chain in everyday practice)
- **Ekedi** — female assistant who assists during possession ceremonies; does not get possessed herself; supports the body of the possessed person
- **Ogã** — male dignitary; non-initiated but acknowledged supporter; often community leaders, intellectuals, artists who patronize the terreiro

The famous early 20th-century terreiros of Salvador are the symbolic centers: **Casa Branca do Engenho Velho** (Ilê Iyá Nassô), **Ilê Axé Opô Afonjá** (associated with the Mãe Senhora). These were run by freed Yoruba women of remarkable organizational capacity.

## Initiation

Initiation (feitura, "making") is the central Candomblé ceremony, paralleling Cuban kariocha:

- The candidate's head orisha is identified through divination
- The initiation involves: ritual head shaving, spiritual baths, seclusion (reclusão) for a period (traditionally 17 days or more; now often abbreviated), marking of the body, installation of the orixá's sacred stones and objects
- The novice emerges as an **Iyawô** (same Yoruba word as Cuba) for approximately one year, wearing white and observing restrictions
- After seven years, the initiate may take higher-degree initiations and eventually become a fully authorized ritual leader

The initiation is understood as a spiritual birth — the person's spiritual identity is established and the orixá takes up permanent residence in their head (ori).

---

## Ceremony: drumming, trance, possession

Candomblé ceremonies are called **festas** (feasts/parties) — public celebrations for the orixás:
- **Drums** (atabaques): three sacred drums (rum, rumpi, lê) of Yoruba/Fon derivation; their rhythms (toques) are specific to each orixá
- **Songs**: call-and-response songs in Yoruba (Ketu), Fon (Jeje), or Bantu languages (Angola); the song calls the orixá
- **Sequence**: initiates circle-dance to the rhythms; eventually one or more will enter trance/possession; the orixá "descends" into (monta, "mounts") their body
- **The mounted person** changes behavior, dress, and expression to embody the orixá's personality
- **Helpers** (ekedi) assist, dress the person in the orixá's ceremonial clothes, and watch over the physical body

Possession is a mark of spiritual development and divine favor. Being "ridden" by your orixá is not frightening or shameful — it is service to the community.

**Giras**: In Umbanda (a related but distinct Brazilian syncretic tradition), the term refers to similar circles of spirit work — Umbanda is more openly syncretic and should not be confused with Candomblé.

## Catholic syncretism and identity politics

Like Santería, Candomblé historically mapped orixás to Catholic saints. But in the 20th century, many Candomblé terreiros — especially Ketu houses — underwent a process of **de-syncretism**, deliberately removing Catholic imagery from their terreiros and emphasizing African origins.

This was partly led by the 1980s "Black consciousness" movement in Brazil. The argument: Catholic syncretism was a survival strategy under slavery; it is no longer needed and it obscures African religious identity.

Not all practitioners agreed. Many terreiros, especially in more popular and less elite contexts, maintain Catholic imagery as genuinely meaningful. The de-syncretism debate is ongoing.

---

## Social and political dimensions

Candomblé has been central to African Brazilian identity, resistance, and survival:
- Throughout Brazilian history, Candomblé was persecuted — terreiros raided, drums confiscated, practitioners imprisoned (as late as the Estado Novo period in the 1930s–40s, police required terreiros to register and get permits)
- Terreiros preserved Yoruba, Fon, and Bantu language, music, food, medicine, and social structures
- The **Bahia Tourism industry** from the 1950s onward commodified Candomblé aesthetics while the religion itself continued to face social stigma
- Evangelical Christian expansion in Brazil (particularly Neo-Pentecostalism from the 1980s onward) has been aggressively hostile to Afro-Brazilian religions, including physical attacks on terreiros and practitioners

Today, Candomblé is recognized by the Brazilian state as a religion with legal protections, and several terreiros in Bahia are listed as cultural heritage sites.

## Umbanda: the comparison

**Umbanda** is a separate Brazilian syncretic religion that emerged in the early 20th century, explicitly mixing:
- African orixás and spirits
- Brazilian indigenous spirits (caboclos)
- Kardecist spiritism (the French spiritualist tradition of Allan Kardec, adapted to Brazil)
- Catholic saints

Umbanda is more egalitarian in structure, cheaper to participate in, and far more widespread numerically in Brazil than Candomblé. It explicitly aims at a "Brazilian" religious synthesis. Candomblé purists consider it dangerously syncretic; Umbanda practitioners find it more accessible. They are related but distinct traditions.
