# Santería / Lucumí (Regla de Ocha-Ifá)

## Purpose
Map the Cuban Yoruba diaspora religion: its African roots, the mechanics of transformation under slavery and Catholicism, the orisha system, initiation, Ifá divination, and how it relates to other Afro-diasporic traditions.

---

## Names and identity

The tradition has multiple names, each carrying a political and theological claim:
- **Santería** — most common in popular usage; pejorative origin (meaning "the saint business," implying fake Catholic piety over real African religion); practitioners often reject the term
- **Lucumí** — the name Cuban-born Yoruba people used for themselves and their language; widely used by practitioners
- **Regla de Ocha** — "Rule of Ocha/Orisha"; the orisha-focused practice
- **Regla de Ifá** — the Ifá divination system, practiced by the Babalawo lineage; technically a parallel but related institution
- **La Religión** — simply "the Religion" among practitioners

The term "Lucumí" reflects the Cuban transformation of a Yoruba identity. "Yoruba" as an ethnic category was itself partly a colonial and missionary construction in the 19th century — the people transported to Cuba came from multiple Yoruba-speaking polities (Oyo, Egba, Ijebu, etc.) but were consolidated under "Lucumí" in Cuba.

---

## Historical formation

**Source tradition**: Yoruba religion from southwestern Nigeria and Benin — see the Yoruba Religion note for the African foundation.

**The Cuban context**: The largest single African ethnic group brought to Cuba during the Atlantic slave trade (especially 18th–19th century) was Yoruba-speaking. Unlike in some other New World contexts where African religious knowledge was violently suppressed from the start, several factors allowed significant transmission:
- Urban slavery in Havana gave some enslaved people more freedom of movement and association
- The **cabildos de nación** — mutual aid societies organized by African ethnic groups, tolerated by Spanish colonial authorities — became the institutional containers for preserving religious knowledge, ritual, and community

**The Catholic overlay**: Orishas were identified with Catholic saints to provide external cover and because both systems were understood to deal with divine intermediary power. This is not deception only — there is genuine syncretism in which the saints and orishas partially merged in practitioners' minds.

The systematic orisha-saint correspondences:
| Orisha | Catholic saint | Domain |
|---|---|---|
| Elegba/Eshu | St. Anthony of Padua / Holy Child of Atocha | Crossroads, communication, fate |
| Ogún | St. Peter | Iron, war, labor |
| Obatalá | Our Lady of Mercy / St. Joseph | White purity, creation, wisdom |
| Yemayá | Our Lady of Regla | Ocean, motherhood |
| Oshún | Our Lady of Charity (Cuba's patron) | Rivers, love, gold |

## The orisha system in Cuban Lucumí

The orishas (also spelled orichas in Cuban practice) are divine forces/personalities who govern different domains of nature and human experience. They have distinct personalities, tastes, colors, numbers, and ritual protocols.

**Key orishas in Lucumí:**

**Elegba / Eshu** — the most important. First in all ceremonies. Governs roads, crossroads, beginnings, and communication between humans and the divine. Nothing begins without his permission. Trickster and teacher. His colors: red and black. His number: 3 (or 21). Received as the first orisha by any initiate.

**Ogún** — iron, labor, the forest, warfare, surgery. All who work with metal (drivers, surgeons, mechanics) fall under his domain. He opens roads that Elegba guards. Colors: green and black.

**Obatalá** — the eldest orisha, associated with creation (Olodumare gave him the task of forming humans from clay), white purity, wisdom, peace. Patron of those with mental illness, disabilities, albinos. His priest is called an Olorisha or Obatalá's child. Color: white only.

**Yemayá** — orisha of the ocean and saltwater. Mother of many orishas. Associated with maternity, deep water, mystery. Color: blue. Her children tend to be emotionally deep and private.

**Oshún** — orisha of rivers, freshwater, love, beauty, gold, sensuality, fertility. Second in importance after Elegba for many purposes. Cuba's patron Our Lady of Charity is identified with her. Colors: yellow, gold. The honey she loves is used in rituals — but you taste it before offering it to prove it hasn't been poisoned.

**Shangó** — thunder and lightning, justice, passion, fire, dance, drumming. Historically the fourth Alafin (king) of Oyo who became deified. Colors: red and white. Belongs to both warriors and kings. Associated with St. Barbara (whose feast day imagery involves lightning).

**Oyá** — wind, storms, transformation, the marketplace, the gate of the dead. She and Shangó are closely linked. She governs transitions — including the transition of death. Colors: maroon/purple/multicolor.

**Babalú-Ayé** — orisha of disease, specifically skin diseases, epidemics; also healing. Extremely popular, especially among the poor and suffering. Known in Brazil as Omolu/Obaluaê. The "St. Lazarus" of Cuba is a major cult figure at the El Rincón sanctuary.

**Orula/Orunmila** — orisha of wisdom and divination, the witness of human destiny, owner of the Ifá oracle. He is not "mounted" (possessed) — he communicates through divination only. His priests are the Babalawos. Colors: green and yellow.

## The ritual system

**Initiation levels:**

1. **Receiving the Warriors** (Elegba, Ogún, Ochosi, Osun) — a preliminary initiation; receiving these four orishas as protective presences
2. **Kariocha / Making Ocha** — the main initiation; "crowning" a head orisha (one's tutelary orisha, determined by divination); a week-long ceremony involving head-shaving, ritual baths, spiritual "birth," and the installation of the orisha's sacred stones (otanes) in the practitioner's home; the initiate becomes a **Iyawó** (bride/novice) for one year, observing many restrictions
3. **Babalawo initiation** — separate, male-only in most lineages, governed by Ifá tradition; the Babalawo is the specialist in Ifá divination

**The Orisha "crown"**: through divination it is determined which orisha "owns" the practitioner's head (ori). This becomes your primary tutelary orisha, whose shrine you maintain and who you are "a child of."

**Ritual objects**: Each orisha has sacred vessels (soperas, clay pots, or other containers) containing consecrated stones (otanes) and other materials. The stones are the physical seat of the orisha's presence. They are bathed, fed, and maintained by the practitioner.

**Divination**: Multiple systems:
- **Dilogún** — 16 cowrie shells thrown by Olorisha priests; 256 possible patterns (odu), each associated with stories (patakí), proverbs, and guidance
- **Ifá** — 256 odu system using either the opele (divining chain) or ikin (palm nuts); practiced only by Babalawos; much larger textual corpus; considered the highest level of divination

**Ebó** (offerings/sacrifice): The system runs on reciprocal relationship. Orishas receive offerings — food, drink, candles, animals (in the case of blood sacrifice, which is central to initiation and major ceremonies). This is not cruelty but covenant — the orisha protects and guides; the practitioner feeds and honors. The Supreme Court's 1993 case *Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye v. City of Hialeah* protected Santería's animal sacrifice as constitutionally protected religious practice.

**Possession**: During drumming ceremonies (bembe, tambor), orishas may descend into ("mount") a practitioner's body and interact directly with the community — giving advice, healing, dancing. Orisha possession is understood as a gift and a service, not a frightening event (from an insider perspective).

## Diaspora spread

Santería/Lucumí spread from Cuba to:
- Puerto Rico (where it absorbed local variations)
- Venezuela, Colombia, Mexico
- United States — primarily with Cuban diaspora post-1959 revolution, centered in Miami and New York
- Globally — initiated practitioners have spread lineages to Europe, Latin America, and beyond

The Cuban revolution had a complex relationship with Santería — officially atheist Marxism suppressed public practice for decades, but Santería persisted underground and has experienced significant revival since the 1990s.

## Relations to other traditions

**Candomblé** (Brazil) — parallel Yoruba diaspora tradition; different colonial context (Portuguese/Brazilian), different saints used, some different orisha names. More conservative about maintaining African forms in some schools (especially the Candomblé Ketu of Bahia). The Candomblé note covers this in depth.

**Trinidad Orisha** / **Shango Baptist** — Yoruba tradition in Trinidad, blended with Baptist Christianity more strongly.

**Vodou** — different source tradition (primarily Fon/Ewe from Dahomey), different pantheon (lwa), different initiation structure; but parallel Atlantic diaspora structure and some overlapping influences.

**21 Divisiones** (Dominican Republic) — syncretic tradition with Yoruba, Taino, and Catholic elements; related but distinct.

## Common misrepresentations
- "Santería is Voodoo" — no; different African source traditions, different pantheons, different structures; both are Afro-diasporic religions but are not the same tradition
- "Santería involves curses and dark magic" — like any tradition, there are practitioners who offer harmful work; the tradition as a whole is concerned with health, protection, love, prosperity, justice, and spiritual development
- Sensational media portrayals consistently focus on animal sacrifice and "dark" aesthetics while ignoring the community care, healing, and ethical framework
