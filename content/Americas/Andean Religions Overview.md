# Andean Religions Overview

## Scope
This note surveys major religious patterns of the Andean world, especially in relation to pre-Columbian civilizations, Inca imperial religion, local sacred geography, agricultural reciprocity, and later continuities under colonial Christianity.

## Why it matters
Andean religion matters because it is one of the clearest cases in world religion of:
- sacred geography as active reality
- mountain and landscape power
- reciprocity between humans, land, ancestors, and sacred beings
- state ritual layered over local cults
- colonial Christianity interacting with, suppressing, and blending with older systems

## Important caution
"Andean religion" is not one single unified creed.
The Andes contain many cultures before, during, and beyond the Inca.
The Inca become politically dominant, but not metaphysically exhaustive.
Local religious life remains crucial.

## Core features
- sacred geography is central
- mountains, earth forces, celestial cycles, and local ritual ecology matter deeply
- religion is closely tied to agriculture, reciprocity, ancestry, and political authority
- local sacred sites and landscape beings can matter more than centralized doctrine

## Important divine beings and sacred powers
- **Inti** — sun deity of major importance in Inca state religion
- **Viracocha** — creator-associated figure in some traditions
- **Pachamama** — earth/mother/land fertility power in later and continuing Andean religious language
- **apus** — mountain spirits or powers in many Andean frameworks

## Ritual life
- offerings to land and sacred places
- calendrical and agricultural rites
- ancestor-linked practices
- state ritual in imperial contexts
- sacred landscape pilgrimage and localized devotion
- reciprocal exchange with land and sacred powers rather than one-way worship alone

## State and local religion
This distinction matters.
Inca imperial religion created broader political-sacred organization, but local sacred life remained strong.
The relation between empire and local cult was one of layering and incorporation, not total replacement.

## Historical patterns
### Pre-Inca and Inca layers
The Andes contain many cultures before and beyond the Inca.
The Inca system becomes politically dominant but does not erase all local variation.

### Colonial transformation
Catholicism overlays, suppresses, and blends with Andean practice in many regions.
That produces strong cases of syncretism and continuity under new symbols, feasts, saints, and shrine structures.

## Why it matters comparatively
Andean religion is especially useful for studying:
- sacred landscape
- reciprocity as religious logic
- mountain power and local shrines
- colonial layering rather than total replacement

## Recommended resources
- Sabine MacCormack
- Gary Urton
- Catherine Allen
- Frank Salomon
- regional Andean and colonial religion scholarship

## Good working summary
Andean religions are landscape-centered, reciprocal, agricultural, ancestral, and highly resilient under colonial pressure. To understand them, you have to read mountains, offerings, empire, and local continuity together.
