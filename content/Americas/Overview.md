# Americas Overview

## Scope
This section covers Indigenous religious worlds of the Americas, Mesoamerican and Andean traditions, colonial transformation, Catholic layering, African diasporic formation, and the continuing reshaping of sacred life across the hemisphere.

## Why the Americas matter religiously
The Americas are not only a site of conquest and replacement.
They are one of the strongest laboratories of religious collision, survival, and recombination.
This shelf matters for understanding:
- Indigenous cosmology and sacred geography
- state religion in Mesoamerican and Andean systems
- colonial Christianity as both imposition and adaptation
- Afro-diasporic religion as transformed continuity
- syncretism under violence
- the survival of land-, ancestor-, and ritual-based systems inside hostile orders

## Major regional clusters
### Mesoamerica
Highly structured calendrical, cosmological, temple-centered, and politically integrated religions among Maya, Mexica/Aztec, and related traditions.

### Andes
Sacred geography, mountain power, reciprocity, agricultural ritual, Inca state religion, and strong continuity under colonial layering.

### Afro-diasporic Americas
Religious systems formed under slavery, displacement, and colonial domination through transformed continuity with African source traditions.

### Colonial and postcolonial Christian interaction
Catholicism becomes historically central, but never simply replaces earlier systems cleanly. Layering, disguise, adaptation, and conflict matter.

### North American Indigenous traditions
Not yet deeply built in this shelf, but they remain a major future gap and should not be forgotten.

## Core themes worth tracking
- sacred geography and land relation
- calendrical ritual and cosmic maintenance
- sacrifice, offering, and reciprocal obligation
- colonial suppression and religious masking
- syncretism under power asymmetry
- ritual continuity through diaspora and reinvention

## Research lenses that help here
- do not reduce Indigenous religion to sacrifice or folklore
- take sacred landscape seriously
- treat colonial Christianity as historically powerful but never total
- distinguish continuity, adaptation, disguise, and invention
- keep Atlantic slavery and African transmission in view when reading the Americas shelf

## Recommended resources
- Davíd Carrasco — *Religions of Mesoamerica*
- Alfredo López Austin — Mesoamerican religion and thought
- Sabine MacCormack — Andean religion and colonial transformation
- Gary Urton / Catherine Allen for Andean worlds
- J. Lorand Matory / Stephan Palmié for Afro-Atlantic religion
- serious regional historical scholarship over flattening survey language

## Good working summary
The Americas are a religious zone of collision, continuity, and transformation. Indigenous sacred worlds, colonial Christianity, and African diasporic traditions meet here under unequal power, producing some of the most complex religious layering in the world.
