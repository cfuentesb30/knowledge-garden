# Afro-Diasporic Religions Overview

## Scope
This note surveys religious traditions in the Americas shaped by African continuities, slavery, colonialism, Catholic overlay, spirit mediation, ritual performance, healing, sacred music, and survival through transformation.

## Why it matters
These traditions are some of the clearest cases of religious continuity under violent rupture.
They matter for understanding:
- syncretism under coercion
- ritual resilience
- spirit possession and mediation
- diaspora transformation without total loss of source tradition
- how religious systems survive by changing form while preserving deep structure

## Important caution
"Afro-diasporic religion" is a library category, not one single religion.
Lucumí, Vodou, Candomblé, and related traditions have overlap, but they are not interchangeable.
A serious library must avoid flattening them into one generic Atlantic mysticism.

## Historical conditions
- Atlantic slave trade and forced displacement
- colonial suppression and criminalization
- Catholic domination and pressure to mask, reinterpret, or layer practices
- survival through coded continuity, adaptation, memory, and ritual reconstruction

These traditions are not leftovers. They are surviving systems under pressure.

## Major examples
### Yoruba-derived traditions
- **Santería / Lucumí** and related formations
- strong orisha systems
- divination, possession, offering, initiation, and sacred music structures

### Vodou
- Haitian and related settings
- **lwa**, ritual service, possession, altar practice, drumming, and strong historical entanglement with revolution, memory, and colonial violence

### Candomblé
- especially in Brazil
- rich orisha-centered ritual life, possession, music, priesthood, and house/temple organization

### Other Afro-Atlantic formations
Other traditions across the Caribbean and the Americas also preserve spirit, ancestor, divinatory, and ritual continuities with distinct local development.

## Important divine/spiritual patterns
Depending on the tradition, one may encounter:
- **orishas**
- **lwa**
- ancestors
- saints mapped onto African divine figures in Catholic settings
- powerful ritual specialists and house-based leadership

## Ritual life
- possession or spirit mediation in some traditions
- drumming and dance
- offerings and sacrifice
- divination
- healing work
- initiation systems
- altar and shrine practice
- sacred language, songs, and embodied transmission

## Why music and embodiment matter
These traditions are not primarily abstract creeds.
They are embodied, rhythmic, social, and ritual.
Music, movement, initiation, and material offerings are part of how sacred power becomes present.

## Why they matter comparatively
Afro-diasporic religions are essential for understanding:
- syncretism
- continuity under suppression
- ritual knowledge outside scriptural dominance
- sacred power mediated through body, sound, possession, and house lineage

## Recommended resources
- J. Lorand Matory
- Stephan Palmié
- Karen McCarthy Brown for Vodou-related study
- Miguel Barnet and regional studies where appropriate
- serious Afro-Atlantic historical and anthropological scholarship

## Good working summary
Afro-diasporic religions are living, adaptive, deeply structured systems forged under extreme historical pressure. They preserve African religious logic through transformation, ritual embodiment, and powerful continuity beneath colonial rupture.
