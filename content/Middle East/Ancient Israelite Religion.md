# Ancient Israelite Religion

## Purpose
Trace Israelite religion from its Canaanite polytheistic roots through the emergence of monotheism. The Hebrew Bible presents a retrospective theological narrative — this note recovers the actual historical development, including what the biblical authors themselves were arguing against.

---

## The core corrective
The Bible is not a description of what ancient Israelites actually believed and practiced. It is a normative theological document written largely by elites who were trying to enforce a particular vision of Yahweh-only worship against persistent popular alternatives. The religious landscape it argues against is itself part of the historical record.

Pre-exile Israelite religion (before 587 BCE) was substantially polytheistic, regionally diverse, and embedded in Canaanite religious culture. Strict monotheism was a late and contested development, not the original baseline.

---

## Canaanite roots

Israel emerged from the Canaanite cultural zone of the Levant. The religious world it inhabited was shaped by:

- **El** — high god of the Canaanite pantheon. Head of the divine council. The name "Israel" means "El rules" or "El strives." El in early texts is identified with Yahweh, or Yahweh is portrayed as one of El's sons.
- **Baal** — storm god, lord of rain and fertility. Major object of prophetic polemic. Baal-worship was a real, popular alternative, not a strawman. The contest between Yahweh and Baal in 1 Kings 18 reflects genuine religious competition.
- **Asherah** — consort of El; also associated with Yahweh. Archaeological evidence (Kuntillet Ajrud and Khirbet el-Qom inscriptions, 9th–8th century BCE) refers explicitly to "Yahweh and his Asherah." She appears in the Bible as a cult object to be destroyed — which means she was actually venerated, not merely hypothetically condemned.
- **Astarte/Ashtoreth** — goddess associated with love, war, and the evening star. Condemned in biblical texts; clearly practiced widely.
- **Molech/Moloch** — associated with child sacrifice, condemned repeatedly; whether actual child sacrifice was widespread or the accusation was polemical is debated, but some sacrificial practice at the Tophet in the Hinnom Valley (Gehenna) is archaeologically attested.

The divine council — a hierarchy of divine beings under El/Yahweh — appears intact in early Hebrew texts. Psalm 82 depicts Yahweh judging the other gods. Deuteronomy 32:8–9 (DSS version) says the nations were divided among the sons of El; Yahweh received Israel as his portion. This is henotheism (one supreme god for a people), not monotheism.

---

## El and Yahweh: two deities becoming one

The scholarly consensus (Mark S. Smith, Frank Moore Cross) is that Yahweh and El were originally distinct deities that merged at some point in Israelite religious history.

Evidence:
- El epithets (El Shaddai, El Elyon, El Olam) are used in Genesis for the God of the patriarchs before Yahweh is introduced by name (Exodus 6:3 explicitly states the patriarchs didn't know the name Yahweh)
- Yahweh has distinct traits: warrior god, possibly associated with the southern wilderness (Seir, Sinai, Edom), not originally Canaanite but absorbed into the Canaanite world
- The Exodus tradition and the patriarchal tradition may have come from different ethnic/tribal groups who identified their gods with each other as they merged into "Israel"

The result is a complex divine figure carrying both El and Yahweh traits. The process of merging probably happened across the early monarchy period.

---

## Popular religion vs. official religion

A critical distinction: the religion enforced by Jerusalem temple elites and royal reformers was not what most Israelites practiced.

**Popular practice included:**
- Household shrines and cult figurines — female pillar figurines are found in enormous numbers in archaeological contexts and almost certainly represent goddess veneration, possibly Asherah
- Local high places (bamot) — outdoor sanctuaries on hilltops; condemned repeatedly by reforming kings (Hezekiah, Josiah) but re-established repeatedly between reforms
- Ancestor veneration — teraphim (household ancestor statues), food offerings to the dead, mourning rites condemned in legal codes
- Divination — condemned but clearly practiced; Saul consults the Witch of Endor; Urim and Thummim are a state-sanctioned divination tool within the temple cult
- Multiple temples — the Jerusalem temple did not have exclusive authority in practice; the rival northern sanctuary at Bethel had its own priesthood and its own deity representation (the golden calf)

**Official reform trajectories:**
- Hezekiah (c. 715–687 BCE) — first major centralization push; destroyed bronze serpent Nehushtan (itself a sacred object in the Jerusalem temple)
- Josiah (c. 640–609 BCE) — the Deuteronomic reform; "discovery" of a law scroll in the temple (probably Deuteronomy or a proto-Deuteronomy) triggers systematic destruction of high places and centralization of all legitimate worship at Jerusalem
- Post-exilic returnees — the group who returned from Babylon brought a much more theologically developed, exclusive Yahwism, which became normative Judaism

## The emergence of monotheism

**Stages (oversimplified but useful):**
1. **Polytheism** — multiple gods, Yahweh among them; early Iron Age
2. **Monolatry/henotheism** — Yahweh as Israel's exclusive god without denying other gods exist; the requirement is exclusive loyalty, not metaphysical uniqueness
3. **Ethical monotheism** — the prophetic tradition insists Yahweh is not only Israel's god but the universal moral sovereign; the gods of other nations are nothing
4. **Strict monotheism** — Deutero-Isaiah (Isaiah 40–55), written during Babylonian exile: "Besides me there is no god." The other gods are explicitly demoted to nothing — not real beings. This is a major theological step beyond monolatry.

The Babylonian exile (587–539 BCE) is the crucible. Jerusalem destroyed, temple gone, Davidic monarchy ended — the theological problem of why this happened forces a radical rethinking. The answer: Yahweh is not defeated; Yahweh is universal. Babylon is his instrument of punishment.

---

## The prophetic tradition

The classical prophets (8th–6th century) are the most important drivers of theological development:

- **Amos** (mid-8th c.) — earliest; social justice, Yahweh's universal sovereignty over all nations, Israel will be punished for injustice
- **Hosea** — Baal-worship as covenant infidelity, marriage metaphor for Israel's relationship with Yahweh
- **Isaiah of Jerusalem** (late 8th c.) — holiness of Yahweh, Assyrian crisis, remnant theology
- **Jeremiah** — temple critique, new covenant, lived through Jerusalem's fall
- **Ezekiel** — priestly prophet in Babylon; divine chariot (merkavah), vision of temple restoration; later foundational for Jewish mysticism
- **Deutero-Isaiah** — explicit monotheism, servant songs, return from exile as new Exodus

The prophets consistently argue against what the population actually practiced — which is how we know popular practice was substantially different from what the prophetic-Deuteronomic program demanded.

---

## The Deuteronomistic History

The books of Joshua, Judges, Samuel, and Kings form a coherent theological narrative (the Deuteronomistic History, DH) written or heavily edited in the 7th–6th century under Deuteronomic influence. The theological logic is:
- National success follows Yahweh-obedience
- National disaster follows apostasy
- The destruction of Israel (722 BCE) and Judah (587 BCE) are thus explained as punishment for syncretism

This retrospective framework explains why earlier periods look worse than later reforms. The DH is not neutral historical reporting — it is theological advocacy read backward.

## Sacrifice, temple, and ritual

Pre-destruction Israelite religion was a sacrificial cult centered on temples (plural, not just Jerusalem):

- **Types of sacrifice**: burnt offering (olah), peace offering (shelamim), sin offering (hattat), first-fruits, grain offerings
- **Temple personnel**: priests (kohanim), Levites, prophets in some periods attached to temple
- **Festivals**: Passover (originally a pastoral/spring rite, later historicized to the Exodus), Shavuot (harvest), Sukkot (ingathering)
- **The Ark of the Covenant** — portable sacred object, divine throne or footstool; eventually housed in Solomon's temple
- **The Holy of Holies** — innermost temple chamber; no image, but divine presence understood to dwell there; high priest enters once annually on Yom Kippur

Post-exile, the destruction of the temple forces transformation: sacrifice cannot continue without the temple. Torah study and prayer become substitutes. This sets up the rabbinic revolution that creates classical Judaism.

---

## The divine council and the Sons of God

Early Hebrew texts preserve traces of a more complex divine world:
- **Bene ha-Elohim** (sons of God) — divine beings who appear in Job, Genesis 6 (who marry human women), Psalm 82; later demonized into rebellious angels
- **The Satan** — in Job and Zechariah, not the adversary of later Christian theology but a member of the divine council functioning as a prosecutor/challenger
- **Cherubim and Seraphim** — divine beings in temple imagery and prophetic visions (Isaiah 6, Ezekiel 1)
- Later development reinterprets the divine council as the angelic court, and the other gods as fallen angels — monotheism is maintained by demotion, not deletion

---

## What this means for later traditions

- **Judaism**: the Babylonian exile produces both strict monotheism and the Torah as a written, portable, studied text — the foundations of Second Temple Judaism and eventually rabbinic Judaism
- **Christianity**: inherits the late Jewish theological framework (monotheism, scripture, apocalypticism, messianism) but reintroduces divine complexity through the Trinity
- **Islam**: explicitly corrects both Judaism and Christianity; claims to restore pure Abrahamic monotheism; Yahweh, God of Israel, and Allah are understood as the same deity
- **Zoroastrianism connection**: Persian Zoroastrianism during the Achaemenid period (when Persian kings permit the Jews to return) almost certainly influenced Jewish theology — particularly angelology, eschatology (resurrection, last judgment), and cosmic dualism

---

## Key scholarly debates
- Whether "monotheism" is even the right category, or whether exclusive henotheism is more accurate
- The relationship between Israelite and Canaanite religion — once treated as polar opposites, now read as variants of the same regional tradition
- The Asherah question — whether she was Yahweh's consort in popular practice, or only in heterodox circles, or more widely accepted than the Bible admits
- Whether the Exodus narrative has any historical basis (most archaeologists doubt a literal mass Exodus; the story may preserve memories of small-scale migrations and slave labor in Egypt)
- The dating and composition of the Pentateuch — Documentary Hypothesis (JEDP sources), supplementary models, and newer approaches all remain contested

- Mark S. Smith's *The Early History of God* and *The Origins of Biblical Monotheism* are the essential starting points
- Frank Moore Cross — *Canaanite Myth and Hebrew Epic* — foundational for El/Yahweh merger argument
- William G. Dever — archaeological evidence for popular religion, Asherah inscriptions

- Richard Elliott Friedman — *Who Wrote the Bible?* — accessible introduction to source criticism
- Do not use the Bible as a neutral historical source; it is the most important primary source but requires critical handling
