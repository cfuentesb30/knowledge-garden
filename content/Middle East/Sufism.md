# Sufism

## Purpose
Islamic mysticism — its origins, core concepts, major figures, orders (*tariqas*), controversies within Islam, and its broader spiritual and cultural output. For the Islamic framework it operates within, see [[Islam Overview]].

## What Sufism is
Sufism (*tasawwuf*) is the inner, mystical, or esoteric dimension of Islam — concerned with direct experiential knowledge of God (*ma'rifa*) and the purification of the soul toward union with the divine. It developed from the earliest centuries of Islam and has produced some of the most influential mystical literature in world history.

The term *tasawwuf* probably derives from *suf* (wool) — referring to the simple woolen garments worn by early Muslim ascetics as a marker of detachment from worldly goods.

## Origins — early Islamic piety and asceticism
The immediate post-Muhammad context produced intense debate about wealth, worldliness, and the meaning of piety. Two currents:
- **Zahid** (ascetics): renunciation of the world, intense prayer, poverty
- **'Arif** (knowers): those who sought inner knowledge beyond law and ritual

**Hasan al-Basri** (642–728): Often cited as a proto-Sufi — an early ascetic and preacher in Basra who emphasized *khawf* (fear of God) and *zuhd* (renunciation). He represents the transition from simple piety to structured interiority.

**Rabi'a al-Adawiyya** (c. 717–801): Female mystic from Basra, one of the first to articulate *mahabba* (love of God) as the central motivation — not fear, not hope for heaven, but pure love. Her poetry expressing selfless love of God is foundational.

## Core concepts

### Fana and Baqa
The most important experiential categories:
- **Fana** (annihilation): the dissolution of the ego-self in God — not literal death, but the falling away of the illusory separate self
- **Baqa** (subsistence): what remains after fana — the mystic subsists in God, having shed the ego but continuing to act in the world

This maps loosely onto Buddhist nirvana/parinirvana, Hindu moksha, and Jewish *devekut* (cleaving to God) — but with distinctive Islamic theological framing.

### Maqamat and Ahwal
The Sufi path (*tariqa*, the Way) involves:
- **Maqamat** (stations): permanent spiritual achievements gained through effort — repentance, renunciation, trust in God (*tawakkul*), contentment, etc.
- **Ahwal** (states): transient spiritual conditions granted by God — joy, grief, longing, expansion, contraction — not earned but given

The classical lists of stations differ between masters, but the general arc is: repentance → renunciation → trust → love → ma'rifa (gnosis) → fana.

### Ma'rifa — mystical knowledge
*Ma'rifa* is direct, experiential, non-propositional knowledge of God — beyond the discursive knowledge of theology (*'ilm*). It is what the Sufi path aims at: not information about God but presence with God. This is the Islamic cognate of *gnosis*, *jnana*, *theoria* in other traditions.

### Dhikr (remembrance)
The core Sufi practice — rhythmic repetition of divine names or phrases (most commonly *La ilaha illa Allah* — "There is no god but God" or simply *Allah*) as a method of focusing consciousness on God, stilling the ego, and eventually entering altered contemplative states. Done individually and communally; communal dhikr is often accompanied by music and movement.

### Sama' (spiritual audition)
Listening to music, poetry, and song as a means of spiritual awakening. Deeply controversial within Islam — the question of whether music is licit for religious purposes generated centuries of debate. The most famous practice: the Mevlevi *sema*, the whirling ceremony of the Whirling Dervishes.

## Major figures

### Al-Hallaj (858–922)
Al-Husayn ibn Mansur al-Hallaj — one of the most controversial figures in Sufi history. His famous declaration *Ana al-Haqq* ("I am the Truth/God") was interpreted by critics as claiming divinity and by supporters as an expression of fana. He was arrested, imprisoned for years, and executed in Baghdad (922). He became the paradigmatic martyr-mystic — his death raised the fundamental question of whether the highest mystical states could be compatible with Islamic law and theology.

### Al-Ghazali (1058–1111)
Abu Hamid al-Ghazali — the most important single figure for reconciling Sufism with mainstream Sunni Islam. His *Ihya' Ulum al-Din* (Revival of the Religious Sciences) is one of the most widely read books in Islamic history — a systematic integration of Sufi interiority with legal practice and theology. Al-Ghazali legitimized Sufism for the broader Islamic world by showing it was the fulfillment, not the transgression, of Islamic practice. His autobiography *Deliverance from Error* describes his spiritual crisis and turn toward Sufism.

### Ibn Arabi (1165–1240)
Muhyi al-Din ibn Arabi — possibly the most philosophically sophisticated Sufi thinker. Born in Murcia (al-Andalus), eventually settled in Damascus. His *Fusus al-Hikam* (Bezels of Wisdom) and *Futuhat al-Makkiyya* (Meccan Openings) developed the doctrine of *wahdat al-wujud* (Unity of Being): there is only one being — God — and all apparent multiplicity is a manifestation within that singular reality. Everything that exists is God in different modes of self-disclosure (*tajalli*).

This was hugely controversial — critics read it as pantheism or apostasy. Supporters read it as the most rigorous statement of Islamic metaphysics. Ibn Arabi's influence on subsequent Islamic thought (and on Western esotericism through Latin translations) was enormous.

### Rumi (1207–1273)
Jalal al-Din Muhammad Rumi — Persian-language mystical poet, founder of the Mevlevi order. Born in Khorasan (present-day Afghanistan), died in Konya (present-day Turkey). His *Masnavi* (6 books, ~25,000 couplets) is considered one of the greatest poems in world literature. His *Divan-e Shams* (Divan of Shams) — ecstatic lyric poetry named for his spiritual companion and catalyst Shams-i Tabrizi — expresses the burning love-longing of the soul for God.

Rumi is currently the best-selling poet in the United States. The popular translations by Coleman Barks, while beautiful, are not literal and remove much of the Islamic theological content — a significant distortion that presents Rumi as a generic universalist poet rather than a Muslim mystic.

### Hafez (c. 1315–1390)
Persian lyric poet. *Divan of Hafez* — poems using wine, love, and intoxication as extended metaphors for spiritual longing and divine love. The *ghazal* form in Persian Sufi poetry uses the imagery of wine-houses, taverns, and intoxication as spiritual allegory (the forbidden nature of the images is part of the point — transgressive surface, sacred interior).

## The Tariqa system — Sufi orders
From the 12th century onward, Sufism organized itself into *tariqas* (orders, paths) — transmission lineages from a founding master (*pir*, *shaykh*) through an unbroken chain of teachers (*silsila*) to the present. Major orders:

- **Qadiriyya**: Founded by Abd al-Qadir al-Jilani (1077–1166); one of the most widespread globally (North Africa, South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia)
- **Shadhiliyya**: North Africa; influential in Egypt, Morocco, and diaspora communities; known for integration with daily life rather than withdrawal
- **Mevleviyya**: Rumi's order, centered in Konya; known for the whirling ceremony; major influence on Ottoman court culture
- **Naqshbandiyya**: Central Asia; emphasizes *silent* dhikr (interior) rather than vocal; highly influential in the Ottoman empire, Central Asia, South Asia, and into China
- **Chishtiyya**: South Asia (India/Pakistan); known for sama' and use of music; deeply embedded in Subcontinental religious life; shrine culture
- **Tijaniyya**: West Africa (founded 18th century in Morocco); massive following in Senegal, Mali, Nigeria, etc.

The *silsila* (chain) matters: each order traces its transmission back to the Prophet Muhammad through specific chains, giving the order's teaching its authority.

## Sufism and orthodoxy — the tension
The relationship between Sufism and mainstream Islamic law (*shari'a*) has never been fully resolved:
- Al-Ghazali represents the "Sufism fulfills the law" position
- Al-Hallaj's execution represents the risk of mystical claims being read as heresy
- Salafi and Wahhabi Islam view Sufism — especially shrine veneration, saint intercession, and sama' — as *bid'a* (forbidden innovation) or shirk (associating others with God)
- Ibn Taimiyya (13th–14th century) critiqued Sufi shrine practices intensely; his influence shaped later reformist movements including Wahhabism

Modern conflicts: Wahhabi/Salafi movements have destroyed Sufi shrines in Saudi Arabia, Libya, Mali, and elsewhere. The bombing of the Lal Shahbaz Qalandar shrine in Pakistan (2017) and attacks on Sufi mosques in Egypt are part of this ongoing violence.

## Sufi shrine culture
Throughout the Islamic world, the tombs of *awliya* (saints, "friends of God") function as pilgrimage sites. Petitioners seek *baraka* (blessing/spiritual power) from the saint. Annually or weekly, communities gather for *'urs* (death anniversary celebrations) that include music, dhikr, and sometimes ecstatic practice.

This practice — shrine pilgrimage, intercession of saints, *tawassul* — is one of the most contested in contemporary Islam.

## Sufism and Western esotericism
Sufism influenced Western esotericism through:
- Medieval Iberia and Sicily (translation contact between Arab and European scholarship)
- The *troubadour* tradition may have absorbed Sufi-influenced concepts of divine love poetry from al-Andalus
- In the 19th–20th century, figures like the Comte de Saint-Martin and later René Guénon (who converted to Islam and joined a Shadhili order) brought Sufi ideas into Western esoteric circles
- G.I. Gurdjieff drew extensively on Sufi ideas (especially Naqshbandi-adjacent)
- Idries Shah's books (1960s–1980s) presented "Sufism" to Western audiences — heavily criticized by traditional Muslims and scholars as syncretic and decontextualized

## Research notes
- The popular English Rumi (Coleman Barks) is a major distortion — remove the Islamic context and you have a different spiritual product. Worth knowing for the cultural phenomenon but not as a source on Sufism
- Ibn Arabi requires significant background to read — not an introductory text
- The divide between Sufism as a living Islamic practice and "Sufism" as Western spiritual product (Universalist Sufism, neo-Sufism) is significant and should be maintained analytically
- Sufism is not uniform — there are profound differences between the rigorous metaphysics of Ibn Arabi, the ecstatic love poetry of Rumi, the sober legalism of the Naqshbandis, and the shrine culture of the Chishtis
