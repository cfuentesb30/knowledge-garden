# Islam Overview

## Scope
Islam is a monotheistic Abrahamic tradition centered on God's oneness, prophetic revelation, the Qur'an, ritual practice, moral order, sacred law, and community.

## Why it matters
Islam matters not only as one of the largest religions in the world, but as a civilizational formation that shapes law, politics, ritual time, philosophy, empire, trade, mysticism, and intellectual history across multiple continents.
A serious overview has to hold together all of these layers at once.

## Foundational dates and period
- **c. 570 CE** — birth of Muhammad in Mecca
- **610 CE** — first revelations begin
- **622 CE** — Hijra (migration from Mecca to Medina), beginning of the Islamic calendar
- **632 CE** — death of Muhammad
- **7th–8th centuries CE** — rapid expansion under early caliphates

## Core theological center
### Tawhid
**Tawhid** is the absolute oneness of God.
This is not simply monotheism as a number count. It is a claim about ultimate sovereignty, uniqueness, and unshared divinity.

### Prophethood
Muhammad is understood as the final prophet in a longer prophetic chain that includes figures known from Jewish and Christian traditions.
That gives Islam both continuity and decisive finality.

## Central texts and authority
### Qur'an
The Qur'an is revealed scripture in Arabic, treated not merely as inspired commentary but as the word of God.
That makes recitation, preservation, and interpretation central.

### Hadith
Hadith are reports about the sayings and actions of Muhammad.
They become crucial for law, ritual, and moral practice, though questions of authenticity and transmission remain central to the tradition itself.

## Five Pillars
1. **Shahada** — declaration of faith
2. **Salat** — ritual prayer, traditionally five times daily
3. **Zakat** — almsgiving
4. **Sawm** — fasting in Ramadan
5. **Hajj** — pilgrimage to Mecca if able

## Ritual and religious life
- daily prayer facing Mecca
- fasting in Ramadan
- communal Friday prayer
- Qur'an recitation
- charity and legal-ethical discipline
- pilgrimage cycles and sacred geography
- life structured through sacred time and discipline rather than occasional private devotion alone

## Law and interpretation
Islam is one of the major traditions in which sacred law becomes a central structuring force.
This means:
- jurisprudence matters
- legal interpretation matters
- schools and methods matter
- scripture alone is not the whole story; interpretive traditions are decisive

## Key internal distinctions
### Sunni
Majority branch, emphasizing community continuity and legal-scholarly traditions.

### Shia
Minority major branch centered on questions of succession, authority, and later distinctive teachings around the Imams.

### Sufism
Mystical and devotional currents found across Islam, emphasizing inward discipline, remembrance, spiritual masters, and nearness to God.

## Historical significance
Islam becomes not only a religion but a civilizational matrix shaping:
- law
- philosophy
- trade
- empire
- art
- sacred geography
- theology
- mysticism
- education and scholarship

## Why it matters comparatively
Islam is essential for studying:
- revelation and prophecy
- scripture and recitation
- sacred law and jurisprudence
- ritual time
- empire and religion
- mystical discipline alongside legal tradition

## Good working summary
Islam is a scriptural, ritual, legal, and civilizational tradition centered on divine unity, revelation, disciplined practice, and community order. It is impossible to understand properly if reduced to belief statements without law, ritual time, interpretation, and history.
