# Zoroastrianism

## Purpose
One of the most consequential religious traditions in world history — not for its current size but for its structural influence on Jewish, Christian, Islamic, and Gnostic thinking. Understanding Zoroastrianism is required for understanding where Abrahamic dualism, apocalypticism, and angelology actually come from.

## What it is
Zoroastrianism is the religion founded by the prophet Zarathustra (Greek: Zoroaster), arising in the Iranian plateau, probably between 1500–600 BCE (dating is contested). It was the state religion of three Persian Empires: the Achaemenid (550–330 BCE), Parthian (247 BCE–224 CE), and Sasanian (224–651 CE). After the Islamic conquest of Persia, it declined sharply; its remaining practitioners today are the Parsis (India, ~60,000) and Iranian Zoroastrians (~25,000).

This is a religion that shaped world history as an imperial framework and then was largely absorbed or displaced — but its ideas survived by being taken up into the traditions that replaced it.

## Zarathustra — the prophet

### Who he was
Zarathustra was a priest (*zaotar*) of the older Iranian religion who underwent a visionary experience and reformed the tradition. The Gathas (17 hymns preserved in the Avesta) are attributed directly to him and are considered his own compositions — making them one of the very few surviving texts credibly attributed to a religion's founder.

His reform: he demoted the older Iranian gods (*daeva*, cognate with Sanskrit *deva*) to the status of demons, elevated Ahura Mazda as the one supreme deity, and restructured the cosmic battle between good and evil as the defining feature of existence.

### Dating problem
The dating of Zarathustra is among the most contested questions in Iranian studies:
- Traditional Zoroastrian chronology: ~600 BCE
- Linguistic evidence from the Gathas: possibly as early as 1500–1200 BCE
- Most scholars: somewhere between 1200–600 BCE
- The problem matters because it affects whether Zoroastrianism influenced the Babylonian exile (and thus Jewish theology) or was itself influenced by contact with Jewish communities

## Core theology

### Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu
The cosmos is structured by conflict between two principles:

- **Ahura Mazda** ("Wise Lord"): supreme deity of truth, light, creation, and order (*asha*)
- **Angra Mainyu** / **Ahriman** ("Destructive Spirit"): the opposing principle of lie, darkness, destruction, and chaos (*druj*)

This is not a fully equal dualism: Ahura Mazda is the ultimate good and will prevail. But the current world is a battleground between these forces, and humans are required to choose sides actively.

This framework is the single most important conceptual source for the cosmic dualism that later enters Jewish intertestamental literature, Christian demonology, and Gnostic cosmology.

### Asha and Druj
*Asha* (truth/order/righteousness) vs. *Druj* (lie/chaos/disorder) is the fundamental moral-cosmic opposition. Every act, thought, and word belongs to one side or the other. There is no neutral ground.

This is not merely moral teaching — it is a cosmic physics. Ahura Mazda's creative acts are acts of *asha*; everything that disrupts, destroys, or deceives belongs to *druj*.

### The divine council — Amesha Spentas
Ahura Mazda is attended by six Amesha Spentas (Bountiful Immortals), which function somewhat like archangels:

| Amesha Spenta | Meaning | Domain |
|---|---|---|
| Vohu Manah | Good Mind | Cattle/animals |
| Asha Vahishta | Best Truth | Fire |
| Khshathra Vairya | Desirable Dominion | Metals |
| Spenta Armaiti | Holy Devotion | Earth |
| Haurvatat | Wholeness | Water |
| Ameretat | Immortality | Plants |

These six + Ahura Mazda = seven, correlating with seven sacred elements/domains. This angelic-being-as-cosmic-administrator model is structurally similar to and likely ancestral to the Judeo-Christian/Islamic angelic hierarchy.

### Yazatas — lesser divine beings
Below the Amesha Spentas are the *Yazatas* ("worshipful ones") — divine beings associated with fire, water, the sun, justice, etc. Among the most important:
- **Mithra**: deity of covenant, contract, and light (later became extremely significant in the Roman Mithraic mysteries)
- **Anahita**: deity of water and fertility
- **Sraosha**: obedience, the divine messenger
- **Atar**: sacred fire

## The afterlife and eschatology

### Individual judgment
After death, the soul crosses the Chinvat Bridge (*Chinvat Peretu* — "Bridge of the Separator"). At the bridge, good deeds, words, and thoughts are weighed against bad ones:
- The righteous cross easily to paradise (*Gar-o-demana*, "House of Song")
- The wicked fall from the bridge into hell
- Those whose deeds are balanced go to an intermediate state (*Hamistagan*)

This individual judgment model — bridge/weighing/heaven or hell — is the clearest precursor to the Islamic judgment at the Sirat bridge and has influenced Jewish and Christian afterlife architecture.

### Cosmic eschatology — Frashokereti
At the end of time, a final savior (*Saoshyant*, "one who benefits") — born of a virgin from Zarathustra's preserved seed — will bring about *Frashokereti* (the "making wonderful"):
- The dead are resurrected
- A final judgment occurs
- A river of molten metal purifies all souls (the wicked suffer; the righteous experience it as warm milk)
- Angra Mainyu and evil are destroyed
- The world is renewed into an immortal, perfect state

This eschatological structure — resurrection of the dead, final judgment, world renewal, messianic figure — is the template for Jewish apocalyptic literature (Daniel, Enoch), and through Jewish transmission, for Christian and Islamic eschatology. The Babylonian exile (586–538 BCE) placed Jews in the Persian sphere precisely when this theology was shaping Persian imperial religion.

## Sacred fire and ritual

### The fire temple
Zoroastrian worship centers on fire as the visible presence of Ahura Mazda — not as a god itself but as the medium of divine presence. Eternal fires are maintained in temples, tended by priests.
- **Atash Bahram**: highest grade of sacred fire, requires an immense purification process to establish
- **Atash Adaran**: fire for warriors/nobles
- **Atash Dadgah**: hearth fire

Purity is paramount: pollution (dead matter, bodily secretions, fire contamination) requires extensive ritual cleansing.

### The Haoma ritual
The Haoma (*soma* in Sanskrit — cognate) was a ritual drink made from a pressed plant, consumed in liturgy. The exact plant is debated (candidates include ephedra, cannabis, fly agaric). The Haoma ritual connects Zoroastrianism to the broader Indo-Iranian ritual complex that also produced the Vedic soma ceremony.

### Towers of Silence (Dakhmas)
The dead body, contaminated by Angra Mainyu, must not pollute the sacred elements (fire, earth, water). Traditional Zoroastrian disposal: expose the body on a stone tower (*dakhma*, "Tower of Silence") where birds of prey consume it. The bones are then treated with lime. This practice continues among the Parsis in India, though it has become difficult in urban environments.

## Zoroastrianism and the Abrahamic traditions

### The Babylonian exile connection
The Jews were in Babylonian captivity from 586 BCE. Cyrus the Great of Persia conquered Babylon in 538 BCE, freed the Jews, and allowed them to return to Judah. He is described in Isaiah 45:1 as God's "anointed" (*mashiach* — messiah). The Persian period was when Jewish theology received the greatest outside influence, and Zoroastrian concepts of:
- A cosmic adversary (Satan develops from *hassatan* into a named cosmic evil)
- Resurrection of the dead (absent from most earlier Hebrew texts, prominent in Daniel)
- Angels with names and specific functions
- Apocalyptic end-times narrative
...all develop in Jewish literature during and after Persian contact.

### The Magi
The *Magi* (from Old Iranian *maga*, possibly related to *magician*) were the Zoroastrian priestly class, expert in astronomy/astrology, fire ritual, and sacred knowledge. They appear in Christian nativity accounts (Matthew 2) as wise men from the East following a star. This is a direct reference to Zoroastrian priestly astronomers from the Persian sphere.

### Influence on Gnosticism and Manichaeism
Zoroastrian cosmic dualism fed directly into:
- **Gnosticism**: the demiurge as an evil or ignorant creator in a world of darkness and trapped divine sparks — structurally parallel to Zoroastrian Angra Mainyu's corrupted material world
- **Manichaeism** (3rd century CE, founded by Mani): an explicit synthesis of Zoroastrian dualism, Christian narrative, and Buddhist/Gnostic elements. Mani called himself the final prophet after Zarathustra, Buddha, and Jesus. Manichaeism spread from Persia to Rome, North Africa (Augustine was a Manichaean for nine years), Central Asia, and China.

## Texts — the Avesta
The sacred scripture. Much was destroyed by Alexander the Great's conquest of Persia (330 BCE) and later the Islamic conquest. What survives:
- **Gathas**: 17 hymns attributed to Zarathustra — oldest and most authoritative
- **Yasna**: liturgical texts including the Gathas
- **Visperad**: supplementary liturgical material
- **Yashts**: hymns to specific Yazatas (Mithra, Anahita, etc.)
- **Vendidad** (*Videvdat*): purity law, creation narrative, rules for dealing with pollution
- **Khorda Avesta**: prayers for daily use

## Research notes
- The dating of Zarathustra is genuinely unresolved — don't commit to a single date without acknowledging the uncertainty
- The Zoroastrian influence on Judaism is significant but debated in scope; most scholars accept structural parallels; direct causation vs. parallel development is argued
- Mithraism (Roman mystery cult) drew from Zoroastrian/Iranian religious imagery but was not simply transplanted Zoroastrianism — it was a Roman mystery cult using Iranian symbols
- Mary Boyce's work remains foundational; some of it has been updated by more recent scholarship
