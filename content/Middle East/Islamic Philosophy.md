# Islamic Philosophy

## Purpose
Map the tradition of Islamic philosophical theology: the translation movement, the major falasifa (philosophers), the Mu'tazila/Ash'ari theological schools, and the long dispute over reason vs. revelation that shapes Islamic intellectual history to the present.

---

## Why this matters

Islamic philosophy is one of the most important and least known chapters in the history of ideas for general readers. It is:
- the primary transmission channel through which Aristotelian philosophy reached medieval Europe
- an independent philosophical tradition of enormous sophistication, not merely a relay station
- the context in which problems about reason and revelation, free will and divine power, the nature of God and the soul were worked out rigorously over centuries

It is not simply "Muslim Aristotle." The Islamic philosophers (falasifa) engaged Neoplatonism, Aristotle, Plato, and Galen while working inside (and sometimes against) Islamic theological concerns.

---

## The Translation Movement (8th–10th century)

Baghdad under the Abbasid caliphs — particularly Harun al-Rashid and al-Ma'mun (the latter founded the Bayt al-Hikma, "House of Wisdom") — sponsored a massive translation project:

- Greek philosophical, medical, and scientific texts translated into Arabic (via Syriac in many cases, then directly from Greek)
- Aristotle, Plato, Plotinus, Galen, Hippocrates, Euclid, Ptolemy — all available in Arabic by the 10th century
- The translations were not passive: Arabic-speaking scholars annotated, corrected, and extended the Greek texts

This is what made subsequent European scholasticism possible. When 12th-century translators in Toledo and Sicily turned Arabic texts into Latin, they were recovering Greek philosophy that had survived in the Islamic world, not the Latin West.

## The Mu'tazila (8th–10th century)

The first major theological school in Islam; the term means roughly "those who withdraw" (origin of the name is disputed).

**Core positions:**
- **Tawhid (divine unity) taken to its logical extreme**: God cannot have attributes that are in any way separate from his essence. Human language about God must be interpreted metaphorically or negatively (via negativa). Anthropomorphic Quran verses must be allegorized.
- **Divine justice (adl)**: God cannot do evil or act unjustly — this is not a limitation on God but a logical truth. If God commands something, it is because it is genuinely good (not good simply because God commands it). This places them in tension with voluntarist divine command theory.
- **Human free will**: Humans have genuine free will and thus genuine moral responsibility. God does not create human actions — humans create their own actions. This is required for justice (God cannot punish people for actions God compelled).
- **The Quran is created**: The Quran is God's word but was created in time (not co-eternal with God). Claiming the Quran is eternal seemed to compromise divine unity by positing something eternal alongside God.

**The Mihna (833–848)**: Caliph al-Ma'mun imposed the Mu'tazilite position (Quran is created) as state doctrine and persecuted those who disagreed. Ahmad ibn Hanbal's refusal and imprisonment became the founding narrative of Sunni traditionalism's resistance to rationalist overreach.

The Mu'tazila eventually lost court patronage and were marginalized. But their problems and methods shaped all subsequent Islamic theology.

## Al-Ash'ari and Ash'arism (10th century onward)

**Abu al-Hasan al-Ash'ari** (874–936) — former Mu'tazili student who broke with his teacher and developed a middle position that became Sunni theological orthodoxy.

**Ash'arite positions:**
- God has real attributes, but they are not identical to his essence and not separate from it — a middle path
- The Quran is God's uncreated word in its essential meaning, though the ink and paper are created
- **Divine voluntarism**: God's power is absolutely unlimited. Good and evil are defined by what God commands, not by independent rational standards (reversing Mu'tazila)
- **Atomism**: the world is composed of atoms and accidents; God continuously re-creates each moment — there are no necessary causal chains, only God's habitual patterns (which is how miracles are possible)
- **Human action**: humans acquire (kasb) their actions, but God creates them — a compromise between free will and divine omnipotence that many critics found incoherent

Ash'arism became the dominant theological school of Sunni Islam, particularly through the work of al-Ghazali.

## The Falasifa: philosophers proper

### Al-Kindi (c. 801–873) — "Philosopher of the Arabs"
The first systematic Islamic philosopher. Tried to show Greek philosophy and Islam are compatible. Worked on optics, cosmology, music theory, metaphysics. His *On First Philosophy* argues for a single Creator-God through philosophical argument. Key: distinguishes between divine knowledge and human knowledge.

### Al-Farabi (c. 872–950) — "Second Teacher" (Aristotle being the first)
Deeply Neoplatonist and Aristotelian. Major works on logic, political philosophy, metaphysics, music.

- **Emanation cosmology**: God (the First Cause) emanates necessary beings in a hierarchical chain (the Active Intellect, celestial spheres, etc.) — structured by Neoplatonic logic. This is philosophically elegant but creates problems for Islamic creation doctrine (eternal emanation vs. temporal creation).
- **Political philosophy**: the ideal state is governed by a philosopher-prophet who has the highest intellectual gifts and can translate philosophical truth into symbolic/religious form for the masses — an explicitly Platonic scheme applied to the Prophet Muhammad.
- **Happiness**: the goal of human life is intellectual contemplation; the philosopher achieves union with the Active Intellect.

### Ibn Sina / Avicenna (980–1037) — the dominant philosophical figure
The most comprehensive and influential Islamic philosopher. His medical encyclopedia (*Canon of Medicine*) was used in European universities until the 17th century. His philosophical system (*Kitab al-Shifa*, Book of Healing) is the most complete philosophical synthesis of the Islamic world.

**Key contributions:**
- **Floating Man thought experiment**: imagine yourself suspended in air, sensory deprivation, no bodily awareness — you would still be aware that you exist. This proves the soul's independence of the body; the soul is a self-aware substance. Often compared to Descartes's cogito.
- **Distinction of essence and existence**: existence is not part of the essence of anything except God. Everything that exists has its existence added to its essence from outside — from God. God alone has necessary existence (wujud wajib), where essence and existence are identical. Everything else has contingent existence.
- **The Active Intellect**: the final of the Neoplatonic emanated intellects; the source of both forms in the material world and intellectual illumination in humans; knowledge comes from "illumination" by the Active Intellect.
- **Soul and immortality**: the soul is a substance that uses the body as instrument; it survives death.
- **Psychology**: his elaborate psychology of the soul — vegetative, animal, rational — became standard through much of medieval Islamic and European thought.

Avicenna's system is philosophically powerful but raised serious questions for orthodox theology: emanation (not creation), the eternity of the world, God's knowledge being universal not particular, and the rejection of bodily resurrection.

### Al-Ghazali (1058–1111) — the great critic
**Not a philosopher** in the falasifa tradition — a theologian and Sufi. But his *Tahafut al-Falasifa* (Incoherence of the Philosophers) is one of the most important texts in Islamic intellectual history.

Al-Ghazali attacks the falasifa on 20 positions, identifying three as outright unbelief:
1. The eternity of the world (contrary to creation)
2. God's knowledge is universal, not of particulars (God doesn't know individual events)
3. The rejection of bodily resurrection in favor of only spiritual resurrection

Beyond these he argued that the philosophers' claimed certainty was philosophically unwarranted — their "demonstrations" were not logically compelling.

**His positive position**: Ash'arite theology plus Sufi practice; his *Ihya Ulum al-Din* (Revival of the Religious Sciences) is the systematic synthesis of Islamic law, theology, and Sufi spirituality; one of the most read books in Islamic history.

**Was al-Ghazali's critique fatal to Islamic philosophy?** In the Eastern Islamic world, the critique was largely accepted; independent philosophical speculation declined after him. In the West (Andalusia/Spain), it produced a counterattack.

### Ibn Rushd / Averroes (1126–1198) — the great defender
Andalusian philosopher, physician, legal scholar. Wrote *Tahafut al-Tahafut* (Incoherence of the Incoherence) directly answering al-Ghazali.

**Key positions:**
- The eternity of the world is not theologically problematic if properly understood — al-Ghazali misunderstood what the philosophers meant
- Reason and revelation cannot genuinely conflict; apparent conflicts require allegorical interpretation of revelation
- **Double truth** (attributed to Averroes, though he may not have held it explicitly): philosophy and religion are parallel paths to truth for different audiences — philosophy for those capable of rational demonstration; theology for those who reason discursively; scripture for the masses who follow rhetoric
- The **Active Intellect is universal** — all humans share one intellect; this raised the question of individual immortality in ways orthodox critics attacked

Averroes's commentaries on Aristotle — *the* commentaries — were translated into Latin and drove the 13th-century scholastic revolution in Europe. His influence on Latin Scholasticism (Aquinas, Siger of Brabant, the "Latin Averroists") was immense and direct.

### Ibn Tufayl (c. 1105–1185)
Wrote *Hayy ibn Yaqzan* ("Living son of Awake") — a philosophical novel. A child raised alone on a deserted island by a gazelle arrives through unaided rational observation at the same truth as revealed religion. Argues natural reason and revelation converge. Influenced Rousseau and early modern European thought.

### Ibn Khaldun (1332–1406) — political philosophy and historiography
Not strictly a metaphysician but the most original political thinker of the Islamic tradition. *Muqaddimah* (Introduction to History):
- **Asabiyya** (social cohesion/group feeling): the binding force that makes civilizations rise and fall; desert peoples have strong asabiyya, urban people lose it
- Cyclical theory of dynastic rise and fall
- The first systematic attempt at a "science of civilization" — a sociological theory of history
- Recognized by modern scholars as a precursor to sociology, economics, and historiography as disciplines

## The Mu'tazila/Ash'ari debate in brief

| Issue | Mu'tazila | Ash'arism |
|---|---|---|
| Divine attributes | No real attributes distinct from essence | Real attributes, not identical to but not separate from essence |
| Quran | Created in time | Uncreated in essential meaning |
| Good and evil | Rational/objective | Defined by divine command |
| Human free will | Real free will | Acquisition (kasb); God creates acts |
| God's knowledge | Universals only (with some) | Universals and particulars |

## Legacy and influence

**On Europe**: Without the Arabic transmission and commentary tradition, the 12th–13th century European rediscovery of Aristotle would have been far less complete. Aquinas's Scholasticism, Dante's philosophical poetry, and the later Renaissance all depend partly on this tradition.

**On Islamic thought**: Sufism (particularly Ibn Arabi, see Sufism note) absorbed significant Neoplatonic philosophical elements. The madrasa system taught logic and philosophy as tools for jurisprudence. The tension between philosophical rationalism and religious authority recurs in every generation of Islamic thought.

**Modern period**: Islamic modernists (al-Afghani, Abduh) and Islamists alike have had to take positions on reason vs. revelation that echo these older debates.
