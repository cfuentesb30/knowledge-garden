# Shi'a Islam

## Purpose
Shi'a Islam as a distinct theological, political, and devotional tradition within Islam — its origins in the succession crisis, its distinctive doctrines (Imamate, Mahdi, intercession), its ritual life (Ashura, pilgrimage, passion), and its major branches. For the broader Islamic framework, see [[Islam Overview]]. For Islamic mysticism, see [[Sufism]].

## The foundational rupture — succession after Muhammad

### The crisis of 632 CE
When the Prophet Muhammad died without designating a clear successor, the Muslim community fractured. Two positions:
- **Sunni position**: The community should choose a leader (*caliph*) by consultation among qualified elders. Abu Bakr, Muhammad's father-in-law and close companion, was chosen as the first caliph.
- **Shi'a position**: Muhammad had designated his cousin and son-in-law **Ali ibn Abi Talib** as his rightful successor — most explicitly at Ghadir Khumm, where Muhammad reportedly declared "Whoever considers me their master (*mawla*), Ali is also their master." The first three caliphs (Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman) were usurpers.

*Shi'a* derives from *Shi'at Ali* — "the party/faction of Ali."

### The Battle of Karbala (680 CE) — the defining trauma
Ali eventually became the fourth caliph (656 CE) but was assassinated in 661. His son Hasan renounced the caliphate. Ali's second son **Husayn** refused to pledge allegiance to the Umayyad caliph Yazid I, whom he considered illegitimate and impious. Husayn marched toward Kufa (Iraq) with a small group of family and companions — approximately 72 fighters and their families — expecting support that did not arrive.

On the 10th of Muharram (Ashura), 680 CE, at Karbala in modern Iraq, Husayn's group was surrounded and massacred by an Umayyad army. Husayn was killed, decapitated, his body mutilated. The women and children were taken captive to Damascus.

Karbala is to Shi'a Islam what the Crucifixion is to Christianity: the paradigmatic act of redemptive suffering, the innocent righteous killed by corrupt power, the event that demands perpetual memory and moral response. It is the emotional and theological center of Shi'a religious life.

## The Imamate — the central doctrine

### What the Imam is
The *Imam* (with capital I — distinct from the generic Arabic word for prayer leader) in Shi'a theology is not merely a political leader. The Imam is:
- The divinely appointed, infallible (*ma'sum*) guide of the Muslim community
- The inheritor of esoteric religious knowledge from Muhammad through Ali
- The interpreter of the Quran's hidden meaning
- The cosmic axis connecting the human world to the divine — without a living or occluded Imam, the world cannot properly function
- The proof of God (*hujjat Allah*) on earth

This is a fundamentally different religious anthropology from Sunni Islam, which has no such office. The Imam is not a prophet — prophethood ended with Muhammad — but is the guardian and interpreter of prophetic truth.

### The line of Imams
Twelve Imams according to Twelver Shi'a (the largest branch):
1. Ali ibn Abi Talib (d. 661)
2. Hasan ibn Ali (d. 670)
3. Husayn ibn Ali (d. 680, Karbala)
4. Ali ibn Husayn Zayn al-Abidin (d. 713)
5. Muhammad al-Baqir (d. 733)
6. Ja'far al-Sadiq (d. 765) — the most important for Shi'a jurisprudence
7. Musa al-Kazim (d. 799)
8. Ali al-Ridha (d. 818)
9. Muhammad al-Jawad (d. 835)
10. Ali al-Hadi (d. 868)
11. Hasan al-Askari (d. 874)
12. Muhammad al-Mahdi (b. 869) — the Hidden Imam

### The Occultation
The 12th Imam, Muhammad al-Mahdi, went into *ghayba* (occultation — hiddenness from the world) in 874 CE. Shi'a theology holds that he did not die but was hidden by God. He will return at the end of times as the *Mahdi* (the guided one) to restore justice to a world filled with oppression.

Two phases:
- **Minor Occultation** (874–941): Four successive agents communicated between the Imam and the community
- **Major Occultation** (941–present): Direct communication ceased; the Imam is present but inaccessible until his return

This doctrine has enormous practical and theological implications: in the absence of the Imam, who has religious authority? The answer to this question shaped Shi'a political theology for over a thousand years — and is the foundation of Khomeini's doctrine of *Wilayat al-Faqih* (guardianship of the jurist), which gave clerics direct political authority in Iran.

## The Mahdi and eschatology
The Mahdi's return will be accompanied by:
- The appearance of Dajjal (the Deceiver, analogous to the Antichrist)
- The return of Jesus (*Isa*) — who will pray behind the Mahdi
- A final confrontation with evil
- The establishment of perfect justice

The Mahdi belief is present in Sunni Islam too, but is structurally more central in Shi'a theology because it is tied to the specific person of the 12th Imam rather than an eschatological expectation without a named figure.

Historically, various figures have claimed to be the Mahdi or his representative — with political consequences (the Fatimid caliphate, the Safavid dynasty, and more recently Shi'a revolutionary politics).

## Ashura — the central ritual

### What it is
Ashura (10th of Muharram, the first Islamic month) commemorates Husayn's martyrdom at Karbala. It is the most emotionally intense event in the Shi'a ritual calendar — not a celebration but a mourning:
- Passion plays (*ta'ziyeh* in Iran): Dramatic re-enactments of the events of Karbala
- Processions with black-clad mourners chanting and beating chests (*latm*)
- Self-flagellation (*tatbir* — cutting the head with swords or chains): Practiced by some communities; controversial within Shi'a jurisprudence; many senior clerics discourage it
- Vast gatherings at Husayn's shrine in Karbala (the largest annual human gathering in the world — over 20 million pilgrims some years)

The theology of Ashura: mourning for Husayn is not merely remembrance. It is participation in his suffering; it generates intercession; it is an act of solidarity with the oppressed righteous against corrupt power. Husayn's death is understood as redemptive — a sacrifice that keeps the Muslim community spiritually alive.

The political implications: Husayn as symbol of resistance to tyranny has been used in every major Shi'a political movement, most explicitly in the Iranian Revolution (Khomeini: "every day is Ashura, every land is Karbala").

## Shrine culture and intercession
Unlike normative Sunni theology, Shi'a Islam actively practices intercession (*tawassul*) through the Imams and their family:
- The Imams' tombs are major pilgrimage sites: Najaf (Ali), Karbala (Husayn), Mashhad (Ali al-Ridha in Iran), Samarra (10th and 11th Imams), and others
- Pilgrims pray *at* the shrines — addressing the Imam as intercessor, not just God
- The Imams' family (especially Fatima, Muhammad's daughter, and Zaynab, Husayn's sister) are also venerated

This is the primary point of Sunni-Shi'a theological conflict: mainstream Sunni theology considers intercession through the dead as shirk (associating others with God); Shi'a theology considers it the legitimate invocation of divinely appointed intermediaries.

## Major Shi'a branches

### Twelvers (Ithna'ashariyya) — the majority
~85% of all Shi'a. Believe in 12 Imams. Dominant in: Iran (state religion since the Safavid dynasty, 1501), Iraq (~65% of population), Lebanon (~30-40%, represented by Hezbollah and Amal), Bahrain (majority), Azerbaijan, and large communities in Pakistan, India, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia's Eastern Province.

### Ismailis (Seveners)
Diverged at the 7th Imam — recognizing Ismail ibn Ja'far rather than Musa al-Kazim. The Imam line continues to the present: **Aga Khan IV** (Shah Karim al-Husayni, b. 1936) is the 49th Imam of the Nizari Ismaili branch, considered by his followers as the living, present Imam. About 15 million followers globally (significant in South Asia, East Africa, Central Asia, and diaspora communities). The Fatimid Caliphate (909–1171, ruling North Africa and Egypt) was Ismaili.

Ismaili theology developed a sophisticated esoteric dimension — the *batin* (inner meaning) of scripture, accessible through the Imam's teaching. This fed significantly into Sufi and broader Islamic philosophy.

### Zaidis (Fivers)
Recognize Zayd ibn Ali (son of the 4th Imam) as the 5th Imam rather than Muhammad al-Baqir. The most "moderate" branch theologically — closer to Sunni in some respects. Historically important in Yemen, where Zaidi imamates ruled from the 9th century until 1962. The Houthi movement in Yemen comes from a Zaidi background.

## Shi'a jurisprudence and clerical authority

### Ja'far al-Sadiq and Shi'a law
The 6th Imam, Ja'far al-Sadiq (d. 765), is considered the founder of the Ja'fari school of jurisprudence — the Shi'a legal school. He is also important for the development of early Sufism (several Sufi chains trace through him) and for Islamic philosophy and science (Jabir ibn Hayyan, the alchemist, is traditionally his student).

### The marja' system
In the absence of the Hidden Imam, qualified jurists (*mujtahids*) interpret religious law. The most senior become *marjas* (sources of emulation) — laypeople and students *choose* a marja to follow on questions of religious practice and pay religious taxes (*khums*) to them. The most senior marjas hold the title of *Grand Ayatollah*.

Major marjas have historically been cautious about political involvement — the traditional quietist position, exemplified by Grand Ayatollah Ali Sistani (Najaf, Iraq), who explicitly holds that clerics should not govern.

### Wilayat al-Faqih
Khomeini's revolutionary doctrine: in the absence of the Hidden Imam, a qualified jurist should exercise *political* guardianship of the community. This was contested within Shi'a jurisprudence — many senior clerics opposed it. It is the constitutional foundation of the Islamic Republic of Iran.

## The Sunni-Shi'a divide today
The division is not merely theological — it has deep political and geopolitical dimensions:
- Iran (Shi'a state) vs. Saudi Arabia (Wahhabi Sunni) as regional rivals
- Iraq's sectarian civil war and ongoing tensions
- Lebanon's confessional political system
- Bahrain's Shi'a majority ruled by a Sunni monarchy
- Pakistan's recurring anti-Shi'a sectarian violence
- Houthi-Saudi Arabia proxy war in Yemen

## Research notes
- Vali Nasr's *The Shia Revival* (2006) is the best accessible account of contemporary Shi'a-Sunni dynamics
- Heinz Halm's *Shi'a Islam* is the best academic introduction to theology and history
- The Karbala paradigm as a political tool has been analyzed extensively — Hamid Dabashi's work is important
- Don't reduce Shi'a Islam to Iranian Islam — significant Shi'a communities predate Safavid conversion of Iran (1501) and exist independently of it
