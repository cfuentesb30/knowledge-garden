# Manichaeism

## Purpose
The most globally successful syncretic religion of late antiquity — a deliberate synthesis of Zoroastrian dualism, Gnostic Christianity, and Buddhism that spread from Persia to Rome, North Africa, Central Asia, and China. The single most important bridge tradition between Zoroastrian dualism, Gnosticism, and later esoteric traditions. For context, see [[Zoroastrianism]], [[../Europe/Gnosticism in Context|Gnosticism in Context]], and [[../Europe/Western Esotericism Overview|Western Esotericism Overview]].

## The founder — Mani (216–274 CE)

### Who he was
Mani (*Mānī*, Syriac: Mani Hayya — "Living Mani") was born in Babylonia (modern Iraq) into a Jewish-Christian baptismal sect (*Elchasaites*). From childhood he received visions from a divine twin (*al-Tawm* — the Twin, his spiritual counterpart). At age 12 and again at 24, his Twin commanded him to leave his sect and proclaim a new universal religion.

He traveled to India (encountering Buddhism), became court physician to the Sasanian king Shapur I, and built a religious community with explicit missionary infrastructure. He was executed under Shapur's successor Bahram I in 274, dying in chains — his followers called his death the "crucifixion."

### His explicit universalism
Mani's self-understanding was uniquely deliberate: he knew he was founding a universal religion and designed it accordingly:
- He claimed to complete and supersede Zarathustra, Buddha, and Jesus — three partial prophets, each reaching only their own people; he would reach all
- He personally wrote and illustrated his scriptures (rare in antiquity — most prophets wrote nothing)
- He trained missionaries for systematic global outreach
- He translated his texts into multiple languages from the beginning

This is not syncretism by accident; it is syncretism as conscious theological strategy.

## Core theology

### Cosmic dualism
Manichaean cosmology is radically dualist — two eternal, co-equal principles:
- **The Realm of Light** (*Eqlimā d-Nhurā*): God, the Father of Greatness, infinite light, absolute goodness
- **The Realm of Darkness** (*Eqlimā d-Hashukā*): Matter, the Prince of Darkness, eternal evil, absolute darkness

These two realms coexisted, separate, until the Darkness attacked the Light. In the resulting conflict, particles of divine light were captured and embedded in matter — the human soul is a fragment of trapped divine light. The entire cosmos is a machine designed to extract this light from matter and return it to the Realm of Light.

This is structurally identical to Gnostic cosmology (divine sparks trapped in matter, awaiting liberation) but with Zoroastrian dualism's eternal equal principles rather than the Gnostic emanationist descent from a single good source.

### Anthropology — the light soul trapped in matter
The human body contains a portion of divine light. Matter (including the human body itself) is the enemy — the prison of the light soul. Liberation requires:
- Knowledge of the soul's divine origin
- Practices that stop adding new material entanglement
- The soul's eventual return to the Realm of Light

The world's physical processes — digestion, breath, plant growth — are understood as light-extraction mechanisms: light particles move up through vegetation, through the food chain, through the bodies of Elect who release them through ascetic practice and prayer.

### The role of Jesus
Mani incorporated Jesus as the messenger of the Realm of Light who came to reveal the truth about the soul's situation. But Mani's Jesus is:
- Docetic — he did not truly have a material body; he only appeared to be human
- Not a redeemer through sacrifice — his death is not salvific; his teaching is what matters
- The "Jesus of Light" distinct from the "suffering Jesus" of orthodox Christianity

This Docetic Christology connects directly to Gnostic and Cathar theology.

### The Elect and the Hearers
Like Catharism (which may have inherited this structure), Manichaeism had two tiers:

**The Elect** (*electi*): Full initiates who lived under strict discipline:
- No meat, no wine, no sexual activity
- Did not cook their own food (to avoid killing)
- Constant prayer and fasting
- Their digestion was understood as literally releasing light particles
- The most important Manichaean religious function: their bodies were machines for liberating divine light

**The Hearers** (*auditores*): Ordinary adherents who could not live the Elect life, provided food and support for the Elect, believed in Mani's teaching, and would eventually be reincarnated as Elect themselves. Augustine of Hippo was a Hearer for nine years.

## Geographic spread — the most global pre-Islamic religion

### Western spread
- Mani himself traveled to the Roman Empire
- Manichaean communities spread across the Mediterranean world, North Africa, Spain
- Roman emperors repeatedly banned it (Diocletian 297 CE, Theodosius 381 CE) — the bans reflect how seriously it was taken
- Augustine encountered it in Carthage and was a committed Hearer before his conversion to Catholic Christianity

### Eastern spread
- Manichaean missionaries followed Silk Road trade routes eastward
- Major communities in Transoxiana (Central Asia), Sogdiana
- **China**: Manichaean missionaries reached China by the 7th century; the Tang court received them; the Uighur Khaganate (8th–9th century) adopted Manichaeism as a state religion — the only time a Central Asian state converted; Manichaean temples were built across Tang China
- Medieval Chinese Manichaeism survived underground (mixing with Buddhism and folk religion) until the 17th century in Fujian province — one of the longest survivals of any "extinct" religion

### Why it eventually collapsed in the West
- Roman imperial persecution from the 3rd century onward
- Competition with increasingly powerful orthodox Christianity
- The charge of "Persian religion" — foreign, suspect, threatening — made it politically vulnerable
- Augustine's later refutation of Manichaeism was enormously influential

## Augustine and Manichaeism — the significance
Augustine of Hippo (354–430) was a Manichaean Hearer from approximately 373–382 CE — nine years. His entire subsequent theological career can partly be understood as a working-out of his rejection of Manichaeism:

- **The problem of evil**: Manichaeism offered a clear answer (evil is eternal and co-equal with good; it's not God's fault). Augustine's rejection required him to develop an alternative — evil as *privatio boni* (privation of good, not a positive substance). This became the standard Catholic answer.
- **Free will**: The Manichaean determinism (matter = evil, always pulling us down) threatened free will and moral responsibility. Augustine had to develop a nuanced account of will, grace, and original sin.
- **The body**: Manichaeism's absolute rejection of the body as matter influenced Augustine's thought on sexuality, concupiscence, and original sin — even in his rejection, the shadow of Manichaean body-suspicion is visible

When medieval heresiologists wanted to attack dualism (Cathars, etc.), they had Augustine's anti-Manichaean writings as their primary arsenal.

## The texts
Manichaean texts were largely destroyed by Christian and later Muslim authorities. What survives:
- **Coptic texts** (found at Medinet Madi, Egypt, 1930): Psalms, homilies, kephalaia (teachings)
- **Central Asian fragments** (found at Turfan, early 20th century): Texts in Middle Persian, Parthian, Sogdian, Turkish, Chinese
- **Chinese texts**: Manichaean sutras preserved in Chinese
- **Arabic accounts**: al-Biruni, al-Nadim's *Fihrist* preserve descriptions and quotes

The discovery of the Turfan manuscripts (early 20th century German expeditions) revolutionized Manichaean studies. We now have far more primary material than scholars had a century ago.

## Connection to later traditions

### Dualist heresies
The Manichaean dualist framework fed into or paralleled:
- **Paulicians** (Armenia, 7th–9th century): Dualist Christian movement
- **Bogomils** (Bulgaria, 10th century): Dualist Christian movement
- **Cathars** (12th–13th century Western Europe): Whether direct descent or parallel development is debated, but the structural parallels are striking

### Western esotericism
The Manichaean tradition influenced:
- Medieval heresy in ways that shaped how orthodoxy defined itself
- 19th–20th century esotericists' interest in "dualist gnosis"
- René Guénon and Traditionalist accounts of perennial esoteric wisdom
- Jung's psychology (the shadow as the dark side of a personality; his *Answer to Job* engages Manichaean-adjacent dualist thinking)

## Research notes
- The collapse of Western Manichaeism was so complete that medieval historians sometimes applied the term "Manichaean" loosely to any dualist heresy — not always accurately
- The Central Asian and Chinese Manichaean survivals are better documented than most Western scholars know
- Samuel N.C. Lieu's *Manichaeism in the Later Roman Empire and Medieval China* is the best single scholarly work in English
- The Gnostic-Manichaean connection is real but should not collapse the two traditions — they are related but distinct systems
