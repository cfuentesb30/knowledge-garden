# Religion and Economics

## Purpose
Map the relationship between religious systems and economic life: temple economies as ancient state infrastructure, tithing and religious taxation, prohibitions on usury, the Weber thesis on Protestantism and capitalism, and the contemporary prosperity gospel.

---

## Why this matters

Religion is rarely just belief. It is a material practice — buildings, lands, labor, taxes, ritual obligations, dietary regulations, calendar restrictions, marriage transactions, inheritance rules. Every major religion has had to organize itself economically, and many have functioned as central economic institutions in their societies.

Three structural patterns recur:
1. **The temple as economic center** — religion centralizes and redistributes resources
2. **Religious regulation of economic conduct** — ethics, prohibitions, sacred contracts
3. **Religious legitimation of economic order** — sanctifying or contesting wealth, hierarchy, and material accumulation

This note surveys major examples across regions and traditions.

## Ancient temple economies

The temple in many ancient civilizations was not separate from the state — it was the state's primary economic institution.

### Mesopotamia (Sumerian, Akkadian, Babylonian)

The temples of Inanna at Uruk, Enki at Eridu, Marduk at Babylon, and others functioned as:
- **Landowners** at massive scale; temple estates were among the largest economic units
- **Employers** of thousands of laborers — weavers, farmers, brewers, scribes, ritual specialists
- **Bankers** — temples lent silver and grain at interest (the earliest documented interest-bearing loans are temple loans, c. 2000 BCE)
- **Tax collectors and redistributors** — tithes and offerings flowed in; rations and wages flowed out
- **Record-keepers** — cuneiform tablets that documented economic transactions developed in temple contexts

Hammurabi's code (c. 1750 BCE) regulates temple loan interest, debt slavery, and economic conduct within a frame where temple and royal economic activity are continuous.

### Ancient Egypt

The Pharaonic state and the temple system were inseparable. The Karnak temple complex at Thebes is estimated to have owned roughly **a third of all cultivable land in Egypt** at the height of New Kingdom temple wealth. Priests of Amun-Re were major political and economic figures, sometimes rivaling the pharaoh.

Temple offerings, redistribution to workers, and the integration of agriculture, craft, and ritual production made the temple a central economic node.

### Greek and Roman temples

Less concentrated than Egyptian/Mesopotamian temples but still economically significant:
- **Sanctuaries as banks**: deposits could be left at temples (Olympia, Delphi) where divine protection made theft sacrilegious; Greek city-states sometimes used temple treasuries as state reserves
- **Religious festivals as economic events**: the Olympic Games and similar Panhellenic gatherings were trade fairs and labor markets as well as cultic events
- **Temple offerings**: massive treasures accumulated at sanctuaries; these treasuries were targets in war (Phocis's seizure of Delphi's treasury triggered the Third Sacred War, 4th c. BCE)
- **The Roman state religion** financed public buildings, festivals, and dole through interweaving of priestly authority with state finance

### India (Hindu temples, especially South Indian)

The South Indian temple, especially under the Chola and Vijayanagara empires, was a major economic institution:
- **Landowner** on a vast scale; temples held thousands of villages
- **Irrigation infrastructure** built and maintained by temples
- **Banking and credit** — temples lent and borrowed; some functioned as financial intermediaries
- **Tirupati and other major temples** continue today as major economic entities; Tirupati is reportedly one of the wealthiest religious institutions in the world by annual offerings

## Tithing, taxation, and offerings

Religious giving structures vary widely but several patterns recur:

### Hebrew Bible / Judaism
- **Maaser** (tithe) — one tenth to the Levites; second tithe for festivals; third-year tithe for the poor
- **Terumah** — first fruits and other offerings for priests
- **Temple tax** — half-shekel annually for adult males
- The Temple in Jerusalem combined sacrificial, redistributive, and political functions until its destruction in 70 CE

### Christianity
- Early church voluntary giving (Acts 4); later institutional tithes
- **Medieval European tithes** — a tenth of agricultural produce to the Church became state-enforced taxation in much of Latin Christendom; abolition of tithes was a major demand in revolutionary moments (the French Revolution abolished tithes in 1789)
- Ongoing **Church Tax** (Kirchensteuer) in Germany, Austria, parts of Scandinavia — automatic deduction by the state for registered members of recognized churches

### Islam
- **Zakat** — one of the Five Pillars; obligatory annual giving (typically 2.5% of accumulated wealth) for the poor, the indebted, travelers, and other categories specified in the Quran
- **Khums** — additional 20% on certain types of income, especially important in Shi'a Islam
- Zakat is administered by the state in some Muslim-majority countries (Saudi Arabia, Pakistan); voluntary in others; functions as both religious obligation and welfare system

### Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism
- Dana (giving) is a religious virtue; merit accrues to the giver
- Buddhist sangha (monastic community) historically depended on lay almsgiving
- Jain temple support, especially among the Jain merchant community in Gujarat and Rajasthan, has been an important economic phenomenon
- No fixed percentage tithe; the religious framework is generosity (dana) more than quantified obligation

## Religion and the regulation of credit

### Usury prohibitions

Most major religious traditions have at some point prohibited or restricted interest on loans (usury):

- **Judaism**: Deuteronomy 23 prohibits charging interest to fellow Israelites (but permits it for foreigners); this distinction shaped medieval European Jewish moneylending — Christians prohibited from lending at interest, Jews not prohibited from lending to Christians, with the social consequences this implied
- **Christianity**: The Church Fathers and medieval canon law forbade usury (interest of any kind, not just excessive interest). The 1311 Council of Vienne declared usury heretical. This prohibition was massively eroded by the rise of commerce, with workarounds (loans structured as sales, partnerships, foreign exchange) and eventually theological reinterpretation
- **Islam**: **Riba** (any interest on loans) is explicitly prohibited in the Quran. Modern Islamic banking developed since the 1970s structures financial products to avoid riba while functioning economically (murabaha cost-plus sales, musharaka partnership, sukuk Islamic bonds). Whether these are genuinely riba-free is debated within Islamic finance
- **Hindu dharmasutras** — restricted moneylending for brahmins; permitted in regulated ways for vaishyas; usury rates legally capped

The functional issue across traditions: lending at interest tends to concentrate wealth and exploit the poor; religious systems have repeatedly tried to constrain this dynamic.

### Just price doctrine

Medieval Scholastic economics (Aquinas, the late Scholastics of Salamanca) developed "just price" theory:
- A price is just if it reflects the value contributed by sellers and the necessity to buyers
- Deceiving buyers about quality is sin
- Hoarding to drive up prices is sin
- Speculation on basic necessities is suspect

These ideas persisted through the early modern period and resurface in Catholic social teaching, distributism, and contemporary debates over predatory pricing.

## The Weber thesis

**Max Weber** — *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* (1905) — the most influential single argument in the sociology of religion and economics.

**The argument:**
- Calvinist predestination doctrine created psychological anxiety: how do I know I'm among the elect?
- The unintended solution: this-worldly success became a sign of election
- Combined with the ascetic Calvinist refusal to spend on luxury or self-indulgence, this produced systematic capital accumulation
- The combination of methodical hard work, frugal consumption, and reinvestment of profits constituted the "spirit of capitalism"
- Protestantism (especially Calvinism) thus helped catalyze the distinctive form of modern Western capitalism

**Status of the thesis:**
- The strict empirical claim (Calvinist regions developed capitalism faster) is heavily contested; Catholic Northern Italy and the Spanish Netherlands had sophisticated commerce before the Reformation
- The conceptual contribution (religious ethics shaping economic culture) is more durable and remains useful
- Weber didn't claim Calvinism *caused* capitalism; he argued it provided one important psychological/ethical contribution to its distinctive form

**Counter-arguments:**
- R.H. Tawney — *Religion and the Rise of Capitalism* (1926) — broader institutional argument
- Werner Sombart — argued Catholic, Jewish, and Italian factors mattered more
- Modern scholarship (Jared Rubin, others) often emphasizes institutional and legal factors over religious psychology

The Weber thesis is no longer treated as settled but remains the founding argument for a serious literature on religion's role in economic culture.

## Religion as economic critique

Major religious traditions have produced systematic critiques of wealth concentration and economic injustice:

### Hebrew prophets
- Amos, Micah, Isaiah on the rich oppressing the poor; the rejection of ritual without justice (Isaiah 1: "I hate, I despise your feasts" if accompanied by social oppression)
- **The Jubilee** (Leviticus 25) — every 50 years, debts cancelled, slaves freed, land returned to original owners. Whether actually practiced is doubtful, but the principle of periodic economic reset is theologically embedded

### Jesus and early Christianity
- "Sell what you have and give to the poor" (Mark 10); "The rich young ruler"; the camel and the eye of the needle
- The Acts 4 portrait of the early community holding everything in common
- This radical tradition is recurrently reactivated: Francis of Assisi, the radical Reformation, liberation theology

### Islamic critique
- Quran's repeated condemnation of hoarding wealth; the warning of hellfire for accumulators
- Sufi traditions of voluntary poverty (faqir, "poor one")
- Modern Islamist economic doctrines arguing against both capitalism and communism in favor of zakat-based redistributive justice

### Buddhism
- The renunciant ideal (the monk owns nothing)
- The doctrine that craving (tanha) for material things is the root of suffering
- Engaged Buddhism (Thich Nhat Hanh, Buddhist Peace Fellowship) applying Buddhist ethics to economic systems

### Liberation theology

Latin American Catholic theological movement from the 1960s onward (Gustavo Gutiérrez, Leonardo Boff, Jon Sobrino):
- **Preferential option for the poor** as a hermeneutical principle
- Marxist analysis of structural injustice combined with Catholic theology
- The Christian community as an agent of social transformation, not just personal salvation
- Persecuted by the Vatican under John Paul II and Cardinal Ratzinger (later Benedict XVI); partially rehabilitated under Francis
- Significant influence on Latin American Catholic culture, base communities, and political movements

## The prosperity gospel

The mirror image of religious critique of wealth: the prosperity gospel teaches that **material wealth is a sign of God's favor** and faithfulness produces financial success.

**Origins**:
- Emerged from 20th-century American Pentecostalism and New Thought (Norman Vincent Peale, "positive thinking")
- Major figures: Kenneth Hagin, Oral Roberts, Kenneth Copeland, Joel Osteen, Creflo Dollar, Joyce Meyer
- Theological basis (such as it is): "seed faith" — give money to ministries as a "seed" that will return multiplied; God wants His children to be wealthy

**Spread**:
- Megachurches in the United States built on this teaching
- Massive growth in **Latin American Pentecostalism**, especially in Brazil (Universal Church of the Kingdom of God / Igreja Universal do Reino de Deus, founded 1977, now global)
- Sub-Saharan African Pentecostalism — Nigerian and Ghanaian megachurches with global reach
- Rapid growth in South Korea, the Philippines

**Critique**:
- Theological: the rejection by mainline Christian denominations as a distortion of the gospel; Jesus's actual teaching points the opposite direction
- Material: the personal enrichment of pastors, often through aggressive tithing demands on poor congregants
- Sociological: the prosperity gospel functions as a religious legitimation of inequality and a coping mechanism in conditions of economic precarity

The prosperity gospel is now one of the fastest-growing religious orientations globally — and represents a major shift in how Christianity functions economically in the Global South.

## Religious labor and gender economics

A persistent pattern across traditions: women's religious labor (cooking for festivals, maintaining household shrines, caring for elderly relatives, preparing the dead, teaching children) is largely unpaid and unrecognized, while male religious labor (priesthood, theological scholarship, public liturgy) is institutionalized and compensated.

The economic significance of women's religious labor in Hindu domestic worship, Catholic parish life, Buddhist lay practice, and Islamic family ritual is enormous and largely invisible in standard accounts. Feminist religious studies have made this visibility one of their central projects.

## Key analytical points

1. **Religion has never been just ideology** — it is always also material infrastructure
2. **The temple/sacrament/church is a distributive institution** as much as a salvific one
3. **Religious ethics shape economic culture** even when they don't determine economic outcomes
4. **Critiques of wealth are recurrent and sometimes radical** within virtually every religious tradition
5. **Religious legitimation of wealth** is also recurrent — most clearly in the prosperity gospel
6. **The economic dimensions are often what religion most directly does for or against people**, even when believers and theologians focus on belief and ritual
