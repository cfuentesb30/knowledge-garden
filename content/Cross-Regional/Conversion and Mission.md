# Conversion and Mission

## Purpose
Compare how religions spread: which traditions actively missionize and which don't, the major missionary structures and methods across history, conversion mechanisms (from sword to syncretism), and the contemporary dynamics of global religious change.

---

## Why this matters

Not all religions are missionary religions. The difference between a religion that actively seeks converts and one that doesn't is structural — it shapes the religion's institutions, demographics, geography, and political relationships. Understanding mission is essential to understanding why some religions are global and others remain regional, why some absorb new populations and others maintain ethnic boundaries.

The major dichotomies:
- **Missionary vs. non-missionary** religions
- **Universal vs. ethnic** religious identity
- **Voluntary vs. coercive** conversion methods
- **Top-down (state) vs. bottom-up (popular)** transmission

---

## Missionary vs. non-missionary religions

### Strongly missionary traditions
- **Christianity** — Great Commission (Matthew 28); universal scope from Paul onward; the most aggressively missionary religion in history
- **Islam** — universal scope (the umma open to all converts); active da'wah (calling to Islam) tradition; rapid early expansion through both conquest and conversion
- **Buddhism** — universal salvation message; Ashoka's missionary edicts in the 3rd century BCE; spread across Asia through monks, traders, and royal patronage
- **Sikhism** — universal in scope, though less aggressively missionary than the above three

### Generally non-missionary traditions
- **Judaism** — accepts converts but does not actively seek them; identity strongly tied to ethnic/peoplehood category; halakhic restrictions on accepting converts make voluntary entry deliberately difficult
- **Hinduism** — most forms have no missionary structure; until the 19th century reform movements, you were born Hindu or not (with some exceptions); the category of "Hindu" itself is partly a colonial construction over multiple traditions
- **Zoroastrianism** — Parsi tradition generally does not accept converts; Iranian Zoroastrians more open; effectively a closed community
- **Shinto** — Japanese ethnic religion; no missionary structure; difficult or impossible to "convert" to in any meaningful sense
- **Most indigenous traditions** — tied to specific peoples, lands, and ritual relationships; not designed to be exported or universalized
- **Daoism** — has spread through cultural transmission but no organized missionary structure comparable to Buddhism or Christianity

### Selectively missionary
- **Bahá'í Faith** — strongly missionary in its universal aspiration
- **Hare Krishna (ISKCON)** — very actively missionary
- **Mormonism** — formal mission system; the two-year young adult mission is a defining institution
- **Jehovah's Witnesses** — door-to-door evangelism as core practice

## Mechanisms of conversion

Conversion happens through many different mechanisms. Most historical mass conversions involve more than one operating at once.

### Voluntary religious conversion
The classical paradigm in popular religious history. An individual encounters a new religion, experiences personal transformation, and changes affiliation. Examples:
- Paul on the road to Damascus (the foundational Christian conversion narrative)
- Buddhist converts in early Asian transmission contexts
- Sufi-led conversions in Central and South Asia (often through the personal magnetism of holy men)
- Contemporary religious switching in liberal-pluralist societies

Whatever individual conversions feel like to the convert, sociologically they tend to follow accessible social networks, plausibility structures, and crises (personal, political, civilizational) that destabilize prior commitments.

### Conversion by conquest

When one polity subdues another, religious change often follows — sometimes through compulsion, sometimes through opportunism, sometimes through generational drift.

**Important caveat**: The image of mass forced conversion at sword point is often historically wrong. Even in clearly violent contexts (Spanish reconquista, early Islamic conquests, Christianization of Saxons under Charlemagne), the dynamics were more complex:
- Conquering elites often allowed substantial religious continuity at the population level (the Pact of Umar with Christians and Jews under Islamic rule; Islamic millet system in Ottoman Empire)
- Conversion was often incentivized rather than forced — tax advantages, social mobility, alignment with the ruling class
- Conversion at the population level typically took generations and was uneven by region, urban/rural, and class

Cases where coercion played a major role:
- Charlemagne's wars against the Saxons (8th–9th c.); the Capitulatio de partibus Saxoniae prescribed death for refusing baptism
- The Spanish Reconquista's later phases — forced conversions of Jews (1492) and Muslims (1502, 1525) following expulsion threats
- Russian Orthodox conversion of Volga peoples in some periods
- Various Islamic dynasties at various times (the Almohads in 12th-c. Spain and North Africa enforced conversion or expulsion)

But also cases where coercion is wrongly attributed:
- Most early Islamic conquests did not require conversion of conquered Christians, Jews, or Zoroastrians; the conquered paid jizya and kept their religion. The conversion of Egypt and Syria from majority Christian to majority Muslim took centuries.
- "Christianization of Europe" was uneven and slow; many regions kept significant pre-Christian practice for centuries

### Conversion by state policy

States can convert populations through:
- Aligning state office with religion (you cannot hold office unless you convert)
- Tax differentials (jizya for non-Muslims; recusancy fines for English Catholics under Elizabeth I)
- Marriage restrictions (cannot marry into elite without converting)
- Education monopoly (state schools teach the dominant religion)
- Public religious requirements (oaths, rituals, public celebrations)

This kind of slow institutional pressure has produced more conversions historically than dramatic mass coercion.

### Conversion through trade and travel

Many religions spread through commercial networks:
- **Buddhism** along the Silk Road through merchant communities
- **Islam** spread across the Indian Ocean and into West Africa primarily through traders, not conquerors
- **Christianity** spread to Kerala, India through trade networks (the Thomas Christian tradition); Syriac Christianity reached China via Silk Road merchants
- **Judaism** in Yemen, Cochin (Kerala), and the Mediterranean diaspora spread through trade networks

Commercial conversion is typically gradual: the trading community is small, intermarriage and conversion happen at the boundary, prestige of the trading partners affects local elites.

### Conversion through marriage

Inter-religious marriage drives substantial demographic change:
- Most religions have rules about which partner converts when (often the woman converts to the husband's religion)
- Children of mixed marriages typically take the dominant or politically advantageous tradition
- Marriage alliances among ruling families have historically driven religious shifts (Frankish princess Bertha helping bring Christianity to Kent through Æthelberht; Roman aristocratic women converting husbands)

### Conversion through syncretism

Religions often spread by absorbing rather than replacing — the new religion incorporates local deities (as saints), rituals (calendar adaptation), and practices, allowing the population to convert nominally while retaining most of their practical religion. Over generations, the new framework dominates while preserving substrate elements.

This is the typical pattern in:
- Latin American Catholicism (saints and pre-Christian deities)
- African Christianity and Islam (ancestor practices, possession, ritual specialists)
- Buddhism in East Asia (absorbing Daoist and local deities)
- See Syncretism Overview for more

### Conversion through patronage

The conversion of a political/military leader often drives mass conversion among followers:
- **Clovis I** (c. 500 CE) — Frankish king's conversion to Catholic Christianity (rather than Arian) drove the Catholic identification of Western Europe
- **Æthelberht of Kent** (early 7th c.)
- **Vladimir of Kievan Rus'** (988 CE) — selected Byzantine Orthodoxy after considering Islam, Judaism, and Latin Christianity (according to the Primary Chronicle); his conversion brought Rus' into the Orthodox world
- **Olaf Tryggvason** and **Olaf Haraldsson** of Norway — forced or pressured Christianization of Norway and Iceland in the 990s–1030s
- **Mongol Mongke and Kublai** — various leaders patronized Buddhism (especially Tibetan Buddhism in the Yuan dynasty)

The model: ruler converts → court converts → administrative class follows → urban populations → rural populations (slowest, often incomplete). Christianity of the elite often preceded Christianity of the village by centuries.

## Comparative missionary methods

### Buddhist missionary tradition
- **Ashoka's edicts** (3rd c. BCE) — the first systematic state-sponsored missionary effort, sending monks to Sri Lanka, Hellenistic kingdoms, and elsewhere
- **Monk-merchant networks** along Silk Road, into Korea, Japan, Vietnam, Tibet, Southeast Asia
- **Translation as mission** — vast Buddhist translation projects (especially into Chinese, Tibetan)
- **Royal patronage** — the standard pattern: monks reach royal court, king converts, monasteries are funded, broader population follows over generations
- **Tantric mission** — Padmasambhava's mission to Tibet (8th c.); Vajrayana's spread to Mongolia, Bhutan, parts of Russia

### Christian missionary tradition
- **Apostolic mission** — Paul's strategic urban mission across the Roman world; Thomas tradition (India); Mark to Egypt
- **Monastic mission** — Irish monks (Columba, Aidan) Christianized northern Britain; Anglo-Saxon monks (Boniface) Christianized Germany; Benedictine mission to England under Augustine of Canterbury (597)
- **Crusader mission** — military and religious combined; mostly unsuccessful as evangelism though successful as conquest
- **Mendicant mission** — Franciscans and Dominicans from 13th century; mission to Mongol courts (William of Rubruck); systematic mission accompanying European colonialism
- **Jesuit mission** — sophisticated cultural adaptation; Matteo Ricci in China adapting to Confucian intellectual culture; Roberto de Nobili in India adopting Brahmin lifestyle; Francis Xavier in Japan; ultimately mostly suppressed by the Vatican (the Chinese Rites controversy ended in 1742)
- **Protestant mission** — explosive growth from the 18th–19th century: William Carey (India), Hudson Taylor (China), David Livingstone (Africa); often coupled with European colonial expansion
- **Modern Pentecostal/evangelical mission** — fastest-growing form of Christian mission; explosive growth in Latin America, Africa, Asia from mid-20th century onward
- **Mormon mission** — uniquely institutionalized: most young adult Mormons spend two years as full-time missionaries; the LDS Church is the most missionary religion per capita

### Islamic missionary tradition
- **Da'wah** — the invitation to Islam; structurally part of Muslim identity
- **Conquest period** (7th–8th c.) — created conditions for conversion but did not force it
- **Sufi mission** — the most important driver of long-term conversion in many regions; the personal example, ritual practice, and miracles of Sufi shaykhs converted populations more than theological argument; Sufi-led conversion is central to Islam's spread in South Asia, Southeast Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia
- **Trade-based mission** — Indian Ocean network spreading Islam through merchant communities to East Africa, Southeast Asia
- **Contemporary da'wah movements** — Tablighi Jamaat (founded 1926, India; now one of the largest grassroots religious movements globally), various other organizations

### Hindu reform missionary movements
Historically Hinduism didn't missionize. From the 19th century, reform movements that adopted missionary structures:
- **Arya Samaj** (founded 1875) — actively sought to "reconvert" Hindus who had become Muslim or Christian; first significant Hindu missionary organization
- **Ramakrishna Mission** (founded 1897) — global Hindu mission focused on Vedanta and service
- **ISKCON** (founded 1966) — explicitly missionary Vaishnava movement; covered in Bhakti Tradition note

## Resistance to conversion

Religions that maintain themselves against missionary pressure use various strategies:

- **Strong communal identity** that makes leaving costly (Judaism, Parsi Zoroastrianism, Druze, Alawites, Yazidis)
- **Esoteric/initiatory structure** that prevents the religion from being fully exported (Druze)
- **Synthesis** that absorbs the missionizing religion's prestige without conversion (Japanese mixed Shinto-Buddhist practice; Chinese "three teachings"; Hindu absorption of Christian and Islamic elements into a still-Hindu framework)
- **State protection** when the political conditions allow
- **Counter-mission** — actively contesting the missionary religion's claims; the Hindu Arya Samaj's "shuddhi" (reconversion) movement

## Contemporary dynamics

### Growth trends
- **Christianity** — total numbers stable globally but radical geographic shift: declining in Europe, growing rapidly in Africa (the world's Christian center shifted from Europe to Africa in the late 20th century); also growing in China
- **Islam** — fastest-growing religion globally; both through birth rate and conversion; Sub-Saharan Africa is the major growth zone
- **Hinduism** — relatively stable globally; concentrated in India but with significant diaspora
- **Buddhism** — modest growth globally; major adoption among Westerners particularly in secularized form (mindfulness)
- **No religion / unaffiliated** — rapid growth in the West; smaller but real growth in formerly highly religious societies; remains rare in the Global South

### Conversion in liberal pluralism

Modern liberal societies create a religious "market" where conversion happens at higher rates than traditional societies:
- Individuals encounter many religions through media, education, mobility
- Switching religions has low social cost
- Identity is more individually constructed
- Result: religious entrepreneurship and innovation, but also rapid loss of inherited religious identity to no religion at all

### Conversion-related conflict

Conversion remains politically charged in many places:
- **India** — anti-conversion laws in multiple states limiting Christian and Muslim evangelism among Hindus; politicized as "love jihad" or aggressive Christian mission
- **Middle East** — converting from Islam to another religion remains illegal or socially impossible in many Muslim-majority countries
- **Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand** — Buddhist majoritarianism increasingly suspicious of Christian and Muslim conversion activity
- **China** — state regulation of religion, particularly suspicion of foreign missionary networks; restrictions on Christianity, Falun Gong, Tibetan Buddhism, Uyghur Islam

---

## Analytical points

1. **Most "conversion" historically is slow generational drift**, not dramatic individual moments
2. **State policy and elite conversion drive mass change** more than missionary preaching alone
3. **Trade and marriage networks** are major hidden drivers of religious geography
4. **Missionary religions and non-missionary religions** exhibit very different demographic patterns over time
5. **Voluntary conversion in genuinely pluralist conditions** is a relatively modern and limited phenomenon — most of history's "conversions" happened under conditions of social/political pressure
6. **Syncretism vs. replacement**: religions often spread by being absorbed into existing frameworks first, then gradually displacing them — or never fully doing so
