# Cults and New Religious Movements

## Scope
This note separates the loaded word "cult" from the more useful academic category of new religious movements (NRMs), while still taking manipulation and coercive control seriously.

## Why it matters
The word **cult** is emotionally powerful but analytically sloppy.
People use it to mean:
- minority religion
- weird ritual group
- manipulative leader-centered group
- dangerous coercive movement
- anything they dislike

That is a mess.

## Better distinction
### New Religious Movements (NRMs)
A more neutral academic term for newer religious formations, alternative spiritual groups, and emerging traditions.
Not all NRMs are abusive.

### Cult in popular/critical use
Often refers to groups marked by:
- manipulative leadership
- heavy psychological control
- social isolation
- exploitation
- severe information control
- apocalyptic pressure or totalizing demands

## Why the distinction matters
Without this distinction, analysis collapses.
A strange religion is not automatically abusive.
An abusive group is not harmless just because it is technically a religion.

## Better evaluation questions
- how centralized is leadership?
- how much control exists over members' relationships, information, or movement?
- are exit costs high?
- is secrecy protective, initiatory, or coercive?
- is the group parasitic on fear, dependency, or exploitation?

## Historical examples and caution
Some groups start as reformist or minority spiritual movements and later harden into exploitative forms.
Others remain unusual but non-coercive.
Do not substitute aesthetics for analysis.

## Recommended resources
- Eileen Barker on NRMs
- Janja Lalich on bounded choice and cultic dynamics
- sociological studies of NRMs

## Good working summary
"Cult" is usually a warning word, not a precise category. For serious study, separate new religion from coercive control, then judge structure, power, and harm directly.
