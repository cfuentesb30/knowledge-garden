# Runes in Esotericism and Modern Revival

## Purpose
Traces the history of runes as esoteric and magical symbols — distinct from their history as a writing system. For the actual linguistic and epigraphic history, see [[Runes — History and Epigraphy]]. For the mythological context (Odin's runic ordeal), see [[Eddas and Norse Primary Sources]].

## Why this note matters
The modern occult understanding of runes as a divinatory and magical system is largely a 19th–20th century construction. Understanding where it came from is essential to using it critically — or to understanding what it actually is versus what it claims to be.

## The 19th century: Romanticism and nationalist revival

### The context
The 19th century saw a massive revival of interest in Norse/Germanic antiquity across Scandinavia and Germany, driven by:
- Romantic nationalism (each nation-state needed its own ancient, heroic past)
- Reaction against French cultural dominance
- The Brothers Grimm and the recovery of Germanic folk traditions
- Wagner's operas (*Ring Cycle*, 1848–1876) reshaping Norse mythology for a mass audience
- Scholarly publication of the Eddas and sagas for the first time in accessible editions

This created an enormous popular and intellectual appetite for "ancient Germanic wisdom" — and a corresponding market for people who claimed to have it.

### Johan Gottfried Herder and the Volksgeist framework
Herder's concept of *Volksgeist* (folk spirit) — that each people had a unique national spirit expressed in language, mythology, and folklore — provided the intellectual scaffolding for seeing runes as specifically *Germanic* spiritual technology. This was not a neutral scholarly idea; it had ethnic and eventually racial implications.

## Guido von List and the Armanen runes (1902–1908)

### The central figure in modern occult runology
Guido von List (1848–1919) was an Austrian occultist, mystic, and nationalist who is directly responsible for the form that most modern magical rune systems take. His work is the single most important rupture between historical runic use and modern esoteric runic practice.

### The Armanen system
In 1902, while recovering from cataract surgery during which he was temporarily blind, List claimed to have received a revelation of an 18-rune system — the *Armanen Futharkh* — which he said was the original, primordial rune row from which all others derived. The 18 runes corresponded to the 18 stanzas of Hávamál's Ljóðatal (the list of spells Odin claims in the poem).

This system has no historical or linguistic basis. It is a visionary construction.

List's broader claim: the Armanen were an ancient Germanic priest-king caste who encoded cosmic wisdom in the runes. The historical Eddas and sagas were corrupted survivals of this older tradition, deliberately obscured by Christianity.

### His broader ideology
List's work was explicitly volkisch-nationalist and proto-racist. He believed in:
- An Aryan master race with a primordial spiritual heritage
- Christianity as a Jewish-Roman conspiracy to destroy Germanic spiritual knowledge
- A coming restoration of Aryan-Germanic spiritual sovereignty

He founded the *List Society* (1908) which attracted figures across the German-Austrian nationalist and occult milieu.

## The Armanenschaft and Ariosophy (early 20th century)

### Lanz von Liebenfels
Jörg Lanz von Liebenfels (1874–1954) combined List's runic mysticism with extreme racial theory and Social Darwinism in a system called *Ariosophy* ("Aryan wisdom"). He published the journal *Ostara* (which the young Hitler reportedly read). He used runes as symbols of Aryan racial identity.

### The Thule Society
The Thule Society (Munich, 1918) was a volkisch/occult organization that used the *Thule* myth (legendary Nordic homeland) and employed runic imagery, including the *Hakenkreuz* (swastika — also technically a solar symbol in many world traditions but centrally deployed here in specifically Aryan-nationalist context).

### The SS and Nazi runic use
The National Socialist movement adopted runic symbolism extensively:
- **Sig rune (ᛋ)** doubled = SS insignia (Schutzstaffel). Designed by Walter Heck, using List's Armanen variant.
- **Totenkopf** combined with runes in SS insignia
- **Lebensrune / Todesrune** (life rune/death rune): Algiz/Elhaz (ᛉ) and its inversion used on SS gravestones for birth and death dates
- **Odal rune (ᛟ)** used by the SS Race and Settlement Office (symbolizing blood and soil/inherited land)
- Heinrich Himmler was deeply invested in runic mysticism and funded pseudo-archaeological expeditions through the *Ahnenerbe* (SS ancestral heritage bureau) seeking to "recover" ancient Aryan-Germanic spiritual knowledge

The Nazi use of runes is not a corruption of an otherwise pristine tradition — it is the direct application of the ideology that the List-Lanz tradition built.

## Post-WWII: The runic tradition resets

### The problem
After 1945, runic symbolism was so contaminated by Nazi association that Germanic/Norse traditions became extremely difficult to engage publicly in Germany and Austria. In the English-speaking world the contamination was less total but still significant.

### The new channel: Anglo-American neopaganism
The revival of interest in runes as a positive magical system in the English-speaking world came primarily through:

**Ralph Blum's *The Book of Runes* (1982)**: The single most commercially successful runic divination text. Blum drew on the I Ching model and Jungian depth psychology to create a 25-rune oracle set (he added a blank "Odin's rune" — historically meaningless). The system is almost entirely his own invention. It sold millions of copies and shaped popular runic understanding more than any historical or academic source. It has been strongly criticized by runic scholars for historical inaccuracy.

**The Futhark and Germanic magic revival (Edred Thorsson / Stephen Flowers)**:
Edred Thorsson (pen name of Stephen Flowers, b. 1953) is the most academically informed figure in the modern runic magical tradition. His *Futhark: A Handbook of Rune Magic* (1984) and subsequent works attempt to build a runic magical system on actual historical and linguistic scholarship, while explicitly acknowledging it as a modern construction rather than ancient survival. His *Rune-Gild* (founded 1980) is an initiatory organization focused on runic study and magical practice.

Thorsson/Flowers has a PhD in Germanic languages and his work engages seriously with the scholarship — while still building a contemporary magical system that goes beyond what the historical record contains. His books are more reliable on history than Blum but still produce a modern system, not a recovery of something ancient.

## Modern runic divination — what it actually is
Contemporary runic divination (casting or drawing runes for oracular guidance) is primarily:
- Built on the 24-rune Elder Futhark, not the historically more common Younger Futhark
- Using interpretive meanings derived from: the runic poems (Old English, Old Norwegian, Old Icelandic), Eddic poetic associations, and significant modern-era elaboration
- Influenced heavily by Jungian psychology (archetypes mapped onto rune meanings)
- Systematized primarily in the 20th century

The runic poems are historical:
- **Old English Rune Poem** (c. 8th–10th century): 29 stanzas, one per rune
- **Old Norwegian Rune Poem** (c. 13th century): 16 stanzas
- **Old Icelandic Rune Poem** (c. 15th century): 16 stanzas

These give poetic/gnomic descriptions of each rune character — useful as the closest historical analogues to "rune meanings," but they are poems, not divinatory manuals.

## The political dimension — a live issue
In contemporary Heathenry and runic practice, the Nazi contamination remains a live issue:
- The Odal rune (ᛟ), Tiwaz (ᛏ), and the Othalan variant are still used by white nationalist groups
- The Sonnenrad (Black Sun, a 12-armed swastika using sig-runes) appears in far-right spaces
- The *Valknut* has been appropriated as a white nationalist symbol though it also has legitimate Heathen use
- Folkish Heathenry's racial-identity framing draws from the same ideological well as the volkisch tradition

This is not peripheral. Anyone studying or practicing in this tradition needs to understand this history clearly.

## Summary map: historical vs. modern constructed
| Element | Historical basis | Modern construction |
|---|---|---|
| Runic alphabet | Yes (1st century CE+) | — |
| Runes as writing system | Yes | — |
| Protective/magical inscriptions | Limited evidence | Greatly elaborated |
| Odin's runic ordeal | Literary (Eddic) | Foundational for esoteric reading |
| 24-rune Elder Futhark as divination set | No historical divination use | Modern (20th century) |
| Individual rune "meanings" for divination | Runic poems (partial, oblique) | Mostly modern systematization |
| Armanen 18-rune system | None | List 1902–1908 |
| SS runic symbolism | None (borrowed and distorted) | List tradition + Nazi ideology |
| Ralph Blum oracle | None | 1982 |
