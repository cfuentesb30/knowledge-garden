# Mithraism

## Purpose
Map the Roman mystery cult of Mithras: what is actually known from archaeology, what the theological system may have been, its spread through the Roman military, and why the "Mithras copied Christianity" claims are historically wrong.

---

## The core corrective: what we don't know

Mithraism is one of the most over-claimed topics in popular religion discourse. The claims — that Christianity copied Mithras, that Mithras was born on December 25 from a virgin, that he died and was resurrected, that he had 12 disciples — are essentially all false or unsubstantiated. They originate mainly with 19th-century scholar Franz Cumont's speculative reconstructions and were popularized by 20th-century atheist polemics.

The actual problem with Mithraism is: **we have almost no texts**. The entire religion is reconstructed from iconography and archaeology. The central scene (the tauroctony — Mithras killing a bull) appears in virtually every Mithraeum, but what it *means* was never explained in a text that survived. Modern scholars disagree substantially on interpretation.

What we can say with confidence is more limited — and more interesting — than the popular mythology.

---

## Origins: Iranian or not?

The cult of Mithras (Latin spelling) shares the name with the Indo-Iranian deity Mithra — a deity associated with covenants, light, and the sun in both Vedic (*Mitra*) and Avestan/Zoroastrian religion.

**The question**: Is Roman Mithraism a transmission of Iranian Mithra worship to the Roman world, or a Roman invention using an Iranian divine name?

**Cumont's position** (19th century): direct transmission; Roman Mithraism is Western Zoroastrianism.

**Current scholarly consensus** (since Roger Beck, David Ulansey, Richard Gordon, and others): Roman Mithraism is substantially a Roman creation. It uses the Iranian name but:
- there is no documented Iranian mystery cult with the Roman features
- the Iranian Mithra doesn't kill a bull (the tauroctony has no Iranian parallel)
- the astral symbolism is more Greco-Roman than Iranian
- the cult seems to have emerged in Rome or the western Roman Empire, not imported from Iran

The Iranian name and imagery give the cult an "eastern" prestige and mystique — a common Roman religious strategy. The content is largely Roman.

## What we know about the cult

**The Mithraeum**: Mithraic worship took place in cave-like underground rooms (mithraea), many of which are preserved archaeologically. They are small — rarely holding more than 30–40 people — and always designed to resemble a cave. The ceiling was often painted with stars. Two benches ran along the sides; initiates reclined to feast.

**The tauroctony**: The central image in every Mithraeum: Mithras, wearing a Phrygian cap and cloak, kneeling on a bull's back, pulling its head back and plunging a knife into its neck. Around the scene: a dog and a snake leap toward the blood; a scorpion attacks the bull's genitals; a raven is perched nearby; the torchbearers Cautes and Cautopates flank the scene (one with torch raised, one lowered — light/dark, rising/setting). Sol (the Sun) is sometimes depicted nearby or as a witness.

**Ulansey's stellar interpretation**: The tauroctony represents a stellar map — Mithras as a force superior to the celestial sphere, whose bull-killing symbolizes precession of the equinoxes. The figures correspond to constellations (Taurus, Canis Major/Minor, Hydra, Corvus, Scorpio). Controversial but influential.

**Beck's reading**: The cave/Mithraeum is a cosmic image; the banquet replicates celestial positions; initiation involves symbolic journey through planetary spheres.

**The grades (7 levels)**: The primary evidence comes from Jerome and from mosaic evidence at Ostia. The seven grades have planetary associations:
1. Corax (Raven) — Mercury
2. Nymphus (Male Bride) — Venus
3. Miles (Soldier) — Mars
4. Leo (Lion) — Jupiter
5. Perses (Persian) — Moon
6. Heliodromus (Sun-runner) — Sun
7. Pater (Father) — Saturn

Initiates ascended through the grades in a ritual sequence. Higher grades had authority in ceremonies. The astronomical/planetary structure is clearly significant.

## Distribution and demographics

Mithraism spread primarily through the **Roman army** and through merchant and administrative classes:
- Mithraea are found along the Rhine and Danube frontier zones, in Rome, Ostia, and other major cities, in Britain, in the Near East
- The membership appears to have been overwhelmingly male — no evidence of female initiates
- Strong representation among soldiers, freedmen, merchants, and imperial bureaucrats
- **Not a religion of the poor or the masses** — the small meeting rooms, the grade system, and the demographics suggest a male elite fraternal/initiatory organization

Estimates of total adherents vary wildly; it was clearly one of several major mystery cults competing for the same demographic in 2nd–4th century Rome, alongside Isis worship, Cybele/Attis, and later Christianity.

## Theology: reconstructed uncertainties

What can be reconstructed from iconography, inscriptions, and non-Mithraic sources:

- **Mithras and Sol**: A key ceremony depicted in Mithraea is Mithras and the sun god (Sol/Helios) reclining together at a banquet, often on the bull's skin — a covenant of some kind between Mithras and the Sun
- **Mithras as mediator**: Mithras (from the Iranian root meaning "covenant/bond") may have functioned as a cosmic mediator or guarantor of order
- **Light/darkness dualism**: The cave setting, torch imagery, and association with the sun suggest a cosmic drama of light overcoming darkness — possibly influenced by Zoroastrian dualism but not identical to it
- **Astral salvation**: The planetary grade system suggests the soul's journey upward through the spheres toward liberation — a standard late antique soteriological scheme shared with Neoplatonism and some Gnostic traditions
- **The meal**: The communal reclining feast was important — an echo of the heavenly banquet of Mithras and Sol; meals were a central ritual (parallel to the Christian Eucharist, but coincidence of parallel structures, not borrowing)

## The Mithras/Christianity comparison problem

The most common popular claim: Christianity borrowed key elements from Mithraism. The specific claims don't survive scrutiny:

| Popular claim | Status |
|---|---|
| Born on December 25 | No ancient source says this. December 25 is associated with Sol Invictus (not identical to Mithras); early Christians chose December 25 for reasons of their own. |
| Born from a virgin | No. Mithras was born from a rock (petra genetrix). |
| Had 12 disciples | No ancient source. The image of Mithras surrounded by 12 figures refers to the zodiac, not disciples. |
| Died and was resurrected | No death-and-resurrection narrative exists in any Mithraic source. |
| The Eucharist | Mithraic communal meal was a real practice; Christian Eucharist is also a real practice; both may reflect general Greco-Roman sacred meal culture, but direct borrowing is not established. |

**What's true**: Both Mithraism and early Christianity operated in the same late antique religious market, drew on shared Greco-Roman religious vocabulary (initiation, sacred meal, grades, cosmic salvation), and competed for some overlapping demographics. Similarity in form doesn't establish borrowing — shared cultural environment does.

The "Mithras = Jesus" argument is bad history deployed as atheist polemic. The actual history — two mystery religions competing in the same 2nd–4th century Roman environment — is more interesting.

## Decline

Mithraism declined rapidly after Constantine's conversion and the Christianization of the empire:
- Imperial patronage shifted to Christianity
- Military Mithraism was associated with the pagan religious identity of the Roman army that Christian emperors were transforming
- Several Mithraea show evidence of deliberate destruction, likely by Christians
- The last datable Mithraic inscription is from 389 CE — shortly before Theodosius's anti-pagan legislation
