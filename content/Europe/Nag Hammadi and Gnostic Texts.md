# Nag Hammadi and Gnostic Texts

## Scope
This note focuses on the Nag Hammadi discovery and why it transformed the study of Gnostic and related late antique texts.

## Why it matters
For a long time, much of what scholars knew about so-called Gnosticism came from its enemies.
That meant the archive was distorted by polemic.
Nag Hammadi changed that.

## Discovery
- **1945** — codices discovered near Nag Hammadi in Egypt
- collection includes numerous Coptic texts
- not every text in the collection is "Gnostic" in a simple sense, which is exactly why the collection is so important

## Why the discovery matters
Nag Hammadi provided direct access to:
- revelatory discourses
- cosmological speculation
- alternative Christian and near-Christian writings
- texts linked with Sethian, Valentinian, Thomasine, and other currents or debates

## Important caution
Nag Hammadi is not a single religion in a box.
It is a library of varied texts.
Some are more clearly associated with Gnostic currents than others.
Do not treat the whole collection as one unified creed.

## Important texts often discussed
- **Gospel of Thomas**
- **Gospel of Philip**
- **Apocryphon of John**
- **Hypostasis of the Archons**
- **Tripartite Tractate**
- other revelatory and cosmological works

## What these texts reveal
They show a late antique world full of:
- competing interpretations of Jesus
- layered cosmology
- hidden teaching claims
- alternative creation narratives
- revelation as transformative knowledge
- disagreement over matter, salvation, and authority

## Why it matters for Christianity
Nag Hammadi demonstrates that early and late antique Christianity was far more diverse, unstable, and contested than tidy later orthodoxy suggests.
That does not mean "everything was equally Christian." It means the struggle over what counted as Christian was real and sharp.

## Recommended resources
- James M. Robinson — Nag Hammadi introductions/translations
- Bentley Layton
- Karen King
- Marvin Meyer
- primary text collections with scholarly introductions

## Good working summary
Nag Hammadi matters because it lets the late antique religious world speak in more of its own voice instead of only through the accusations of later orthodoxy.
