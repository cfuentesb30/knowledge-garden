# Christianity in Europe Overview

## Scope
This note maps how Christianity developed within Europe as a religious, political, institutional, and cultural force after its Middle Eastern origins. It is a navigation note — use the linked depth notes for serious study of any period or branch.

## Important framing
Christianity did not begin in Europe. It began as a Jewish-origin movement in the eastern Mediterranean. Europe became one of its major historical theaters through: Roman imperial adoption, the conversion of Germanic and Slavic peoples, Byzantine cultural dominance in the East, and the institutional Catholic church in the West.

## Historical arc

### Imperial Christianity (4th–5th century)
- **Constantine** (r. 306–337): Legalized Christianity via Edict of Milan (313); convened the Council of Nicaea (325) to settle the Arian controversy; made Christianity the favored religion of the empire
- **Council of Nicaea (325)**: Affirmed Christ as *homoousios* (same substance) with the Father — the defeat of Arianism (which held Christ as a subordinate creation)
- **Theodosius I** (380): Made Nicene Christianity the official state religion; banned other religions
- **The Church Fathers**: Ambrose, Jerome, Augustine of Hippo — theological architects of Western Christianity

### Post-Roman Christianization (5th–10th century)
- The fall of Rome (476) did not destroy the Church — the papacy, monasteries, and diocesan structure provided continuity
- **Monasticism**: Spread of Benedictine rule; preservation of learning through the Dark Ages
- **Conversion of the Germanic kingdoms**: Clovis of the Franks (496); Anglo-Saxon missions; Charlemagne's forced conversion of the Saxons
- **Irish and Scottish Christianity**: A distinctive monastic tradition (Iona, Lindisfarne) that preserved and transmitted learning

### High Medieval Christianity (11th–13th century)
Full treatment: [[Medieval Christianity]]
- Papal reform and claims to universal authority
- The Crusades (1096–1272)
- The universities and scholasticism (Aquinas, Abelard)
- The mendicant orders (Franciscans, Dominicans)
- Heresy and the Inquisition
- The Gothic cathedral as theological architecture

### The Great Schism (1054)
Full treatment: [[Eastern Orthodox Christianity]]
- Latin West vs. Greek East: filioque controversy, papal supremacy, liturgical differences
- Not a clean break — centuries of drift; the 1204 sack of Constantinople by Crusaders was the defining wound
- Two distinct civilizations of Christianity: Catholic West and Orthodox East

### Late Medieval and Reform (14th–15th century)
- The Black Death (1347–1353): Killed ~30–50% of Europe; destabilized church authority
- The Avignon Papacy (1309–1377) and the Great Schism (rival popes, 1378–1417)
- Wycliffe (England) and Hus (Bohemia): Proto-reformers; Hus burned at the Council of Constance (1415)
- Christian mysticism flowering: Eckhart, Tauler, Julian of Norwich, *The Cloud of Unknowing*

### The Protestant Reformation (16th century)
Full treatment: [[Protestant Reformation]]
- Luther (1517), Zwingli, Calvin, the English Reformation
- The five *solae* and their dismantling of medieval Catholic structure
- The Wars of Religion and the Peace of Westphalia (1648)
- The Catholic response: Council of Trent, the Jesuits

### Modernity and secularization (18th century–present)
- The Enlightenment's challenge to religious authority
- The French Revolution's violent anti-clericalism
- The rise of biblical criticism (historical-critical method)
- The *Kulturkampf* (Bismarck vs. the Catholic Church in Germany)
- Vatican I (1870): Papal infallibility defined
- Vatican II (1962–1965): Modernizing Catholic council — vernacular mass, ecumenism, engagement with the modern world
- Declining church attendance in Western Europe; growth in Eastern Europe and the Global South

## Three branches — structural comparison

### Roman Catholicism
- **Authority**: Pope + bishops in apostolic succession; teaching authority (*magisterium*)
- **Scripture and tradition**: Both sources of revelation
- **Sacraments**: Seven; valid only through ordained priests
- **Salvation**: Faith + works + sacraments; purgatory; saints as intercessors
- **Worship**: Eucharist-centered; Latin historically (vernacular since Vatican II)
- **Size**: ~1.3 billion globally

### Eastern Orthodox Christianity
- **Authority**: Conciliar (ecumenical councils); Ecumenical Patriarch has primacy of honor, not jurisdiction
- **Scripture and tradition**: Both; but Tradition is lived liturgy and practice as much as text
- **Salvation**: *Theosis* — genuine divinization, participation in God's energies
- **Worship**: The Divine Liturgy; icons; incense; standing
- **Size**: ~220–260 million globally; dominant in Russia, Greece, Eastern Europe

### Protestant Christianity
- **Authority**: Scripture alone (*sola scriptura*); varied interpretations across denominations
- **Salvation**: Faith alone (*sola fide*); grace alone
- **Sacraments**: Typically 2 (baptism and Eucharist); symbolic or real presence disputed
- **Worship**: Sermon-centered; simpler; vernacular
- **Size**: ~900 million globally across ~45,000 denominations
