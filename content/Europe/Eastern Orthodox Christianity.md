# Eastern Orthodox Christianity

## Purpose
Eastern Orthodox Christianity as a distinct theological and liturgical tradition — separate from both Roman Catholicism and Protestantism. Covers the Great Schism, theological distinctives (theosis, hesychasm, iconography), institutional structure, and major regional churches. For the Western Christian context, see [[Medieval Christianity]] and [[Christianity in Europe Overview]].

## What Eastern Orthodoxy is
Eastern Orthodox Christianity is the family of churches sharing apostolic succession, the seven ecumenical councils, and communion with the Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople. With approximately 220–260 million members globally, it is the second-largest Christian denomination. It represents the Christian tradition of the Byzantine Empire and its cultural heirs — Greece, Russia, Serbia, Bulgaria, Romania, Georgia, and others.

The self-description: *Orthodox* (right belief) and *Catholic* (universal) — terms the Orthodox churches claim as properly their own, not as the property of Rome.

## The Great Schism (1054)

### What happened
July 16, 1054: Cardinal Humbert, representing Pope Leo IX, placed a bull of excommunication on the altar of the Hagia Sophia in Constantinople against Patriarch Michael Cerularius. The Patriarch reciprocated. This formalized a split that had been developing for centuries.

### The underlying tensions
- **Filioque controversy**: The Latin West added "and the Son" (*filioque*) to the Nicene Creed — "the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father *and the Son*." The East insisted this was a unilateral doctrinal change requiring an ecumenical council and was also theologically wrong (it subordinated the Spirit)
- **Papal supremacy**: Rome claimed the pope had universal jurisdiction; Constantinople claimed primacy of honor but not universal jurisdiction
- **Liturgical and disciplinary differences**: Latin use vs. Greek rite; clerical celibacy (Roman) vs. married parish priests (Orthodox); the use of unleavened vs. leavened bread in the Eucharist
- **Cultural and linguistic drift**: The Roman Empire's Latin West and Greek East had been culturally diverging for centuries; theology was increasingly conducted in different languages with different philosophical frameworks

The 1054 date is a convenient marker; the real break was gradual and took centuries to solidify. The mutual excommunications were lifted in 1964 by Pope Paul VI and Patriarch Athenagoras — but full communion has not been restored.

### The Fourth Crusade's catastrophe (1204)
The definitive wound: Latin Crusaders sacked Constantinople — the greatest Christian city — for three days of murder, rape, and looting. The Orthodox still remember this. It made eventual reunion effectively impossible in popular memory.

## Theological distinctives

### Theosis (Deification)
The central soteriological category in Orthodoxy, largely absent from Western Christianity. *Theosis* (Greek: deification, divinization) — the process by which human persons are genuinely transformed into the likeness of God, participating in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4 is the key text).

The famous formulation of Athanasius: "God became man so that man might become God." This is not metaphorical in Orthodox theology — it describes an actual ontological transformation. The goal of the Christian life is not primarily forgiveness and heaven but genuine divinization, restored communion with God through participation in his energies.

This produces a different soteriology from the West's legal-satisfaction frameworks (Anselm) or the Reformation's forensic justification. The Orthodox would say: the West became obsessed with guilt; we are concerned with transformation.

### Essence and Energies — the Palamite distinction
The crucial theological framework, developed by Gregory Palamas (1296–1359) and confirmed at the councils of 1341 and 1351:
- God's **essence** (*ousia*) is absolutely unknowable and unapproachable — no creature can share in it
- God's **energies** (*energeiai*) are God himself in his self-communication — uncreated, real, distinguishable from the essence but not a separate being

Theosis is participation in God's energies, not his essence. This resolves the apparent contradiction: God is utterly transcendent AND genuinely communicates himself to creatures.

This distinction is philosophically significant: it was developed to defend the reality of mystical experience (the Hesychast monks claimed to see uncreated divine light) against the claim that all experience of God must be merely creaturely.

### Hesychasm — the prayer of the heart
*Hesychia* (stillness, quiet) is the contemplative tradition of the Orthodox Church:
- The **Jesus Prayer**: "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner" — repeated continuously until it becomes involuntary, synchronized with breathing, operating in the heart rather than merely in the mind
- **Nepsis** (watchfulness/sobriety): vigilant attention to inner states, guarding the heart against *logismoi* (troubling thoughts)
- The goal: *theoria* — direct vision of God; specifically, vision of the uncreated light that the Apostles saw at the Transfiguration

The most systematic account: the *Philokalia* (anthology of texts on hesychast prayer, compiled 1782 by Nikodimos of the Holy Mountain and Makarios of Corinth). Still the primary manual of Orthodox contemplative practice.

**Gregory Palamas** defended hesychasm against the charge that the monks were claiming to see the divine essence (which would be impossible). His essence/energies distinction was the theological defense.

### Iconography — the theology of the image
The veneration of icons (*eikones* — sacred images) is central to Orthodox liturgical life. This is not "worshipping pictures" — the Orthodox carefully distinguish *latria* (worship, due to God alone) from *proskyneisis* / *veneration* (honorable salutation due to the person the image represents).

The Iconoclast Controversy (726–842) — two waves of imperial attempts to destroy sacred images — produced the definitive Orthodox theology of the icon:
- The icon is valid because the Incarnation validated matter as capable of bearing the divine (this is the anti-Gnostic point)
- The image depicts the *person* (hypostasis), not the nature; therefore depicting Christ is valid because he is a specific person with a human nature, not an abstract divinity
- Venerating the image reaches the prototype (the person depicted)

The restoration of icons in 843 (the "Triumph of Orthodoxy") is the most important liturgical feast of the Byzantine calendar after Easter.

Icons are not devotional art in the Western sense — they are *written* (iconographers speak of writing icons, not painting them), produced within a theological and ascetic discipline, and understood as windows into the divine presence.

### Liturgy — the Divine Liturgy
The Orthodox Divine Liturgy (primarily the Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom) is the central act of worship — a 90-minute (minimum) sung liturgy in which heaven and earth are understood to meet. The liturgy:
- Takes place simultaneously in time and in the eternal divine reality
- Is not a re-enactment but a participation in the one eternal liturgy of the heavenly throne room
- Involves all the senses: incense, light, chanting, the iconostasis (icon screen), prostrations
- The congregation stands (no pews in traditional Orthodox churches — though this has varied)

## The conciliar structure
Orthodoxy is organized as a communion of autocephalous (self-governing) churches, each headed by a patriarch or metropolitan, all in communion with each other and with the Ecumenical Patriarch of Constantinople (who holds primacy of honor but not universal jurisdiction).

Major Orthodox churches:
- **Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople**: Symbolic primacy; jurisdiction over diaspora traditionally; currently reduced to Istanbul (a few thousand faithful) but globally symbolic
- **Russian Orthodox Church**: By far the largest Orthodox church — 100+ million; politically entangled with the Russian state historically and currently
- **Greek Orthodox Church**
- **Romanian, Serbian, Bulgarian, Georgian Orthodox Churches**
- **Coptic Orthodox Church** (Egypt): Technically Oriental Orthodox, not Eastern Orthodox — it did not accept the Council of Chalcedon (451) and has a distinct Christology
- **Ethiopian Orthodox Church**: Also Oriental Orthodox; ancient tradition

## Monasticism — Mount Athos
Mount Athos (the Holy Mountain, Greece) is a peninsula hosting 20 Orthodox monasteries and approximately 2,000 monks. It is an autonomous monastic republic under Greek sovereignty and the Ecumenical Patriarch's spiritual jurisdiction, closed to women since the 10th century. It has been the center of Orthodox hesychast practice for a thousand years. The Philokalia was compiled from its libraries.

## Orthodox Christianity in Russia
The Russian Orthodox Church received autocephaly (1448) and declared Moscow "the Third Rome" after Constantinople's fall to the Ottomans (1453). It became the established church of the Russian Empire. The relationship between church and state has been a defining issue: from Byzantine *symphonia* (harmony of church and state) to Peter the Great's subjugation of the church to the state to Soviet persecution (martyrs estimated in the tens of thousands) to the contemporary Putin-era alliance.

## Research notes
- The theological-philosophical depth of Orthodox theology is significantly underrepresented in English-language religious studies — most English scholarship focuses on Western Christianity
- Kallistos Ware (Bishop Kallistos Ware of Diokleia) is the most accessible English-language Orthodox theologian and teacher
- The distinction between Oriental Orthodoxy (Coptic, Ethiopian, Armenian, Syriac — non-Chalcedonian) and Eastern Orthodoxy (Chalcedonian) is frequently confused
- Don't read Orthodox theology through Protestant or Catholic frameworks — the questions it's asking are different
