# Cult Dynamics: Charisma, Control, and Exit Costs

## Scope
This note analyzes coercive group dynamics more precisely than the generic word "cult."
It focuses on leadership charisma, information control, dependency, isolation, punishment, and the cost of leaving.

## Why it matters
A lot of weak analysis stops at vibes.
Strange symbols or unusual beliefs do not prove abuse.
The real question is structural control.

## Core variables
### Charismatic leadership
A leader may be treated as uniquely enlightened, chosen, infallible, or spiritually necessary.
This can create obedience far beyond ordinary religious respect.

### Information control
Groups may control:
- texts and teachings by level
- outside criticism
- who members can trust
- the narrative of reality itself

### Social isolation
Isolation can be physical, emotional, or informational.
The goal is often to make the group the main world.

### Exit costs
This is one of the biggest indicators.
Leaving may trigger:
- loss of family/community
- fear of spiritual doom
- reputational destruction
- financial loss
- retaliation or harassment
- collapse of identity

### Behavioral regulation
The group may increasingly regulate:
- sex and relationships
- money
- labor
- diet
- reading
- sleep
- movement
- confession and secrecy

## Why secrecy matters differently here
Secrecy in mystery traditions or initiatory schools is not automatically abusive.
The key question is whether secrecy serves sacred pedagogy or coercive domination.

## Better diagnostic questions
- can members disagree safely?
- can members leave without punishment or social annihilation?
- is leadership accountable?
- does the group monopolize truth?
- are members pushed into dependency?

## Recommended resources
- Janja Lalich
- Robert J. Lifton
- Eileen Barker
- sociological and psychological work on coercive control

## Good working summary
The real danger in cultic systems is not weird belief. It is concentrated power, dependency, blocked exit, and reality control.
