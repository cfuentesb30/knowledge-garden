# Catharism and the Albigensian Crusade

## Purpose
The most significant medieval Christian heresy — a dualist religious movement that rivaled Catholicism in parts of southern France and northern Italy for over a century before being destroyed by crusade and inquisition. Directly connects the medieval Christianity and Gnosticism shelves. For context, see [[Medieval Christianity]] and [[Gnosticism in Context]].

## What Catharism was
Catharism (from Greek *katharoi*, "pure ones") was a dualist Christian religious movement active primarily c. 1150–1330 CE in Languedoc (southern France) and northern Italy. At its peak in the 12th–13th centuries it had organized church structures, a theology, a clergy, and the support of significant sections of the southern French nobility.

The Cathars believed:
- The material world was created by an evil or inferior deity (not the true God)
- The true God was wholly spiritual and had nothing to do with matter, flesh, or creation
- Human souls were divine sparks imprisoned in a cycle of reincarnation in evil material bodies
- Christ did not truly take on flesh (Docetism) — his body was an illusion or a spiritual body
- The Catholic Church, with its wealth, sacraments, and material focus, was the church of the evil creator, not of the true God
- Liberation required the *consolamentum* — the single Cathar sacrament

This is recognizably Gnostic — the parallel to Gnostic demiurge theology (see [[Demiurge and Aeons]]) is direct, though the historical transmission pathway is debated.

## Origins and theological background

### The dualist question
Catharism was one of two types of dualism:
- **Mitigated dualism** (most Cathars, "Albanensian" and French): One God created both the spiritual and material realms; a rebellious subordinate power (the devil/evil god) is responsible for the material world's corruption. The material world *was* good but has been corrupted.
- **Absolute dualism** (some Cathars, "Concorezzensians"): Two co-eternal principles — a good God of spirit and an evil god of matter. These are genuinely equal. The material world was *never* good.

### Connection to Bogomilism
The immediate ancestor of Catharism was Bogomilism — a Bulgarian dualist movement originating c. 10th century, itself connected to earlier Paulician movements in Armenia and Anatolia. Bogomil missionaries brought their theology westward; direct contact between Bogomil bishops and emerging Cathar leadership is documented.

The chain: Gnostic currents of late antiquity → Paulicians (Armenia) → Bogomils (Bulgaria) → Cathars (Western Europe)

## The Cathar church

### The Perfect and the Believers
Catharism had a two-tier structure:

**The Perfect** (*perfecti*, also called *bons hommes* / good men): The full initiates, who had received the *consolamentum* and lived under strict discipline:
- Strict vegetarianism (no meat, no eggs, no milk — only fish, the "cold" food)
- Celibacy
- No lying under any circumstances
- Communal life
- Itinerant preaching
- No physical violence

The Perfect were relatively few — perhaps a few thousand at the movement's height. They were revered by the believers as having achieved genuine spiritual transformation.

**The Believers** (*credentes*): The ordinary adherents who could not yet live the Perfect life but who received the *consolamentum* at death — the "consoled" death, ensuring liberation. Between the *consolamentum* and death, they would fast (*endura*) to prevent accumulating new karma through eating. This deathbed conversion model meant many Believers lived relatively normal lives, believing they would be saved at the end.

### The consolamentum
The single Cathar sacrament — a laying-on of hands by a Perfect, transmitting the Holy Spirit and admitting the recipient to the ranks of the Perfect (or, in the deathbed form, ensuring liberation). It replaced the entire Catholic sacramental system.

The logic: material things cannot mediate spiritual reality; the consolamentum was purely spiritual, transmitted through touch and prayer. Water baptism (Catholic) was meaningless because water is material.

### Cathar theology of Christ
Christ in Cathar theology was wholly spiritual — either a pure celestial being who only *appeared* to have a human body (Docetism) or the first created angelic being. He could not have truly suffered, died, or been resurrected in a material body. The cross was not a redemptive event but a symbol of the material world's evil power over a being who transcended it.

The implication: the Catholic Mass (re-presenting Christ's sacrifice) was meaningless or worse — a deception by the evil church.

## Catharism in Languedoc
Languedoc (the region of southern France where *oc* — yes — was spoken, as opposed to the northern *oïl*) became the heartland of Catharism for reasons only partly theological:
- Troubadour culture and courtly love poetry flourished here — a culture more permissive of heterodox ideas
- The southern nobility (including the Counts of Toulouse, the most powerful southern lords) protected or tolerated Cathars, partly from genuine sympathy, partly to resist northern French and papal interference in their domains
- The Cathar Perfect were admired for their genuine asceticism and poverty, contrasting sharply with a visibly corrupt Catholic clergy
- Women had more prominent roles in Catharism — becoming Perfect themselves, running Cathar houses — than in Catholic structures

The city of Albi became so associated with Catharism that they were also called *Albigensians*.

## The Albigensian Crusade (1209–1229)
Pope Innocent III had tried peaceful missionary conversion (including sending Francis of Assisi's contemporary Dominic). When Peter of Castelnau, a papal legate, was murdered in 1208 (probably by a servant of Count Raymond VI of Toulouse), Innocent called a crusade against the Cathars.

### The crusade
The northern French nobility — led by Simon de Montfort — responded eagerly: southern lands and wealth were the prize. The crusade produced some of the worst atrocities of the medieval period.

**The Massacre of Béziers (July 22, 1209)**: Crusaders sacked the city. Catholics and Cathars mingled in the population; the crusade leaders allegedly asked the papal legate Arnaud Amalric how to tell Catholics from heretics. The attributed response: *"Caedite eos. Novit enim Dominus qui sunt eius"* — "Kill them all. God will know his own." Whether or not he said this, the massacre followed: between 7,000 and 20,000 people killed. The *Chanson de la Croisade Albigeoise* describes the horror.

**Carcassonne, Minerve, Lavour, Termes**: Systematic reduction of Cathar strongholds; mass burnings of Perfect who refused to recant.

**Montségur (1244)**: The last major Cathar castle. After a long siege and negotiated surrender, ~225 Perfect were burned together in a mass pyre at the base of the fortress. Montségur became the defining symbol of Cathar martyrdom.

### The aftermath
The Treaty of Paris (1229) ended the formal crusade but the region was politically annexed to the French crown. The Inquisition (formally established 1231) systematically hunted remaining Cathars. The *domus* (Cathar houses) were destroyed; the Perfect were driven underground. By 1330 organized Catharism had effectively ceased to exist.

The last known Cathar Perfect, Guillaume Bélibaste, was burned in 1321.

## Historical significance

### For understanding heresy and persecution
Catharism is the clearest case of:
- Genuine theological diversity within medieval Christianity (not merely popular ignorance but organized alternative belief)
- A crusade directed against European Christians rather than Muslims
- The Inquisition's original institutional purpose and methods
- The use of religious persecution as political conquest (the southern French nobility was stripped of power)

See [[Medieval Christianity]] on the Inquisition.

### For understanding Gnosticism's survival
The question of whether there is a continuous underground Gnostic tradition from late antiquity through Catharism to modern esotericism is debated. The connection Bogomil → Cathar is documented. The further connection Cathar → Rosicrucian → modern Gnosticism is largely a 19th-century romantic construction with minimal historical basis — but the Cathar episode demonstrates that Gnostic-type ideas did resurface independently in Christian Europe.

### Montségur mythology
In the 19th–20th century, Montségur and the Cathars were romanticized heavily — by Romanticism, by Theosophy, by Nazi occultists (Otto Rahn's *Crusade Against the Grail*, 1933, pursued the idea that the Holy Grail was a Cathar relic and sent SS expeditions to Montségur), and by popular Dan Brown-style fiction. Most of this is romantic projection onto a genuine historical tragedy. The Cathars had no Grail; the Nazi interest was pure mystical-nationalist fantasy.

## Research notes
- Malcolm Lambert's *The Cathars* is the standard academic history
- The primary sources (Inquisition registers, especially those of Jacques Fournier at Montaillou) are extraordinary records of medieval popular belief
- Emmanuel Le Roy Ladurie's *Montaillou* reconstructs a 14th-century Cathar village from the Fournier register — one of the most remarkable microhistories ever written
- The "Cathar treasure" (gold, Grail, etc.) is fiction; there is no historical evidence for it
