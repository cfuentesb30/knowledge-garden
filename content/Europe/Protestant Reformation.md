# Protestant Reformation

## Purpose
One of the most consequential events in European and world history — the 16th-century fracture of Western Christianity into competing confessional blocs. Covers theological causes, major reformers, the spread and variety of Protestant movements, the Catholic response, and long-term consequences. Connects to [[Medieval Christianity]], [[Christianity in Europe Overview]], and [[Early Modern Witchcraft and the Witch Trials]].

## Why it matters beyond religion
The Reformation reshaped:
- European politics (Wars of Religion, the Peace of Westphalia, the concept of religious tolerance)
- Literacy and print culture (vernacular Bible, mass printing)
- Law and education (Protestant states built new school systems)
- Economic culture (Weber's "Protestant ethic" thesis — contested but formative)
- The concept of individual conscience as a category with public weight
- The shape of modern secularism (religious plurality → no single authority → state arbitration)

## The immediate trigger — Luther and indulgences

### Martin Luther (1483–1546)
An Augustinian friar and professor of theology at Wittenberg. His personal spiritual crisis — he was terrified of God's judgment and found no peace through the traditional penitential system — led him to a theological breakthrough: *sola fide* (faith alone). The sinner is justified before God not by accumulated merit or ritual performance but by trust in God's grace in Christ, received by faith.

**October 31, 1517**: Luther allegedly posts 95 *Theses* on the Wittenberg church door (the dramatic hammering may be legendary; he certainly circulated them). The theses are not a rejection of the church — they are an academic challenge to the theology of indulgences. But the printing press turned a local academic dispute into a European crisis within weeks.

**1520–1521**: The escalation. Leo X excommunicated Luther; the Emperor Charles V summoned him to the Diet of Worms:
> "Unless I am convinced by the testimony of the Holy Scriptures or by clear reason (for I do not trust either in the pope or in councils alone, since it is well known that they have often erred and contradicted themselves), I am bound by the Scriptures I have cited and my conscience is captive to the Word of God."

This moment — individual conscience against institutional authority — becomes a founding myth of Western individualism regardless of Luther's own views (which were quite anti-individualist in other respects).

### The five *solae* — the theological core
The Reformation's theological program can be compressed into five Latin phrases:
1. **Sola fide** (faith alone): Justification by faith, not works or merit
2. **Sola scriptura** (scripture alone): The Bible as the sole ultimate authority — not tradition, councils, or popes
3. **Sola gratia** (grace alone): Salvation is God's initiative entirely, not earned
4. **Solus Christus** (Christ alone): Christ is the sole mediator — no saints, no priests required
5. **Soli Deo gloria** (glory to God alone): All glory belongs to God, not to human institutions

These five theses dismantled the structure of medieval Catholic Christianity: the sacramental system (works), the papacy and councils (tradition and authority), the saints (intermediaries), and the priest as necessary mediator.

## The Swiss Reformation

### Huldrych Zwingli (1484–1531)
Reformed the church in Zurich simultaneously with Luther in Wittenberg, independently. More humanist in orientation (Erasmus was his model). His key divergence from Luther: the Eucharist. Zwingli held that "This is my body" means "This signifies my body" — the Eucharist is a memorial, not a real presence. Luther emphatically disagreed. The Marburg Colloquy (1529) failed to unite the two reformations on this point — a fracture that persists to this day.

### John Calvin (1509–1564)
The most systematic Reformation theologian. French, settled in Geneva, which he shaped into a Reformed city-state. His *Institutes of the Christian Religion* (1536, expanded 1559) is the Reformation's most comprehensive theological work.

Key Calvinist distinctives:
- **Absolute divine sovereignty**: God controls all things absolutely
- **Predestination** (double): God has decreed from eternity who will be saved and who will be damned — not based on foreseen faith or merit but by pure divine choice (*election*). This is the most controversial Calvinist doctrine; Calvin considered it essential to preserving the purity of grace
- **Discipline**: The consistory (church discipline board) enforced moral conduct in Geneva — producing what some called a "holy commonwealth" and others found oppressive
- **Plain worship**: Stripped of images, liturgical elaboration, and saints; the sermon is the center

Calvinism spread widely: Reformed churches in the Netherlands, Scotland (Presbyterianism), France (Huguenots), Hungary, and Calvinist influence shaped Puritanism in England and New England.

## The English Reformation
The English case is distinctive: driven initially by dynastic rather than theological concerns (Henry VIII needed a divorce), but developing its own theological logic.

**Henry VIII**: Broke with Rome 1534 via the Act of Supremacy — making himself Supreme Head of the Church of England. Dissolved the monasteries (1536–1541), seizing enormous wealth. The English church remained largely Catholic in doctrine initially.

**Edward VI** (r. 1547–1553): Protestant direction accelerated — Thomas Cranmer wrote the *Book of Common Prayer* (1549, 1552), the liturgical foundation of Anglicanism.

**Mary I** (r. 1553–1558): Restored Catholicism; burned ~280 Protestants (earning "Bloody Mary" — though her total was lower than that of many Protestant rulers)

**Elizabeth I** (r. 1558–1603): The Elizabethan Settlement — a deliberately ambiguous Anglican compromise. The 39 Articles provided a broadly Protestant theological framework; the *Book of Common Prayer* preserved Catholic liturgical form. This theological fuzziness was the point — a national church that most English people could inhabit.

## The Radical Reformation
Beyond Luther and Calvin, a more radical stream rejected both Catholic and magisterial Protestant churches:

**Anabaptists**: Rejected infant baptism (hence *ana*-baptism — rebaptizing adults on the basis of personal faith). Advocated voluntary church membership, separation of church and state, pacifism. Severely persecuted by both Catholics and Protestants. Their descendants: Mennonites (after Menno Simons), Amish, Hutterites, and they influenced later Baptists.

**Spiritualists** (e.g., Caspar Schwenckfeld): Inner spiritual experience supersedes external Word and sacrament.

**Antitrinitarians**: Groups that rejected the Trinity doctrine — ultimately producing Unitarianism.

The Radical Reformation was the most consistently persecuted wing, by all sides.

## The Catholic Reformation (Counter-Reformation)

### The Council of Trent (1545–1563)
The Catholic response — both a reform of abuses and a doctrinal clarification against Protestant positions. Key decisions:
- Reaffirmed: tradition alongside scripture as sources of authority (directly against *sola scriptura*)
- Reaffirmed: justification involves both faith and works (against *sola fide*)
- Defined: transubstantiation formally
- Reformed: seminary education for priests, tightened discipline
- Affirmed: the seven sacraments, purgatory, veneration of saints, Latin Vulgate

### The Jesuits (Society of Jesus, founded by Ignatius of Loyola, 1540)
The spearhead of Catholic renewal — a mobile, educated, obedient order focused on: preaching, education (Jesuit universities and colleges), missions (Matteo Ricci in China, Francis Xavier in Asia, Paraguay reductions), and intellectual engagement with Protestantism. Their *Spiritual Exercises* (Ignatius' method of directed meditation) remains one of the most systematic contemplative methods in Christianity.

## The Wars of Religion
The Reformation produced decades of religiously framed civil and international war:
- **German peasants' War** (1524–1525): Luther's theology was read as social liberation; Luther himself condemned the peasants' revolt
- **Schmalkaldic Wars** (1546–1555): Protestant princes vs. Emperor; resolved at Peace of Augsburg (*cuius regio, eius religio* — whose realm, his religion; each prince determined the religion of his territory)
- **French Wars of Religion** (1562–1598): Catholics vs. Huguenots; St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (1572): ~5,000–30,000 Protestants killed in days
- **Thirty Years' War** (1618–1648): The most destructive European war before the 20th century; began as religious conflict, ended as power politics; the Peace of Westphalia (1648) established the modern state system and religious toleration as a political principle

## Long-term consequences
- **Denominational Christianity**: Western Christianity never re-unified. Catholicism, Lutheranism, Calvinism, Anglicanism, Anabaptism spawned hundreds of further divisions. Today ~45,000 Christian denominations globally.
- **Vernacular scripture and literacy**: Luther's German Bible became a standard for the language; Bible translation drove literacy
- **Print culture**: The Reformation was the first mass media event — made possible by Gutenberg's press (1440s), amplified by it
- **Secularization**: Religious pluralism eventually required states to manage competing claims through non-religious frameworks — a major driver of Western secularism
- **Conscience and individual authority**: The precedent of Luther at Worms lodged individual conscience as a category with political and legal weight

## Research notes
- The "Weber thesis" (*The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, 1905) — that Calvinist predestination theology produced the psychological conditions for capitalism — is historically contested but culturally formative
- Alister McGrath's *Reformation Thought* is the best single academic introduction
- Diarmaid MacCulloch's *Reformation: Europe's House Divided 1490–1700* is the most comprehensive single-volume narrative
- Eamon Duffy's *The Stripping of the Altars* is the essential corrective to Whig narratives that treat the Reformation as obvious progress
