# Three Books of Occult Philosophy

## What it is
*De Occulta Philosophia Libri Tres* (Three Books of Occult Philosophy) by **Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa von Nettesheim**, published in full in 1531, is the most ambitious and comprehensive synthesis of Renaissance learned magic ever produced. It is the primary reference text of Western esoteric tradition from the 16th century through the 19th-century occult revival.

## Agrippa: who he was
Heinrich Cornelius Agrippa (1486–1535) was a German polymath — physician, lawyer, soldier, theologian, court secretary, and scholar. He moved across German courts, France, Italy, and the Low Countries, worked for the Holy Roman Emperor, and spent his life navigating the tension between learned respectability and the accusation of heresy and witchcraft.

He began writing *De Occulta Philosophia* around 1509-1510, circulated it in manuscript among scholars, then published a substantially revised version in 1531. He was already known for *De Incertitudine et Vanitate Scientiarum* (On the Uncertainty and Vanity of the Sciences, 1530) — a skeptical attack on all human knowledge including magic — which makes the relationship between the two works philosophically complex.

## The three-book structure

### Book 1: Natural Magic
The world of elemental, material correspondences. How physical objects — plants, stones, animals, metals — carry occult virtues that can be exploited by the magician.

Core theory: the Neoplatonic chain of sympathy connecting all material things to higher principles. Every object participates in a cosmic hierarchy; natural magic works by identifying and leveraging those connections.

Topics: elemental properties, the four humors, planetary sympathies, the power of numbers in nature, the occult virtues of specific materials.

Primary sources drawn on: Aristotle's natural philosophy, Pliny, Dioscorides, Arabic medical-magical tradition, Picatrix for planetary sympathies.

### Book 2: Celestial Magic
The world of mathematics, astrology, and the intermediate sphere between matter and spirit. How numbers, geometric figures, and astrological configurations carry power that the magician can channel.

Core theory: numbers are not just quantities — they are the formal principles by which the divine mind structures the cosmos. Mathematical structures (squares, triangles, specific numerical ratios) participate in divine order.

Topics: the celestial spheres, the power of numbers (numerology in the proper philosophical sense), magic squares and their planetary attributions, musical harmonics as cosmic ordering principles, the images of the decans and mansions of the Moon, talismanic timing.

Primary sources: Pythagoreanism, Platonic mathematics (especially *Timaeus*), Ficino's stellar medicine, Picatrix for the technical material.

The magic squares in Book 2 — the famous **Kameas** (Saturn's 3×3, Jupiter's 4×4, Mars's 5×5, Sun's 6×6, Venus's 7×7, Mercury's 8×8, Moon's 9×9) — became standard reference material for Western magical practice. They appear in the Golden Dawn, in Crowley, in virtually all subsequent ceremonial systems.

### Book 3: Ceremonial Magic
The world of intelligences, angels, and the divine. How to work with spiritual beings and divine names through ritual, invocation, and theurgy.

Core theory: the universe is populated by a hierarchy of spiritual intelligences mediating between the One and matter. The highest magic is contact with these intelligences, which requires purification, ritual preparation, and knowledge of divine names.

Topics: the three worlds (elemental, celestial, intellectual), the hierarchy of spiritual beings (angels, daemons, spirits of the elements), the divine names and their powers (extensive Kabbalistic material), prophetic dreams and divine inspiration, the ritual conditions for effective ceremonial work.

Primary sources: Neoplatonism (especially Iamblichus and Proclus on theurgy), Jewish Kabbalah, Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite's angelology, Hermeticism, *Sefer Yetzirah*.

## Agrippa's synthesis
The three-book structure is not arbitrary. It follows a Neoplatonic cosmological hierarchy:
- Matter → Nature → Soul → Mind → The One
- Natural magic works at the material level
- Celestial magic works at the soul level (numbers and stars mediate)
- Ceremonial magic works at the level of intellect and divinity

Each level is real; each type of magic is appropriate to its level. The entire system is philosophically coherent — not a collection of recipes but an attempt at a complete metaphysical account of how magic works and why.

## What Agrippa synthesized
Agrippa drew from:
- Classical Greek philosophy: Plato, Aristotle, Pythagoras, Plotinus, Proclus, Iamblichus
- Hermeticism: *Corpus Hermeticum*, *Asclepius*, Ficino's translations
- Kabbalah: substantial use of Kabbalistic divine names, *Sefer Yetzirah*, Pico della Mirandola's Christian Kabbalah
- Arabic magic: Picatrix is the dominant technical source for the planetary material
- Medieval natural philosophy: Albertus Magnus, Roger Bacon
- Classical mythology and astrology
- Neo-Pythagorean number theory

*De Occulta Philosophia* is the point where all these streams converge. It is the reason why subsequent Western esotericism looks like it does — the Hermetic-Kabbalistic-astrological synthesis is Agrippa's achievement more than anyone else's.

## Historical influence

### Direct influence on later Renaissance figures
- John Dee owned a copy, annotated it heavily, and built on it directly for his angelic magic project
- Giordano Bruno's memory magic and cosmological work engages with Agrippa
- Most subsequent grimoire traditions incorporate Agrippan material, often without attribution

### Influence on the 19th-century revival
The occult revival rediscovered Agrippa as the foundational systematic text:
- Eliphas Lévi's magical system draws heavily on Agrippa
- The Golden Dawn's system of correspondences — colors, symbols, divine names, planetary attributions — is substantially Agrippan in structure
- Crowley knew Agrippa directly and through the Golden Dawn inheritance

### Modern use
*De Occulta Philosophia* remains in active use. The Kameas (magic squares) appear in contemporary ceremonial magic. The three-world structure is standard in modern occult cosmology. Most "books of shadows" and correspondence tables in Wicca and contemporary paganism trace back ultimately to Agrippa.

## The text history
- Written: ~1509-1510 (early version)
- First partial publication: 1531 (Book 1 only, pirated)
- Full authorized publication: 1531 (all three books)
- First English translation: 1651 (J.F., London) — the version circulated in English occultism for centuries
- Modern scholarly edition: Tyson (Llewellyn, 1993) — accessible but with Tyson's annotations
- Critical Latin edition: still incomplete; the authoritative scholarly edition remains a gap in the field

## What to read
- For context: Wayne Shumaker, *The Occult Sciences in the Renaissance* (1972)
- For Agrippa specifically: Vittoria Perrone Compagni's work on the Latin text
- For the wider synthesis: Brian Vickers, *Occult and Scientific Mentalities in the Renaissance* (1984)
- The Tyson translation (Llewellyn) is accessible but adds Tyson's interpretation; approach with awareness

## Good working summary
*Three Books of Occult Philosophy* is the most important synthetic text in Western esoteric tradition. It assembled the Renaissance magical synthesis — Hermeticism, Kabbalah, Neoplatonism, astrological magic, natural philosophy — into a single coherent system. Its magic squares, correspondence tables, and three-world cosmology became the structural skeleton of virtually all subsequent Western ceremonial magic. You cannot understand the Golden Dawn, Crowley, or modern occultism without understanding that Agrippa is the source they all inherit from.
