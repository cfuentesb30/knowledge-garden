# Norse Magic — Seiðr, Galdr, and the Galdrabók

## Purpose
Deep treatment of the Norse magical tradition: its two primary modes (seiðr and galdr), their social and cosmological logic, and the later Icelandic grimoire tradition (galdrabók). For the religious context, see [[Germanic and Norse Paganism]]. For runic magic specifically, see [[Runes — History and Epigraphy]] and [[Runes in Esotericism and Modern Revival]].

## Framing: what kind of magic?
Old Norse sources describe magic not as a unified system but as a cluster of practices with different mechanisms, practitioners, and social valuations. The two dominant categories are:

- **Seiðr** — trance-based, receptive, concerned with fate and spirit worlds
- **Galdr** — voice-based incantation, active, concerned with direct effect (binding, healing, compelling)

These operated alongside a broader category of *fjölkynngi* ("much-knowing") — general magical or uncanny knowledge — and practices associated with specific substances, objects, and spaces.

Magic in the Norse world was not systematized into a single cosmology. Different practitioners operated with different models, and the sources preserve those differences.

## Seiðr in depth

### What it is
Seiðr (from a root related to "binding" or "seething") is the most sophisticated magical-shamanic practice in the Norse tradition. It involves:

1. **Altered-state journey**: The practitioner enters trance, often through specific preparations (sitting on a raised platform — *seiðhjallr*, music, song), to travel to other realms or invite spirit communication.
2. **Fate-working**: The practitioner could perceive, and in some cases alter, the threads of fate. This is the most feared form — the ability to bind or harm destinies.
3. **Divination**: Most of the attested völva practice is oracular — the community asks questions about harvests, futures, deaths, missing persons.
4. **Shapeshifting (*hamrammr*)**: Advanced practitioners could detach the *hamr* (shape/form) and send it out — appearing at distant locations, taking animal shapes.

### The equipment and setting
The best ethnographic description is the *Eiríks saga rauða* scene with the völva Þorbjörg:
- Blue cloak with cat-skin trimmings (blue = magic/liminal throughout Norse culture)
- Cat-skin gloves
- A staff with a brass knob (*seiðstafr* / *völr*) — the defining tool of the völva; staff has been found in female burials confirmed as probable practitioners
- Specific foods were prepared and offered to her
- A circle of women sat around her platform and sang the *varðlokur* — spirit-calling songs — to bring the spirits close

Without the varðlokur, Þorbjörg cannot work; the community's participation is required.

### Seiðr and gender
The gendered dimension is central and cannot be separated from the practice:
- Seiðr was the domain of women (völvur, seiðkonur)
- Male practitioners existed but were stigmatized as *argr* (unmanned, sexually passive)
- Odin practices seiðr and is explicitly called *argr* for it — one of the genuine tensions in his character
- The gender logic suggests seiðr involves receptivity and permeability — qualities associated with femininity in Norse ideology — as opposed to the active, bounded, aggressive qualities of warrior culture

Neil Price's research (*The Viking Way*) connects this to circumpolar shamanic traditions where the shaman often occupies a liminal gender role, sometimes dressing as or living as the other sex.

### Seiðr and the cosmological structure
Seiðr works because the practitioner can access *Hel* (the realm of the dead), communicate with *náir* (the dead), perceive the Norns' weaving at Urðarbrunnr, and potentially pull information or influence from those layers of reality. The *völuspá* frame — a seeress raised from death by Odin — is itself a seiðr performance model.

### Archaeological evidence
Several female burials have been identified as probable völvur:
- **Ósberg ship burial** (Norway, 834 CE): Two women buried in extraordinary wealth. One is interpreted as possibly a high-status völva.
- **Funen bog burial** (Denmark): Female burial with a staff and blue textiles
- **Haraldskaer Woman**: High-status Danish female burial with atypical goods

Neil Price has documented approximately 40 burials with the "witch's staff" (*seiðstafr*) across Scandinavia.

## Galdr in depth

### What it is
Galdr derives from *gala* — to crow, to sing, to chant. It is vocal magic: spoken or sung incantations that produce direct effects. More active than seiðr; less associated with trance or the underworld. Associated with:
- Runic inscriptions activated by speech
- Charms against illness, wounds, or hostile magic
- Binding and compulsion
- Cursing (*níð*)

### Galdr and runes
The Eddic sources link galdr and runes explicitly. The Sigrdrífumál passage catalogs rune-types activated through cutting and coloring, but also through vocalization:
> "Victory-runes you must know, if you wish to have victory... cut them on the sword hilt, some on the guard, and name Tyr twice."

The activation formula — cut, color (with blood), name — implies that the spoken name (*galdr*) completes the working. Runes are not self-activating; they require voiced intention.

### Galdr poetry
Old Norse had a specific meter associated with magic: *galdralag* (incantation meter). It appears in Hávamál and other Eddic poems, characterized by an added half-line that creates a circling, binding effect in the verse structure. The form itself was thought to carry power.

### Níð — the magical insult/curse
*Níð* was a particularly potent form of verbal magic — a formalized attack on another person's honor, particularly their masculine identity. A *níðstöng* (curse-pole) was a pole carved with insults and topped with a horse skull, erected facing the enemy's land. The *Egils saga* describes Egill Skallagrímsson erecting such a pole against King Eiríkr Blóðöx.

This was both magical act and legal/social weapon — the boundary between speech-act and supernatural working was porous.

## Icelandic grimoire tradition — the Galdrabók

### The transition
After Christianization, the Norse magical tradition did not disappear — it went underground, merged with Christian demonology, and eventually produced a distinct Icelandic grimoire tradition. This is one of the clearest cases of pagan-Christian magical syncretism in European history.

### The Galdrabók
The *Galdrabók* ("Book of Magic/Incantations") is an Icelandic grimoire, compiled c. 1600 (though drawing on older material). It contains:
- 47 spells including healing, love magic, protection, and cursing
- *Galdrastafir* — magical diagrams/symbols (distinct from historical runes but sometimes incorporating runic forms)
- Christian elements: names of God, Jesus, angels, alongside pagan deities
- Practical instructions: how to prepare materials, timing, activation words

The *galdrastafir* are visual symbols drawn or carved with specific intent — the most famous examples are:

**Ægishjálmr (Helm of Awe / Aegishjalmur)**: An eight-armed symbol, each arm a *ísaz* variant. Applied to the forehead before battle to induce terror in enemies and protection for the wearer. Mentioned in Fáfnismál (the dragon Fáfnir wore it). Widely used in modern neo-pagan contexts.

**Vegvísir (Viking Compass)**: A symbol with eight arms pointing in different directions. The Galdrabók version is intended to help the bearer find their way in storms. Often (incorrectly) called a "Viking compass" in popular culture — it appears in a 17th-century Icelandic manuscript, not in the Viking Age.

**Dreprún**: A cursing symbol, intended to harm an enemy.

### The Christian synthesis
The Icelandic grimoire tradition explicitly combines:
- Norse-derived magical logic (runic forms, fate-binding, spirit working)
- Christian demonology (spirits are named and commanded, with Christian divine names as power sources)
- Catholic ritual logic (timing by liturgical calendar, holy water as component)

This syncretism is the norm across European grimoire traditions — see [[Grimoires and Magical Manuals]] for parallel developments in the broader Western tradition. The Norse case is not unique; it is a regional variant of a general pattern.

## Connections to the broader Western esoteric tradition
Norse magic connects to the wider esoteric tradition at several points:

- **Runic use in Agrippa and later Renaissance occultism**: Cornelius Agrippa includes runes in his planetary correspondence tables in *Three Books of Occult Philosophy* — one of the early points where runic symbolism enters mainstream European occult writing
- **The grimoire tradition**: The Icelandic galdrabók belongs to the same broader category as the Key of Solomon, the Picatrix, etc.
- **19th-century occult revival**: List and the Theosophical milieu; Blavatsky's *Secret Doctrine* gestures toward Germanic/Aryan primordial wisdom
- **Golden Dawn**: Did not centrally incorporate Norse material but Aleister Crowley and later figures engaged with it
- **Modern neopaganism**: Ásatrú and Wicca developed on parallel tracks; many practitioners draw from both traditions

## Research notes
- Neil Price's *The Viking Way* (2019 expanded ed.) is the definitive scholarly work on seiðr
- The *Galdrabók* is available in English translation (Stephen Flowers, *The Galdrabók: An Icelandic Grimoire*)
- The *ægishjálmr* and *vegvísir* are often falsely attributed to Viking Age sources in popular culture — both appear in post-Conversion Icelandic manuscripts (17th century)
- The category *seiðr* has been compared to Siberian shamanism, Sámi *noaidi* practice, and Finnish *tietäjä* tradition — plausible given circumpolar cultural connections
