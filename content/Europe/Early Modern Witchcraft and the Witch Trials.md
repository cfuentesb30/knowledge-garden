# Early Modern Witchcraft and the Witch Trials

## Purpose
The early modern European witch trials as a religious, social, and legal phenomenon — what was believed about witches, where the theology came from, how trials operated, and what the phenomenon actually was. Connects to the broader European occult shelf and to the origins of modern Wicca. For the broader occult context, see [[Western Esotericism Overview]] and [[Occult Revival and Secret Orders]].

## Scale and geography
The great witch trials occurred approximately 1430–1750 CE, with peak intensity 1560–1650. Scholarly estimates:
- ~50,000–60,000 executions for witchcraft in Europe (not the 9 million of popular myth)
- ~100,000+ trials; execution rate roughly 50% of those tried
- Most intense in: the Holy Roman Empire (southwestern Germany, Switzerland, Austrian territories), Scotland, France, and Scandinavia
- Notably less intense in: England (which used hanging not burning; relatively fewer executions), Spain and Italy (where the Inquisition was actually more skeptical), Ireland and Portugal
- The Salem trials (Massachusetts, 1692) produced ~19 executions — significant historically but minor in European scale

## What people believed witches were

### The theological construction
The elite intellectual concept of the witch that drove the trials was not simply folk belief in malefic magic. It was a specific theological-legal construct built from multiple elements:

**1. Maleficium** (harmful magic): The ancient and widespread belief that some people could cause harm through supernatural means — disease, death of livestock, impotence, crop failure. This belief is universal and pre-Christian.

**2. The diabolic pact**: The crucial theological addition. The witch's power comes from an explicit compact with the Devil — the witch surrenders her soul and body to Satan in exchange for power. This transforms witchcraft from a practice into heresy: it is fundamentally apostasy, the rejection of God for the Devil.

**3. The Sabbath**: Witches gather (physically or spiritually) at night assemblies presided over by the Devil. They fly (on brooms, animals, or in spirit form) to these gatherings. They engage in feasting, dancing, sexual intercourse with demons, and the ritual inversion of Christian rites (the Black Mass construct). They receive new powers and instructions.

**4. The pact confirmed bodily**: The Devil marks his witches — the *witch's mark* (an insensitive spot on the body, thought to be a secondary nipple or seal) was searched for by prickers in trials as physical evidence of the pact.

This theological construct — pact + sabbath + mark + maleficium — is largely a 15th-century intellectual construction, not a description of anything people were actually doing.

### The Malleus Maleficarum (1486)
*The Hammer of Witches* — written by Heinrich Kramer (Institoris), a Dominican inquisitor, with claimed co-authorship by Jacob Sprenger. Arguably the most influential text in shaping the persecution. It:
- Synthesizes the theological witch-concept into a systematic manual
- Argues at length that witchcraft is real and that skepticism is itself a form of heresy
- Emphasizes women's susceptibility to diabolism (with misogynistic theology: women are weaker in faith, more lustful, easier prey for the Devil)
- Provides instructions for identifying, interrogating, and prosecuting witches
- Received skeptical or hostile reception from many contemporaries but was widely reprinted

The *Malleus* was not an official Catholic or Inquisition document — Kramer tried to get Papal endorsement (*Summis desiderantes affectibus*, 1484) and largely forged the theological faculty's endorsement. But it was influential nonetheless.

## Where the ideas came from

### Night-flying women — the Canon Episcopi
A 10th-century canon (*Canon Episcopi*) stated that women who claimed to fly through the night with Diana were deluded by the Devil — their night-flights were illusions. For centuries, the Church's official position was skepticism about actual witchcraft: it was a delusion, not a real practice.

This reversed in the 15th century. The "real sabbath" idea — physical, not illusory — made the witch a real criminal rather than a deluded person.

### Heresy trial procedures
The Inquisition's heresy procedures (developed against Cathars and Waldensians, 12th–13th centuries) provided the legal infrastructure — intensive interrogation, torture, testimony against networks, the social idea of a conspiracy of heretics. The witch-trial adopted this framework wholesale: witches were a conspiracy of Devil-worshippers, like a secret heretical sect.

### Folk magic and cunning folk
Most people accused of witchcraft were not practicing anything like the theological construct. They were:
- Cunning folk (*wise women*, *cunning men*, *pellars*): practitioners of folk healing, charms, divination, unbewitching
- People with neighborhood conflicts who had spoken threats
- People accused by neighbors in the context of misfortune
- Midwives (a significant targeting pattern in some regions)
- Elderly poor women without social protection (the stereotypical target)

The gap between the theological witch (sabbath-attending diabolist) and the accused person (herbalist, quarrelsome neighbor) is the core tragedy.

## Torture and confession
Most confessions of sabbath attendance, flight, pacts, and diabolism were produced under torture or the threat of it. The *question extraordinaire* — judicial torture — was standard practice in continental European (Inquisitorial) legal procedure for serious crimes. Once a confession was obtained, accused witches were asked to name accomplices — producing chain-reaction panics.

The dynamic: accusation → arrest → torture → confession → denunciation of others → more arrests → more torture. In severe panics (Würzburg 1626–31: ~300 executions; Bamberg 1626–31: ~300 executions) the chains reached into high civic officials.

## Who was accused
Demographics (from modern historical analysis):
- **Sex**: ~75–80% of accused were women (varies by region; some regions show more male accusations)
- **Age**: Often older women, post-menopausal, widowed — people with reduced social protection
- **Economic status**: Mixed — many accused were poor, but during mass panics the accusations could reach wealthy citizens
- **Social position**: People involved in folk healing, midwifery, reputation for *evil eye* or malicious speech; people with ongoing neighborhood conflicts

The misogynistic theology of the *Malleus* is not merely intellectual — the statistical pattern of female accusation is real and requires sociological explanation.

## The decline of the trials
The trials declined because of:
- Growing skepticism among elites about the reliability of torture-extracted confessions
- Legal reform (many German principalities revised their torture and evidence rules)
- Philosophical skepticism (Reginald Scot's *Discoverie of Witchcraft*, 1584; Balthasar Bekker's *The World Bewitched*, 1691)
- The shift toward mechanistic natural philosophy — the "disenchantment" narrative
- Political exhaustion after the mass panics revealed the social destruction they caused

The last execution for witchcraft in England: 1685. Germany: 1775. Poland: 1793 (among the last in Europe).

## The witch trials and gender
The witch trials were not "the burning times" targeting a suppressed feminist-pagan religion (a claim made by early feminist and Wiccan writers, especially influenced by Margaret Murray's now-discredited thesis). They were a multi-causal social phenomenon in which:
- Patriarchal theology made women structurally more vulnerable to accusations
- Neighbor dynamics and legal processes amplified specific social conflicts
- Men were also accused (particularly in some regions)
- The victims were not practitioners of a coherent alternative religion

This is an important historical-political distinction. The suffering was real. The "burning times" mythology is not.

## Margaret Murray and the witch-cult thesis
Margaret Murray (*The Witch-Cult in Western Europe*, 1921) argued that the accused witches were practitioners of a surviving pre-Christian fertility religion. This thesis was enormously influential on Gerald Gardner, who used it as the historical legitimation for Wicca.

It is now considered thoroughly discredited by historians. The pattern of accusations Murray saw as evidence of a real cult is fully explicable by the shared theological framework that questioners applied — not by an actual pre-Christian religious underground.

## Connection to modern Wicca
The pathway: Murray's thesis → Gerald Gardner (*Witchcraft Today*, 1954) → Wicca as a religious movement claiming descent from the ancient witch-cult. The actual historical discontinuity means Wicca is a 20th-century religious creation (with genuine historical influences from ceremonial magic, folk practice, Romanticism, and Theosophy) rather than a survival. This does not make Wicca invalid as a religion — but it changes the historical claim.

See [[Occult Revival and Secret Orders]] for the Golden Dawn / ceremonial magic input into Gardner's synthesis.

## Research notes
- The "9 million burned" figure is false — the actual figure is ~50,000–60,000 (over 300 years)
- Brian Levack's *The Witch-Hunt in Early Modern Europe* is the standard academic introduction
- Lyndal Roper's *Witch Craze* examines the trials through a psychoanalytic-social lens
- Carlo Ginzburg's *The Night Battles* and *Ecstasies* argue for genuine folk traditions underlying some trial confessions — a more nuanced position than "nothing was there"
- The Inquisition was in several regions *less* likely to execute accused witches than secular courts
