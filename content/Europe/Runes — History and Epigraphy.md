# Runes — History and Epigraphy

## Purpose
Historical and linguistic study of runes — what they actually were, how they evolved, and what the inscriptions show. For magical and esoteric use, see [[Runes in Esotericism and Modern Revival]]. For mythological context (Odin's runic ordeal), see [[Eddas and Norse Primary Sources]].

## What runes actually are
Runes are the letters of the alphabets used to write Germanic languages before (and in some contexts alongside) the adoption of the Latin alphabet. They are not primarily magical symbols — they are a writing system. The magic came later and unevenly.

The word *rún* (Old Norse/Old English) did carry meanings beyond alphabet character: secret, mystery, whispered counsel. This semantic depth is real but should not be back-projected to claim that all runic inscriptions are magical. Most are practical.

## Origins
The origin of the runic script is debated but the mainstream scholarly view is that it derived from a North Italic or Latin-related alphabet, probably via contact between Germanic peoples and Mediterranean or Celtic populations in the 1st–2nd centuries CE. The earliest unambiguous runic inscription is the Vimose comb inscription from Denmark (c. 160 CE).

The derivation from an indigenous Germanic magical system (as claimed by 19th-century and modern occult traditions) is not supported by the linguistic evidence. The letter shapes and values show systematic borrowing from Mediterranean scripts.

## Elder Futhark (c. 150–700 CE)
The oldest and longest-lived runic alphabet. 24 characters, named after the first six letters: F-U-Þ-A-R-K.

### The 24 runes in three *ættir* (groups of eight)

**Freyr's ætt (1–8):**
ᚠ Fehu (cattle/wealth) · ᚢ Uruz (aurochs/strength) · ᚦ Thurisaz (giant/thorn) · ᚨ Ansuz (god/mouth) · ᚱ Raidho (ride/journey) · ᚲ Kenaz (torch/knowledge) · ᚷ Gebo (gift) · ᚹ Wunjo (joy)

**Heimdall's ætt (9–16):**
ᚺ Hagalaz (hail) · ᚾ Nauthiz (need/constraint) · ᛁ Isa (ice) · ᛃ Jera (year/harvest) · ᛇ Eihwaz (yew tree) · ᛈ Perthro (unclear) · ᛉ Algiz (elk/protection) · ᛊ Sowilo (sun)

**Tyr's ætt (17–24):**
ᛏ Tiwaz (Tyr/victory) · ᛒ Berkano (birch/growth) · ᛖ Ehwaz (horse/partnership) · ᛗ Mannaz (human/self) · ᛚ Laguz (water/lake) · ᛜ Ingwaz (Ing/Freyr) · ᛞ Dagaz (day) · ᛟ Othalan (inheritance/estate)

### What Elder Futhark inscriptions contain
- Personal names and ownership marks ("X made this")
- Maker's marks on weapons, tools, jewelry
- Memorial inscriptions
- Protective formulas and charms
- Some inscriptions that appear to invoke or name gods
- The word *alu* (meaning uncertain — ale? protective formula?) appears very frequently

The *Gallehus horns* inscription (Denmark, c. 400 CE) — one of the earliest long Elder Futhark inscriptions: "Ek hlewagastiz holtijaz horna tawido" (I, Hlewagastiz, son of Holt, made the horn). A craftsman signing his work.

## Younger Futhark (c. 700–1100 CE)
During the Viking Age, the Elder Futhark contracted to 16 runes — paradoxically fewer letters to represent a phonologically more complex language. This seems counterintuitive and is partly explained by the theory that the script was in decline and reductive pressures operated differently in a primarily oral culture. The Younger Futhark is the dominant runic script of the Viking Age and is found on the majority of Scandinavian runic inscriptions.

### Long-branch vs. short-twig variants
Two parallel Younger Futhark forms: Danish (long-branch) and Swedish-Norwegian (short-twig/Rök). Functionally equivalent, regionally distributed.

### The runestones
The majority of surviving runic inscriptions (~6,000 of ~6,400 total Scandinavian inscriptions) are on runestones from Sweden, primarily from the Viking Age (900–1100 CE). They are mostly memorial inscriptions — "X raised this stone after Y, his kinsman/father/brother, a good warrior." Christianization is visible in the runestones: many include Christian crosses and formulas alongside older commemorative traditions.

The *Rök stone* (Östergötland, Sweden, c. 800 CE) is the longest runic inscription, containing what appears to be fragments of heroic legend, riddles, and possibly references to Theodoric the Great.

## Anglo-Saxon Futhorc (5th–11th century CE)
The runes used in Anglo-Saxon England, expanded to 28–33 characters to represent Old English phonology. Found in manuscripts as well as objects.

The *Ruthwell Cross* (Dumfriesshire, c. 700 CE) bears one of the earliest Old English poems (*The Dream of the Rood*) in runes — a Christian context.

The *Norwich casket / Franks Casket* (c. 700 CE) contains both runic and Latin inscriptions with mixed pagan and Christian scenes.

## Medieval runes (c. 1100–1800)
After Christianization, runes continued to be used in Scandinavia (especially Sweden) for centuries in:
- Calendars (*primstav*, *rimstav*)
- Church records
- Memorial inscriptions
- Swedish Dalecarlian runes (used into the early 20th century — one of the longest continuous runic traditions)

The notion that runes "died out" with Christianization is false. They remained in use — just gradually displaced by Latin-alphabet literacy.

## Magical and divinatory use in actual history
The evidence for magical runic use is real but more limited than modern occultism implies:

- Some inscriptions appear to be protective charms or curses
- *Alu*, *laukaz*, *auja* appear in contexts suggesting apotropaic (protective) function
- The *Lindholm amulet* (c. 400 CE) has a formula-like text with an unusual sequence suggesting magical intent
- Runic inscriptions on weapons may have been intended to empower them
- The Eddic texts (Hávamál, Sigrdrífumál) describe rune-magic: victory runes, birth runes, healing runes, etc. — but these are literary and later

The *Sigrdrífumál* in the Poetic Edda has a famous catalog: "Victory runes you must know, if you want to have victory... cut them on the sword hilt... Beer runes you must know... Birth runes you must know... Thought runes you must know..." This is a literary/poetic framework, not an archaeological description.

## What the evidence does NOT support
- The idea that runes were primarily or originally a magical system rather than an alphabet
- Continuous magical runic tradition from ancient times to the present
- Most of the divinatory correspondences used in modern runic practice (these are largely 20th-century constructions — see [[Runes in Esotericism and Modern Revival]])
- The connection between specific rune meanings and Jungian archetypes is modern, not ancient

## Research notes
- The standard academic reference for runic inscriptions is the *Rundata* database (Scandinavian Runic-Text Database, Uppsala University) — contains all known Scandinavian runic inscriptions.
- The academic field studying runes is *runology*. Key figures: Klaus Düwel, Michael Barnes, Terje Spurkland.
- Many popular runic books (especially those claiming "ancient" wisdom systems) confuse historical runic use with modern esoteric reconstructions.
