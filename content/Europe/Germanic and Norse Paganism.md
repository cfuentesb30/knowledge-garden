# Germanic and Norse Paganism

## Purpose
Covers the actual religious practice of pre-Christian Germanic and Norse peoples — ritual, cult, priests, sacred sites, afterlife belief, and shamanic traditions. For mythology and cosmology, see [[Norse Mythology Overview]]. For primary texts, see [[Eddas and Norse Primary Sources]].

## What we're actually studying
"Norse paganism" is a modern label for a diverse set of pre-Christian Germanic religious practices that varied significantly by region (Scandinavia, Iceland, Anglo-Saxon England, continental Germanic territories), period (Bronze Age through Viking Age, roughly 3000 BCE–1100 CE), and social context. There was no single church, no unified creed, no central institution, and no orthodoxy. What existed: local cults, shared mythological narratives, seasonal rituals, sacrificial practices, and specialist religious roles.

This must be distinguished from:
- The Eddic mythological corpus (literary, post-Conversion, Icelandic)
- Modern Ásatrú and Heathenry (contemporary reconstruction and revival)
- Romantic/nationalist 19th-century reworkings
- Pop-occult runic mysticism

## Ritual practice

### Blót (sacrifice)
The central Norse/Germanic ritual. *Blót* (Old Norse: "blood sacrifice," cognate with Gothic *blotan*, "to worship") involved animal sacrifice — horses, cattle, boars, and occasionally humans in extreme or high-stakes contexts (though human sacrifice is debated in scope). Blood (*blót*) was sprinkled on altar objects, cult images, and participants. The sacrificed animal was then cooked and eaten communally. These feasts reinforced social bonds and reciprocal relationships with the divine.

Major blóts followed a seasonal calendar:
- **Dísablót** (early spring) — for the dísir (female protective spirits), agricultural fertility
- **Sigrblót** (spring) — for victory in summer raiding or warfare
- **Alfablót** (autumn, after harvest) — for the elves/ancestors, private household ceremony
- **Jólablót / Yule** (midwinter, around winter solstice) — major communal feast; Odin's wild hunt; feasting and gift-giving

### Sacred sites — hof and hörgr
- **Hof**: temple building. The Icelandic sagas describe hof structures with cult images of deities, an inner sanctum, and an altar for sacrifice. Archaeological evidence is sparser than the literary descriptions suggest — what survives is usually evidence of feasting structures rather than purpose-built temples.
- **Hörgr**: outdoor stone cairn or altar. Older than the hof and more directly tied to the land itself.
- **Sacred groves and natural features**: trees (ash, oak), springs, bogs, and mountains were frequently treated as sacred. Bog deposits (including the famous bog bodies of Northern Europe) may reflect sacrificial practice.

**Uppsala temple (Sweden)**: According to Adam of Bremen (11th century), there was a great temple at Uppsala where gold-decked images of Odin, Thor, and Freyr stood; every nine years a festival sacrificed nine males of every animal species (and humans) and hung the bodies in a sacred grove. This description is from a hostile Christian source and is probably exaggerated, but the site's importance is confirmed archaeologically.

### Seiðr — Norse shamanic magic
Seiðr (Old Norse, related to "seething" or "binding") was the most significant magical-shamanic practice in Norse religion. It involved:
- Entering trance states to travel between worlds or communicate with the dead
- Prophecy and divination
- Binding and loosening fates
- Shapeshifting and out-of-body travel (*útiseta*, "sitting out")

**The völva**: A female practitioner (sometimes male) of seiðr. Travelled from community to community performing divination. The *Völuspá* (Prophecy of the Seeress) is framed as a völva speaking to Odin. The story of the völva Þorbjörg in the *Eiríks saga rauða* is one of the best descriptions: she wore a blue cloak, cat-skin gloves, a staff with a brass knob, and required a specific song (*varðlokur*) to be sung by women to summon the spirits. She then answered questions about the future.

**Odin and seiðr**: Odin himself practiced seiðr, learned from Freyja. This was considered *ergi* (unmanning, a form of gendered shame) for a male, which is part of why Odin is ambiguous and liminal. The connection between seiðr and gender transgression is well attested.

**Ergi and social stigma**: Male practitioners of seiðr (except Odin) were seen as *argr* — shamefully passive or unmanned. This says as much about Germanic gender ideology as it does about the practice itself.

### Galdr — spoken/sung magic
Galdr was magical chanting or incantation — spoken/sung spells rather than trance-based practice. More martial and active in character than seiðr. Runes were associated with galdr in many contexts (see [[Runes — History and Epigraphy]]).

## Afterlife beliefs
Norse afterlife was plural and not systematically unified:

- **Valhalla** (Óðinn's hall): Warriors killed in battle, chosen by Valkyries, feast and fight daily in preparation for Ragnarök. This is the elite warrior vision and probably over-represented in the literary record.
- **Fólkvangr** (Freyja's field): Freyja receives half the battle-slain.
- **Hel**: The realm of the ordinary dead — not necessarily grim, just different. The goddess Hel rules it. Most people went here.
- **Ránarísteri** (sea): Drowned sailors may go to Rán, a sea goddess.
- **Ancestor mounds (haugr)**: Strong belief that the dead could remain active in their burial mounds, and could be consulted.
- **Reincarnation within the family**: Some sagas suggest a belief in rebirth within the same kin group.

## The Valkyries
Female supernatural beings serving Odin — they chose who died in battle and escorted the chosen to Valhalla. In later literary sources they become romanticized; in older sources they are more terrifying figures. Their name means "choosers of the slain."

## Ásatrú — modern reconstruction
The contemporary revival religion, formally organized in Iceland in 1972 (Ásatrúarfélagið, founded by Sveinbjörn Beinteinsson). Now practiced globally. Not a single unified movement — ranges from archaeological-reconstruction-oriented practice to more spiritualized or magical forms.

Key distinctions in modern Heathenry:
- **Reconstructionist Heathenry**: tries to practice as close to historical evidence as possible
- **Universalist vs. folkish Heathenry**: a significant and contentious division — folkish Heathenry links practice to Germanic ethnic identity and has overlaps with white nationalist movements; universalist Heathenry is explicitly open to all people. This distinction matters and is not trivial.

## Research notes
- The best evidence for actual Norse/Germanic religious practice is archaeological, not textual. The textual sources are mostly post-Conversion, Icelandic, and shaped by literary and political purposes.
- Adam of Bremen, Saxo Grammaticus, and similar sources are useful but must be treated critically — they are Christian writers describing paganism, often polemically.
- Do not project modern Wicca/neopagan frameworks back onto historical Norse practice. They are different phenomena.
- Seiðr has attracted significant scholarly attention since the 1990s (Clive Tolley, Neil Price) and is much better understood now than in older occult-adjacent sources.
