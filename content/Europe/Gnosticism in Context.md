# Gnosticism in Context

## Scope
This note explains what people usually mean by "Gnosticism," why the label is contested, and how these movements fit into the wider religious world of late antiquity.

## Important caution
"Gnosticism" is useful as a shorthand, but scholars often debate whether it lumps together too many different groups and texts.
So use the term carefully.

## Core themes often associated with Gnostic-type movements
- special or saving knowledge (*gnosis*)
- sharp distinction between higher divine reality and flawed material existence
- complex cosmology and emanation schemes
- intermediary beings or aeons
- a flawed creator/demiurge in some systems
- liberation through revealed knowledge

## Historical context
These movements appear within the wider late antique world shaped by:
- Judaism
- Christianity
- Hellenistic philosophy
- mystery traditions
- apocalyptic and revelatory literature

## Why it matters
Gnostic currents matter because they show how unstable and intellectually competitive the late antique religious world was.
Orthodoxy did not emerge into an empty room.

## Texts and evidence
Much of what we know comes from:
- heresiological critiques by later orthodox writers
- Nag Hammadi texts discovered in the 20th century
- fragments of competing cosmologies and revelatory literature

## Ritual and religious life
Evidence varies by group, and this is exactly why overgeneralization is dangerous.
But themes can include:
- initiation
- revealed discourse
- symbolic ritual
- ascetic tendencies in some groups
- alternative readings of biblical narratives

## Recommended resources
- Karen King — work on the invention/problem of Gnosticism
- Bentley Layton
- Hans Jonas (important but older)
- Elaine Pagels for accessible entry points, with caution and supplementation

## Good working summary
"Gnosticism" is best treated as a contested umbrella for several late antique movements centered on hidden knowledge, cosmological tension, and alternative readings of the divine, world, and salvation.
