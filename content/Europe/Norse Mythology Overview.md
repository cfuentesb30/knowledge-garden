# Norse Mythology Overview

## Purpose
Entry-point note for Norse cosmology, pantheon, and eschatology. For religious practice, see [[Germanic and Norse Paganism]]. For primary texts, see [[Eddas and Norse Primary Sources]]. For runes, see [[Runes — History and Epigraphy]] and [[Runes in Esotericism and Modern Revival]].

## What Norse mythology is and isn't
Norse mythology is the body of stories and cosmological frameworks preserved mainly in Old Norse literature from medieval Iceland — written down largely after Christianization (12th–14th century). It reflects pre-Christian Germanic religious worldview but is filtered through Christian-era writers, most importantly Snorri Sturluson. It is not a unified scripture or theology; it is a collection of narrative and poetic traditions with internal tensions and regional variation.

The popular image (Odin = wisdom, Thor = strength, Loki = trickster, etc.) is real but compressed. The actual tradition is stranger, darker, and less tidy than Marvel or casual pop culture suggests.

## Cosmology — Nine Worlds and Yggdrasil

### Yggdrasil
Yggdrasil is the cosmic world-tree, an immense ash tree that holds the Nine Worlds together. Three roots extend to:
- Asgard / the well of Urðr (fate), tended by the Norns
- Jötunheimr / Mímisbrunnr (Mímir's well of wisdom, where Odin sacrificed an eye)
- Niflheim / Hvergelmir (the primordial cold spring, source of eleven rivers)

Creatures inhabit it: an eagle at the crown, a serpent (Níðhöggr) gnawing the root from below, a squirrel (Ratatoskr) running between them carrying insults. This structure models cosmic interdependence and ongoing entropic pressure.

### The Nine Worlds
The worlds are not always consistently enumerated across sources, but the canonical list includes:
- **Asgard** — realm of the Æsir (principal gods)
- **Vanaheim** — realm of the Vanir (fertility/earth gods)
- **Álfheim** — realm of the light elves
- **Midgard** — middle world of humans
- **Jötunheimr** — realm of the giants (jötnar)
- **Svartálfaheimr / Niðavellir** — realm of dwarves
- **Niflheim** — primordial cold/mist realm
- **Muspelheim** — primordial fire realm
- **Hel** — realm of the dead

### Creation
In the beginning: the gap (Ginnungagap) between Niflheim (cold) and Muspelheim (fire). When they met, the primordial giant Ymir formed from the melting ice. From Ymir's body the gods created the world: bones became mountains, blood became seas, skull became sky, brain became clouds. The gods (Odin, Vili, Véi) then created the first humans (Ask and Embla) from two trees, giving them breath, consciousness, and appearance.

## The Pantheon

### The Æsir
The principal divine family associated with war, kingship, and cosmic order.

**Odin (Allfather)**
Chief of the Æsir. God of wisdom, war, death, poetry, magic (seiðr), and the runes. One-eyed — sacrificed the eye to drink from Mímir's well. Hung on Yggdrasil for nine days, wounded by a spear, to obtain the runes. Accompanied by two ravens (Huginn and Muninn — Thought and Memory) and two wolves (Geri and Freki). Receives half the battle-dead at Valhalla (the other half go to Freyja's Fólkvangr). Relentlessly pursues knowledge because he knows Ragnarök is coming and tries to delay it. A god of ambiguity — deceptive, shape-shifting, patron of poets and kings and the hanged.

**Thor**
Son of Odin. God of thunder, storms, strength, and protection of Midgard (humanity). Wields Mjölnir. Protector against chaos and giants. Most popular cult deity in the Viking Age — widely worshipped by farmers and seafarers. His cult left deeper ordinary-person archaeological traces than Odin's more elite and martial cult.

**Tyr**
God of law, justice, and single combat. Sacrificed his hand in the binding of Fenrir (placed his hand in the wolf's mouth as pledge — and the pledge was broken). Pre-Viking Germanic evidence suggests Tyr (cognate with Latin *deus*, Sanskrit *deva*) may have been the original sky-father before Odin's prominence rose.

**Baldr**
Son of Odin and Frigg. God of light, purity, and beauty. Most beloved of the gods. Killed by a mistletoe dart manipulated by Loki — the event that begins the chain leading to Ragnarök. He resides in Hel until after Ragnarök, when he will return.

**Loki**
A jötunn (giant) who lives among the Æsir as a blood-brother of Odin. A shape-shifting trickster — sometimes helpful, increasingly destructive. Father of Fenrir (the wolf), Jörmungandr (the Midgard Serpent), and Hel. Eventually bound beneath the earth for engineering Baldr's death; his writhing causes earthquakes. At Ragnarök he breaks free and steers Naglfar against the gods.

**Heimdall**
Watchman of the gods. Guards Bifröst (the rainbow bridge). Carries Gjallarhorn — at Ragnarök he will blow it to summon the gods.

**Frigg**
Odin's wife. Goddess of marriage, foresight, and household. Knows the fates of all beings but does not speak them. Sometimes conflated with Freyja in scholarly debate.

### The Vanir
An older family of fertility and nature gods, associated with prosperity, sexuality, and seiðr (shamanic magic). After a war with the Æsir, the two groups exchanged hostages and merged.

**Freyja**
Goddess of love, sexuality, fertility, war, and magic. Most powerful practitioner of seiðr (and reportedly the one who taught it to Odin). Receives half the battle-slain in her hall Fólkvangr. Weeps tears of gold. Owns the Brísingamen necklace and the feather cloak enabling bird transformation.

**Freyr**
Freyja's brother. God of fertility, prosperity, sunshine, and rain. Associated with the boar and the phallus. His cult at Uppsala was prominent.

## Eschatology — Ragnarök
Ragnarök ("doom of the gods" or "fate of the gods") is the pre-determined end of the current world order:

**Sequence:**
1. Fimbulwinter — three years of unending winter
2. Cosmic breakdown — familial bonds collapse, moral order fails
3. Loki and his children break free; Fenrir swallows the sun; Jörmungandr rises from the ocean
4. Naglfar sets sail carrying an army of the dead
5. Major battles: Odin is swallowed by Fenrir (avenged by Víðarr); Thor kills Jörmungandr and dies from its venom; Tyr and Garm kill each other; Freyr dies (he gave away his sword); Heimdall and Loki kill each other; Surtr burns the world

**After Ragnarök:**
The world resurfaces renewed from the sea. Surviving gods regroup. Baldr returns from Hel. A surviving man and woman (Líf and Lífþrasir) repopulate the earth. This cyclical pattern — destruction followed by renewal — distinguishes Norse eschatology from purely apocalyptic frameworks.

## Key Concepts

**Wyrd / Urðr**
Fate, as woven by the three Norns (Urðr, Verðandi, Skuld — roughly Past, Present, Future) at the well beneath Yggdrasil's root. Even the gods cannot escape it — Odin's knowledge of Ragnarök does not allow him to prevent it.

**Honour and shame**
The Norse moral universe was heavily honour-based. A warrior's reputation survived death; cowardice and dishonour were the real deaths. This is the cultural soil in which the Valhalla mythology grew.

**The soul complex**
Old Norse thought held multiple soul-parts: the *hugr* (mind/thought), *hamr* (shape/form, changeable by magic), *fylgja* (attendant spirit, often animal-shaped), and *hamingja* (luck, transmissible within a family). Not a simple mind/body dualism.

## Research notes
- Do not treat the Eddas as direct transcription of Viking Age belief. They are literary/scholarly works by Christian-era Icelanders. Snorri is an editor with an agenda.
- Norse mythology's modern popular form is heavily shaped by 19th-century Romanticism — especially German Romanticism (Wagner, etc.) — and later by 20th-century fantasy.
- Runic associations with Odin and with magic should be read alongside [[Runes — History and Epigraphy]] and [[Runes in Esotericism and Modern Revival]].
