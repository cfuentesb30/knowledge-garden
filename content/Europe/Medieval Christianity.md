# Medieval Christianity

## Purpose
Christianity as a total civilizational system in Western Europe, c. 500–1500 CE — its institutional structure, monastic movements, scholastic theology, popular religion, crusades, heresy responses, and mystical currents. For the broader Christian context, see [[Christianity in Europe Overview]]. For mysticism specifically, see [[Christian Mysticism and Contemplative Tradition]].

## What medieval Christianity was
Medieval Western Christianity was not primarily a religion of personal belief in the modern sense. It was:
- A total social institution structuring birth, marriage, death, agriculture, law, and time
- A bureaucratic-legal system (canon law, ecclesiastical courts, the papacy as supranational power)
- An intellectual tradition (the university, scholasticism)
- An economic system (monastic estates, tithes, the financing of crusades)
- A cosmological frame (the great chain of being, the liturgical year as temporal order)

The parish was the basic unit. Most people's Christian life was: baptism, Sunday mass, confession and penance, last rites, burial in consecrated ground. Saints, relics, pilgrimage, and the liturgical calendar structured the year. Theology was largely the business of clerics; most laypeople had no direct Bible access and followed a ritual Christianity embedded in community and season.

## The institutional structure

### The papacy
The Bishop of Rome claimed primacy over all Western Christianity — a claim contested by Eastern bishops (leading to the Great Schism of 1054) and by secular rulers throughout the medieval period. Papal power peaked in the High Middle Ages:
- **Pope Gregory VII** (r. 1073–1085): *Dictatus Papae* asserted that only the pope could appoint or remove bishops, depose emperors, and call universal councils — the high-water mark of theocratic ambition
- **Investiture Controversy**: Decades of conflict between popes and Holy Roman Emperors over who had the right to appoint bishops (who controlled both church and vast secular estates). Resolved partially at the Concordat of Worms (1122).
- **Pope Innocent III** (r. 1198–1216): The most powerful medieval pope — launched the Fourth Crusade, the Albigensian Crusade against the Cathars, presided over the Fourth Lateran Council (1215) which mandated annual confession, defined transubstantiation, and imposed distinctive dress on Jews and Muslims.
- **The Avignon Papacy** (1309–1377): Popes relocated to Avignon, under French influence — damaged papal prestige significantly. The subsequent Great Schism (1378–1417, multiple rival popes) further eroded authority.

### Canon law
A parallel legal system covering: marriage and family, inheritance, clerical behavior, heresy, usury, and testamentary bequests. Enforced through ecclesiastical courts. Gratian's *Decretum* (c. 1140) was the foundational codification.

### The sacramental system
Seven sacraments defined by the Fourth Lateran Council (1215):
1. **Baptism** — entry into the church, removal of original sin; required for salvation; infants baptized as soon as possible after birth
2. **Eucharist** — the Mass; *transubstantiation* (bread and wine become literally the body and blood of Christ) defined as doctrine 1215
3. **Confirmation** — deepening of baptismal grace
4. **Penance** — confession, contrition, absolution, satisfaction; annual confession to parish priest mandated from 1215
5. **Extreme unction** (last rites) — anointing of the dying
6. **Holy orders** — ordination of clergy
7. **Marriage** — elevated to sacramental status (with significant consequences for divorce law)

### Purgatory
The doctrine of purgatory — a post-death state of purifying suffering for souls not yet ready for heaven — was not formally defined until the Council of Florence (1439) and the Council of Trent (1545), but was practically assumed and shaped Christian life throughout the medieval period. Prayers for the dead, masses for the dead, and indulgences were mechanisms for reducing purgatorial time. This motivated much of medieval religious philanthropy (endowing chantry chapels, founding monasteries to pray for donors' souls) and became a major target of Protestant critique.

### Indulgences
The remission of temporal punishment (purgatorial time) due for confessed sins, granted by papal authority drawing on the "treasury of merit" — Christ's and the saints' surplus merit. Originally granted for crusade participation; later sold. The practice that triggered Luther's revolt.

## Monasticism

### The Benedictine tradition
St. Benedict of Nursia (c. 480–547) wrote the *Rule of Saint Benedict* — a practical guide for communal monastic life: eight hours of prayer (*Opus Dei* — the Divine Office), eight hours of work, eight hours of sleep. The *Rule* became the dominant template for Western monasticism. Benedictine monasteries were:
- Agricultural estates managing vast tracts of land
- Scriptoria preserving and copying manuscripts (how most of classical antiquity survived)
- Schools and hospitals
- Power centers with significant local political authority

### Reform movements
Monastic life repeatedly fell into laxity and required reform:
- **Cluniac reform** (10th century): Cluny (Burgundy) became the center of a reformed, pope-directly-subordinate monastic network with high liturgical standards — hundreds of dependent houses
- **Cistercians** (founded 1098, Cîteaux): Radical austerity — back to strict manual labor, plain architecture, no elaboration in liturgy. Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153) was their most famous voice; Cistercian expansion was rapid and their abbeys became significant agricultural and economic powers
- **Carthusians** (founded 1084): Extreme isolation — individual hermit cells within a community structure; perpetual silence; considered the strictest surviving order

### The mendicant orders
13th century saw a new model: friars (*fratres*) who took vows of poverty and begged (*mendicant* = begging), preached in cities rather than withdrawing from the world, and staffed universities.
- **Franciscans** (Order of Friars Minor, founded by Francis of Assisi, 1209): Radical poverty and simplicity; preaching; care for lepers. Francis himself was a genuine religious innovator — his embrace of poverty as a mystical program was distinctive. The order later split between *Spirituals* (rigorous on poverty) and *Conventuals* (moderate), generating serious controversy.
- **Dominicans** (Order of Preachers, founded by Dominic, 1216): Emphasis on intellectual formation and preaching against heresy. The Dominicans staffed the Inquisition and the universities. Thomas Aquinas was a Dominican.

## Scholasticism

### What it was
Scholasticism was the dominant intellectual method of the high medieval universities — applying rigorous logical analysis (Aristotelian logic, recovered through Arabic translations) to theological questions. The *quaestio* (question) and *disputatio* (formal debate) were the central forms.

### Key figures
**Anselm of Canterbury** (1033–1109): *Fides quaerens intellectum* — faith seeking understanding. His *Proslogion* contains the ontological argument for God's existence. Developed the satisfaction theory of atonement (Christ's death as satisfaction for the debt of sin owed to God's honor).

**Peter Abelard** (1079–1142): *Sic et Non* (Yes and No) — systematically assembled contradictory statements from church fathers on theological questions, demonstrating the need for rational resolution. Controversial figure; famous for his relationship with Heloise. His moral influence theory of atonement (Christ's death as moral exemplar rather than cosmic transaction) was condemned.

**Thomas Aquinas** (1225–1274): The greatest scholastic synthesizer. Dominican. His *Summa Theologiae* and *Summa Contra Gentiles* systematized Catholic theology by integrating Aristotelian philosophy with Christian doctrine. Natural theology (reason can demonstrate God's existence), the five ways, the distinction between grace and nature, the theology of virtues. After his death, Thomism became the semi-official Catholic philosophical tradition.

**Duns Scotus** (c. 1266–1308) and **William of Ockham** (c. 1287–1347): The later scholastics. Ockham's nominalism (universals are mental names, not real entities) and his razor (entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity) challenged the synthesis Aquinas had built and contributed to the gradual dissolution of high scholasticism.

## The Crusades
Nine major crusades (1096–1272) to recover the Holy Land from Muslim control, plus Northern Crusades against pagan Baltic peoples, and internal crusades against heretics (Albigensian Crusade).

**Religious logic**: Jerusalem was the holiest city; Muslim control was a provocation; crusading was framed as armed pilgrimage, with full indulgence (plenary remission of purgatorial punishment). Pope Urban II at Clermont (1095): "God wills it."

**First Crusade** (1096–1099): Took Jerusalem; massacred Jewish and Muslim populations; established crusader states.

**Fourth Crusade** (1202–1204): Diverted to sack Constantinople (a Christian city) instead of Egypt — catastrophic for Christian-Christian relations, feeding the schism.

**Results**: Failed to permanently hold the Holy Land. Significant cultural transmission (Greek texts through Arab scholars, luxury goods, plague pathways). Major demographic violence. Lasting damage to Jewish communities in Europe (pogroms accompanying crusade mobilization). The legacy of crusading shaped Christian-Muslim relations into the present.

## Heresy and the Inquisition

### The Inquisition
The Inquisition was not a single institution but a procedure: *inquisitio* (investigation) conducted by specially appointed papal or episcopal inquisitors to detect, try, and correct heresy. Established formally under Gregory IX (1231). Dominican and later Franciscan friars were the primary inquisitors.

Key features: written records, standard interrogation procedures, use of torture (authorized by *Ad extirpanda*, 1252), penalties ranging from penance to confiscation to imprisonment to death (handed over to secular authorities for burning). The Inquisition was in many regions *less* brutal than popular legend — but in certain times and places (13th-century Languedoc against the Cathars) it caused massive suffering.

### Catharism — see [[Catharism and the Albigensian Crusade]]
The most important medieval heresy. See dedicated note.

### Waldensians
Peter Waldo of Lyon (c. 1140–1218) founded a lay preaching movement emphasizing apostolic poverty and vernacular scripture. Initially tolerated; excommunicated 1184 when they refused to stop unauthorized preaching. Survived (as an ongoing persecuted minority) into the Reformation and still exist as a small Protestant church in Italy. Their emphasis on scripture over church authority anticipates the Reformation.

## Popular religion
Formal theology is only part of the picture. Medieval popular Christianity included:
- **Saints' cults**: Local saints with specific powers, honored at specific shrines; saints were intercessors, healers, protectors of trades and places
- **Relics**: Physical remains or objects associated with saints; miraculous power believed to inhere in them; major pilgrimage routes organized around relic collections
- **Pilgrimage**: Canterbury (Becket), Santiago de Compostela (James), Rome (apostles), Jerusalem. Walking as penance and devotion; transformative experience; also social disruption and opportunity
- **The liturgical year**: Advent, Christmas, Lent, Easter, Pentecost, the feasts of saints — structuring time itself
- **The church as sacred space**: The parish church as the center of community life; burial within the church or churchyard; the architectural theology of light in Gothic cathedrals

## Research notes
- The standard one-volume history: Diarmaid MacCulloch's *A History of Christianity* (2009)
- For popular religion specifically: Eamon Duffy's *The Stripping of the Altars* (English Reformation context but covers late medieval popular practice in extraordinary depth)
- R.I. Moore's *The Formation of a Persecuting Society* is essential for understanding heresy, Jews, and lepers in the high medieval period
- Don't assume medieval theology = contemporary Catholic theology; significant development occurred at Trent and after
