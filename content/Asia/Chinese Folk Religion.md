# Chinese Folk Religion

## Purpose
The actual lived religion of the majority of Chinese people — the syncretic, pragmatic, community-based religious world that doesn't fit neatly into "Confucianism," "Buddhism," or "Daoism" as philosophical categories. For the philosophical-institutional traditions, see [[Confucian Tradition]], [[Daoism Overview]], and [[Buddhism Overview]].

## The category problem
When Western scholars and imperial-era Chinese reformers described Chinese religion, they imposed the concept of discrete "religions" (like Christianity or Islam) with clear boundaries, creeds, and exclusive memberships. This framework fits Chinese religious life poorly.

Most Chinese people historically and today have:
- Venerated ancestors at home
- Visited Buddhist temples for certain purposes
- Visited Daoist temples for others
- Celebrated festivals tied to a shared cosmological calendar
- Consulted spirit mediums and diviners
- Burned incense at local deity shrines
- Done all of this without feeling any contradiction

The Chinese term for this reality: *sanjiao heyi* (三教合一) — "the three teachings are one." Confucianism, Buddhism, and Daoism are understood as complementary rather than exclusive. A person might be a Confucian at court, Buddhist at the temple, and Daoist in their garden — all simultaneously.

What scholars call "Chinese folk religion" or "popular religion" is the practice-level synthesis that most people actually live, distinct from the elite philosophical versions of any single tradition.

## The cosmological framework

### Heaven, Earth, and Humanity
The fundamental Chinese cosmological triad: *Tian* (Heaven), *Di* (Earth), and *Ren* (Humanity). These three are not separate realms but interpenetrating dimensions of a single reality. Human beings stand at the intersection of heaven and earth and have obligations to both.

### Qi and correspondence
*Qi* (vital force/energy/breath) — the fundamental stuff of the cosmos. Everything from stars to rocks to human bodies to social relationships is constituted by qi in different configurations. The quality and flow of qi determines health, fortune, and cosmic harmony.

*Correlative cosmology*: Everything in the universe corresponds to everything else through patterns of resonance. The five phases (*wuxing* — wood, fire, earth, metal, water) govern seasons, directions, organs, emotions, colors, musical notes, and more. Change in one domain resonates through all others. This is the theoretical foundation of Chinese medicine, feng shui, and divination.

### The Jade Emperor
The supreme ruler of the heavenly bureaucracy — *Yù Huáng Dà Dì* (Jade Emperor, August Supreme God). He governs a vast divine administration that mirrors the Chinese imperial bureaucracy: departments, officials, records, promotions, demotions, reports. The gods are not primarily creators or moral exemplars — they are cosmic administrators with specific jurisdictions.

This bureaucratic cosmology has important consequences: gods can be petitioned through proper procedure, promoted or demoted based on performance (there are historical records of emperors stripping gods of their titles for failing their duties), and replaced.

## The deity world

### Locality deities — Tu Di Gong
The *Tu Di Gong* (Earth God, Lord of the Soil) is the most local and common deity: one for each neighborhood, village, or area. He reports to higher bureaucratic deities about the people in his jurisdiction. His shrine is typically a small roadside or shop-corner altar — the most ubiquitous religious object in Chinese communities globally.

### The City God — Chéng Huáng
Every city has a *Chéng Huáng* — a deity who administers the city's supernatural affairs as the city magistrate administers its secular affairs. When people die, their souls first appear before the City God for preliminary judgment. The City God's birthday was a major annual festival.

### Mazu (Tianhou) — the sea goddess
*Mazu* (or *Tianhou*, Empress of Heaven) began as a historical woman (Lin Mo, 960–987 CE, Fujian province) credited with supernatural power to protect sailors. Deified after death, promoted through the imperial bureaucracy to goddess of the sea. Now the patron of sailors, fishermen, and coastal communities throughout southern China, Taiwan, and Southeast Asia. Her temples number in the thousands globally; the Mazu pilgrimage from Taiwan to Meizhou (her birthplace) is one of the world's largest annual religious pilgrimages.

This pattern — historical human → local deity → promoted through the imperial-cosmological system to a major goddess — is characteristic of Chinese popular religion. The cosmos is open to new entrants.

### Guan Yu (Guandi) — the war god
A historical military general of the Three Kingdoms period (3rd century CE), deified for his virtue, loyalty, and martial skill. He is simultaneously: the God of War, the God of Brotherhood and Sworn Oaths, and the God of Business (merchants honor his loyalty and integrity). Venerated by military, police, triads (criminal brotherhoods), Daoist temples, and Buddhist establishments simultaneously. One of the most universally revered figures in Chinese religion.

### Guanyin — Bodhisattva of Compassion
The most popular deity in the Chinese religious world is technically Buddhist: *Guanyin* (*Avalokiteshvara* in Sanskrit, *Kannon* in Japanese) — the Bodhisattva of Compassion, increasingly depicted as female from the Tang dynasty onward. She hears the cries of the suffering and responds with compassion; she is associated with mercy, healing, fertility, and protection.

Guanyin is venerated across Buddhist, Daoist, and folk contexts — the boundaries mean nothing at the popular level. She is one of the clearest examples of the fluid Chinese religious synthesis.

### The Eight Immortals
Eight legendary figures who achieved immortality through Daoist cultivation — each with a distinctive appearance, attribute, and story. They represent different social types (the scholar, the warrior, the musician, the beggar, the female immortal, etc.) and appear on everything from fine art to kitchen objects to playing cards. They are figures of popular imagination as much as religious doctrine.

## Ancestor veneration — the ritual spine
The consistent core across all levels of Chinese religion is ancestor veneration — the ongoing relationship between the living and their dead kin.

**The ancestor hall** (*citang*): A dedicated space in the home or community building for ancestral tablets (wooden plaques inscribed with the ancestors' names). Offerings of food, incense, and paper goods are made regularly.

**Ghost Festival** (*Zhōngyuán Festival*, 7th lunar month): The gates of hell open and hungry ghosts roam freely. Living families make offerings to appease both their own ancestors and the potentially dangerous unattached ghosts. Paper money, paper goods (houses, cars, phones — contemporary additions), and food are burned to transmit them to the dead.

**Qingming** (Clear and Bright, early April): The tomb-sweeping festival — families visit ancestral graves, clean them, make offerings, burn paper goods. One of the most universally observed Chinese rituals, still practiced by non-religious or atheist Chinese families.

**The logic**: The dead remain part of the family and the community. They require maintenance — offerings, respect, inclusion — and in return they provide blessing and protection. Neglected ancestors become troublesome.

## Divination and spirit mediums

### The I Ching (*Yijing*)
The *Book of Changes* — a divination manual and cosmological text. 64 hexagrams (arrangements of six broken or unbroken lines), each with associated judgments and commentaries. Used to read the pattern of change in a situation and determine the appropriate response. The oldest continuous divinatory tradition in China (Shang dynasty oracle bones are its precursor).

The I Ching became one of the most globally influential Chinese texts — absorbed into Confucianism, Daoism, Western esotericism (Leibniz saw binary arithmetic in it), and modern psychological use (Jung wrote the foreword to Richard Wilhelm's German translation).

### Fortune sticks and temple divination
The most common divination method in Chinese temples: shake a canister of numbered sticks (*qian*) until one falls out, then take the corresponding paper with a poetic prophecy. Simple, accessible, and still widely practiced.

### Spirit mediums (*jitong* / *tangki*)
Possessed spirit mediums who speak for deities — particularly common in Taiwan and Southeast Asian Chinese communities. The medium is possessed by a deity (often a local god or a famous historical figure) who gives advice, heals, or adjudicates disputes. The possessed medium may engage in self-mortification (piercing cheeks, back, etc. with skewers) as a demonstration of the deity's presence.

## The death ritual complex
Chinese death ritual is among the most elaborate and important in the tradition:
- **Professional ritual specialists**: Priests (Daoist or Buddhist) hired to perform the correct rites
- **Proper timing**: Auspicious burial times calculated by geomancers
- **Geomancy** (*feng shui*): The burial location must be correctly positioned to channel good qi toward the descendants. Poor feng shui of an ancestor's grave can harm the entire family line.
- **Paper goods**: The dead need the equivalent of everything the living need — burned to transmit them
- **Mourning periods**: Specific mourning durations and clothing requirements

## Chinese religion under Communism
The Cultural Revolution (1966–1976) attempted to destroy all religious practice — temples destroyed, clergy persecuted, practices banned. The suppression was not total (underground practice continued) and has been followed by significant religious revival since the 1980s. Temple reconstruction, religious tourism, and popular practice have all expanded dramatically.

The Chinese state currently manages religion through registered associations for five official religions (Buddhism, Catholicism, Daoism, Islam, Protestantism). Folk religion exists in a gray zone — tolerated locally, sometimes targeted when politically inconvenient.

## Research notes
- The category "folk religion" carries an implicit hierarchy (folk = lower than elite); "popular religion" is preferred by many scholars
- C.K. Yang's *Religion in Chinese Society* (1961) introduced the distinction between "institutional religion" and "diffused religion" — still useful
- The boundary between Daoism and folk religion is often impossible to draw — Daoist priests have staffed popular temples for centuries
- The *Investiture of the Gods* (*Fengshen Yanyi*, 16th century) and *Journey to the West* (*Xiyouji*) are the two most important literary-mythological texts for understanding the popular divine world
