# Jainism

## Purpose
One of the oldest surviving independent Indian religious traditions — with a distinctive metaphysics, extreme ascetic ideal, and profound influence on Indian philosophy and practice (especially on Gandhi's concept of nonviolence). For the Indian religious context, see [[Hindu Traditions Overview]] and [[Buddhism Overview]].

## What Jainism is
Jainism is an ancient Indian religion that rejects the authority of the Vedas (making it *nastika* — non-orthodox — alongside Buddhism), posits an eternal universe without a creator god, and centers its soteriology on the liberation of the individual soul (*jiva*) from the accumulation of karma through radical nonviolence (*ahimsa*) and extreme ascetic self-purification.

It has approximately 4–6 million adherents today, concentrated in India (especially Gujarat, Rajasthan, and Maharashtra), with diaspora communities globally. Despite its small size, Jainism has had outsized influence on Indian culture, philosophy, and business communities.

## Origins and the Tirthankaras

### The Tirthankaras
Jainism holds that the universe is eternal and periodically produces fully awakened teachers (*Tirthankaras*, "ford-makers" — those who build a crossing from the realm of suffering to liberation). In the current cosmic cycle there have been 24 Tirthankaras.

**Rishabhadeva (Adinatha)**: The first Tirthankara of the current cycle — considered the founding figure of civilization itself in Jain mythology; prehistoric in Jain chronology.

**Parshvanatha**: The 23rd Tirthankara, historically plausible (possibly c. 877–777 BCE); worshipped widely; his tradition had four vows that Mahavira later expanded to five.

**Mahavira** (c. 599–527 BCE): The 24th and most recent Tirthankara — the historical founder of the tradition as it currently exists. A contemporary of the Buddha (possibly; dating is debated). Born a Kshatriya prince, he renounced his household at 30, practiced extreme austerities for 12 years, achieved *kevala jnana* (omniscient knowledge), taught for 30 years, and died by *sallekhanā* (voluntary fasting unto death). His teachings form the core of Jain scripture.

## Core metaphysics

### Jiva and Ajiva
The universe consists of two eternal, uncreated categories:
- **Jiva** (soul/living substance): Conscious, eternal; every living being from bacteria to humans to gods possesses a jiva. Jivas are infinite in number and are all fundamentally equal in their capacity for liberation.
- **Ajiva** (non-soul): Everything else — matter (*pudgala*), space (*akasha*), time (*kala*), motion (*dharma*), rest (*adharma*)

There is no creator god. The universe is self-sustaining through the interactions of jiva and ajiva.

### Karma as physical substance
Jainism's most distinctive philosophical position: karma is not merely a causal law or an ethical principle — it is a material substance. Karmic particles (*karman*) literally attach to the soul through activity (thought, speech, action), binding it to material existence and weighing it down. The accumulated karma obscures the soul's natural qualities of infinite knowledge, infinite bliss, and infinite energy.

Liberation (*moksha*) requires: stopping the influx of new karma (*samvara*) + burning off accumulated karma through ascetic practice (*nirjara*).

### The soul's qualities and liberation
A liberated soul (*siddha*) rises to the apex of the cosmos (*siddhashila*) and remains there in eternal, motionless, blissful omniscience — free from birth, death, and karmic entanglement. It does not merge into a universal soul (as in some Hindu schools) and it does not cycle back to help others (unlike the Bodhisattva in Mahayana Buddhism). It simply *is*, perfectly and completely.

## Ethics and the Five Vows

### Ahimsa (nonviolence)
The supreme principle — avoiding harm to any living being. Jains recognize souls in all life forms, including microorganisms (*nigodas* — the smallest jivas). This produces the most extreme ahimsa ethic in world religion:
- Strict vegetarianism (often veganism)
- Avoiding root vegetables (pulling out a root kills the plant-soul)
- Some monks sweep the ground before walking to avoid stepping on insects
- Some wear a cloth (*muhapatti*) over the mouth to avoid inhaling microorganisms
- Avoidance of farming (disturbs the earth and its inhabitants) — one reason Jains historically concentrated in trade and finance

### The Five Great Vows (for monks/nuns)
1. **Ahimsa**: complete nonviolence
2. **Satya**: truth
3. **Asteya**: non-stealing
4. **Brahmacharya**: celibacy
5. **Aparigraha**: non-attachment/non-possession

Lay Jains take modified versions of these vows (*anuvratas*, "small vows") allowing marriage, property, and a more ordinary life, while practicing ahimsa and other virtues within reasonable limits.

## The two major sects

### Digambara ("Sky-clad")
Digambara monks reject all possessions including clothing — they are naked, "clothed by the sky." The logic: if a monk possesses even a cloth, he is attached; true renunciation requires nothing. Digambaras also hold that women cannot attain liberation in their current body (they must be reborn as men first) and that no original Jain scriptures survive.

### Shvetambara ("White-clad")
Shvetambara monks and nuns wear white robes. They hold that women can achieve liberation in female form and that their scriptural canon (*Agamas*) preserves genuine Mahavira teachings, albeit with some corruption.

The split is ancient — traditional dating around 300 BCE, though this is debated. Both traditions agree on the core metaphysics and ethics; the differences are primarily about monastic practice and scripture.

## Sallekhanā — voluntary death by fasting
One of the most distinctive Jain practices. When a person (monk or layperson) determines that death is inevitable and near, they may choose to stop eating and drinking in a gradual, deliberate, serene withdrawal from life. This is not considered suicide (which implies passion and harm) but rather the ultimate act of nonviolence toward one's own body — a conscious, peaceful departure. It is practiced to this day.

## Jain philosophy

### Anekantavada (many-sidedness)
One of the most sophisticated philosophical doctrines in world religion: reality is infinitely complex and can only be partially described from any single viewpoint. No single statement exhausts reality. This implies:
- **Syadvada** (conditional predication): All assertions should be qualified — "in some respect, X is; in some respect, X is not; in some respect, X both is and is not..." etc. (seven-fold logic)
- **Nayavada** (doctrine of partial standpoints): A *naya* (standpoint) grasps one aspect of reality; no naya is the whole truth

This is a systematic philosophical pluralism — reality is too complex for any single perspective to capture completely. This has been applied both to metaphysics and to religious tolerance: other religions are partially right from their standpoints.

Anekantavada's influence on Gandhi's concept of *sarvodaya* (uplift of all) and his insistence on seeing truth from multiple perspectives is significant.

## Jainism and Gandhi
Mahatma Gandhi grew up in Gujarat surrounded by Jain culture and was profoundly influenced by:
- *Ahimsa* — the political weapon of nonviolent resistance derives its philosophical depth from Jain ethics
- *Aparigraha* (non-possession) — his experiments with simple living
- The Jain merchant and activist Raychandbhai Mehta, who was Gandhi's spiritual mentor in his early life

Gandhi's concept of *satyagraha* (truth-force) is not Jain in origin, but the nonviolence framework is substantially Jain-influenced.

## Sacred texts
The Jain scriptural canon is disputed between the two sects:

**Shvetambara Agamas**: 45 texts (12 *Angas*, supplementary texts) in Ardhamagadhi Prakrit, considered authentic transmissions of Mahavira's teachings. The 12th Anga (*Drishtivaada*) is considered lost.

**Digambara texts**: Reject the Shvetambara Agamas as corrupted; rely on later commentarial texts (*Karma Shastras*, Kundakunda's works) as authoritative.

**Important texts**:
- *Acaranga Sutra*: Oldest Jain text; describes Mahavira's ascetic life
- *Tattvartha Sutra* (Umasvati, c. 2nd–5th century CE): The most systematic philosophical statement of Jainism, accepted by both sects
- Works of Kundakunda (Digambara, c. 2nd–8th century CE) — especially *Samayasara* (Soul-Essence)

## Jain art and architecture
Jain temple architecture is among the most elaborate in India:
- **Dilwara temples** (Mount Abu, Rajasthan, 11th–13th century): White marble carving of extraordinary density and refinement
- **Ranakpur temples**: Also white marble; extraordinary carved columns
- **Monolithic Bahubali statue** (Gomateshvara, Shravanabelagola, Karnataka, 10th century): 57 feet tall, carved from a single rock; one of the largest stone statues in the world; site of the *Mahamastakabhisheka* (head-anointing ceremony held every 12 years)

## Research notes
- Jainism is sometimes described as a reform movement within Hinduism — this is incorrect. It is an independent tradition with distinct metaphysics (no creator god, karma as physical substance, no Vedic authority)
- The Jain influence on Indian thought is disproportionate to its numbers — Jain philosophers significantly shaped both Hindu and Buddhist philosophical development
- Paul Dundas's *The Jains* is the standard academic overview in English
- The contemporary Jain business community (especially in Gujarat and the UK/US diaspora) tends toward Shvetambara tradition; their philanthropic culture reflects *aparigraha* ideals
