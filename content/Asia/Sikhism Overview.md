# Sikhism Overview

## Scope
Sikhism is a monotheistic religious tradition rooted in the Punjab region, centered on devotion to one God, the teachings of the Gurus, disciplined ethical life, sacred scripture, communal equality, and the fusion of spiritual practice with worldly responsibility.

## Why it matters
Sikhism matters because it is not simply a blend of Hindu and Islamic elements, even though it emerges in a world shaped by both.
It develops as a distinct tradition with:
- strong scriptural and musical foundations
- a disciplined communal identity
- anti-caste and service-oriented ethical ideals
- a history shaped by devotion, sovereignty, persecution, and institutional formation

## Historical frame
### Guru Nanak and the beginning
- **1469 CE** — birth of **Guru Nanak**
- Guru Nanak's teaching emphasizes devotion to one God, rejection of empty ritualism, critique of ego and social division, and the importance of truthful living, remembrance, and humility

### The line of Gurus
A line of **Ten Gurus** shapes the tradition through teaching, community formation, scripture, organization, and resistance.

### Scriptural consolidation
- **1604 CE** — compilation of the **Adi Granth** in early form under Guru Arjan

### Khalsa formation
- **1699 CE** — founding of the **Khalsa** under Guru Gobind Singh, giving the tradition sharper corporate discipline and visible identity

### Scriptural Guruship
- **1708 CE** — the **Guru Granth Sahib** is affirmed as the enduring Guru in scriptural form after the line of human Gurus

## Core beliefs
- one God
- devotion through remembrance of the divine Name
- rejection of hollow ritualism and inherited hierarchy as spiritually sufficient
- emphasis on ethical action, service, honesty, courage, and communal discipline
- balance between spiritual life and worldly responsibility rather than renunciation of the world as the only holy path

## Scripture and sacred authority
### Guru Granth Sahib
The Guru Granth Sahib is not treated merely as a book of advice.
It functions as living scriptural authority and occupies a central place in Sikh ritual and communal life.

### Why this matters
Scripture, music, recitation, and devotion are tightly linked.
This is a tradition where sung scripture and community practice matter profoundly.

## Key institutions and practices
### Gurdwara
The gurdwara is not just a worship hall.
It is a center of prayer, scripture, hospitality, and community life.

### Langar
The communal kitchen is one of the clearest embodiments of Sikh ethics:
- service
- equality
- shared human dignity
- refusal of social superiority through food hierarchy

### Khalsa discipline
Initiated Sikh identity is especially shaped by the Khalsa tradition, which emphasizes discipline, courage, responsibility, and visible markers of belonging.

### Worship and devotion
- prayer
- hymn singing (*kirtan*)
- scriptural recitation
- remembrance of the divine Name
- service (*seva*)

## Important symbols
The **Five Ks** are major identity markers for initiated Khalsa Sikhs:
- **Kesh**
- **Kara**
- **Kanga**
- **Kachera**
- **Kirpan**

These are not random accessories. They are identity, discipline, and commitment embodied.

## Historical pressure and self-understanding
Sikh history includes devotion and institution-building, but also political struggle, persecution, militarization in response to threat, and debates about authority, sovereignty, and colonial modernity.
That means Sikhism should not be reduced either to peaceful devotion alone or martial identity alone.
Both are historically important.

## Why it matters comparatively
Sikhism is a strong case for studying:
- scripture as living authority
- equality enacted materially through shared practice
- religion and political struggle
- visible disciplined identity
- devotion without monastic withdrawal as the main ideal

## Recommended resources
- W. H. McLeod — Sikh studies
- Nikky-Guninder Kaur Singh — Sikh thought and history
- Pashaura Singh — Sikh scripture and tradition
- academic introductions to Sikh history, the Gurus, and the Khalsa

## Good working summary
Sikhism is a disciplined devotional tradition centered on one God, scripture, service, equality, and moral courage. It emerges in dialogue with the religious world around it, but becomes fully its own tradition with a distinctive community, authority structure, and sacred identity.
