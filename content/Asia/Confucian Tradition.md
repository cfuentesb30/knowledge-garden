# Confucian Tradition

## Purpose
Confucianism as a religious-ethical-political system — not merely a philosophy. Covers its origins, core concepts, ritual dimensions, state-religion relationship, Neo-Confucian development, and interactions with Buddhism and Daoism. For the Chinese religious context, see [[Daoism Overview]] and [[Buddhism Overview]].

## What Confucianism is (and isn't)
Western framing tends to call Confucianism a philosophy or ethics system, not a religion. This is too narrow. Confucianism involves:
- Ritual practice with cosmological significance
- Ancestor veneration as a living religious obligation
- A theory of sacred kingship and cosmic-social correspondence
- An account of human nature with something like a soteriological goal (self-cultivation toward sagehood)
- Institutions (academies, examination systems, shrines) with quasi-religious functions

It lacks: a personal creator god, otherworldly salvation, priestly hierarchy, or revealed scripture in the usual sense. But dismissing it as "mere ethics" misses its functional and structural role in Chinese civilization.

## Confucius (Kong Qiu, 551–479 BCE)

### Who he was
Kongzi ("Master Kong" — Latinized as Confucius by Jesuit missionaries) was a minor official in the state of Lu during the Spring and Autumn Period, a time of political fragmentation and social disorder in Zhou-dynasty China. He traveled between states seeking a ruler who would implement his vision of governance, found none willing, and spent his last years teaching. He claimed not to be inventing but *transmitting* — preserving the wisdom of the Zhou founding period (the Duke of Zhou being his model statesman).

His teaching was preserved by his disciples in the *Analects* (*Lunyu*) — a collection of brief dialogues and sayings compiled after his death.

### His project
Confucius diagnosed the political disorder of his era as a failure of *zhengming* (rectification of names) — roles and titles had become detached from their actual meanings. If a ruler does not rule, a minister does not minister, a father does not father — society decays. The solution: restore the proper correspondence between names/roles and actual conduct.

This is not merely formal. For Confucius, the cultivation of virtue (*de*) and the proper performance of ritual (*li*) were inseparable and mutually reinforcing.

## Core concepts

### Ren (仁) — Humaneness/Benevolence
The central virtue. *Ren* is often translated as humaneness, benevolence, love, or goodness. It is both the source of all other virtues and the quality that makes one fully human. When asked to define it, Confucius gave different answers in different contexts — it is not a formula but a living quality expressed differently in each relationship and situation.

One formulation: "Do not impose on others what you yourself do not want" (*Analects* 12.2) — the Confucian "golden rule."

*Ren* is relational — it exists between people, not as an individual property. You cannot be *ren* alone.

### Li (禮) — Ritual/Propriety
*Li* encompasses: ritual practice, proper ceremony, social etiquette, the forms of appropriate behavior in all relationships. It is not mere formalism — ritual performed with inner *ren* and outer *li* together constitutes the full human moral act.

*Li* includes: sacrificial rites for ancestors and spirits, court ceremony, funerary rites, coming-of-age ceremonies, music and dance forms, the proper forms of address, gift-giving, and mourning. All social interaction has a ritual dimension.

The key insight: form shapes inner experience. Regular practice of the right forms cultivates the right inner states. This is the Confucian equivalent of contemplative practice in other traditions — except that it is embedded in ordinary social life rather than removed from it.

### Yi (義) — Rightness/Righteousness
Doing what is right in a situation — the moral appropriateness of an action. Distinguished from profit (*li*): the *junzi* (exemplary person) acts from rightness, not calculation of benefit.

### Zhengming (正名) — Rectification of Names
Names must correspond to reality: "Let the ruler be ruler, the minister be minister, the father be father, the son be son." Social and political order depends on roles being genuinely inhabited.

### Junzi (君子) — The Exemplary Person
Originally "son of a ruler" — an aristocratic title. Confucius transformed it into an ethical category: the person who has cultivated virtue, practices ritual properly, and exemplifies the human ideal. Not a spiritual savior but a model of lived ethical excellence. The Confucian goal is to become a *junzi*, or in the most developed form, a sage (*sheng*).

## The Five Relationships
Confucian social ethics is organized around five primary relationships, each with specific obligations:
1. **Ruler–minister**: Ruler governs benevolently; minister serves loyally
2. **Parent–child**: Parent nurtures; child practices *xiao* (filial piety)
3. **Husband–wife**: Mutual role-fulfillment; historically stratified
4. **Elder–younger sibling**: Elder guides; younger respects
5. **Friend–friend**: The one equal relationship — mutual faithfulness

These are not one-directional. The *superior* in each pair has obligations of benevolent care; the *inferior* has obligations of respect and service. Confucius was clear: a minister who only obeys a bad ruler is not loyal — true loyalty requires remonstration.

### Xiao (孝) — Filial Piety
The specific form of *ren* directed at parents and ancestors. Often described as the root of all virtue. In practice: caring for parents, mourning them properly when they die, performing ancestral rites, not shaming the family name. Ancestor veneration is not merely custom — it is a moral-religious obligation.

## Ritual and cosmology

### Heaven (Tian 天)
Heaven in Confucian thought is not a personal deity but a moral cosmic order — the ultimate ground of ethical reality. "Heaven knows me" means: moral reality itself witnesses one's inner states. The ruler's authority derives from the *Mandate of Heaven* (*Tianming*): Heaven endorses virtuous rule; catastrophe (flood, famine, rebellion) signals Heaven's withdrawal of the mandate.

This is a quasi-religious political theology: natural disasters and political upheaval are read as cosmic responses to moral failure.

### Ancestor veneration as practice
Ancestral rites — offerings of food, incense, and ritual objects at domestic shrines and grave sites — are central Confucian practice. The dead remain morally present and communally connected; honoring them is both ethical obligation and cosmological maintenance. The *junzi*'s mourning for parents follows specific prescribed forms and durations (three years of formal mourning for parents — though Confucius acknowledged this might be shortened in practice).

### The sage-king model
Confucius idealized the founding kings of the Zhou dynasty (especially King Wen, King Wu, and the Duke of Zhou) as embodiments of proper governance through virtue and ritual. The sage-king rules not through coercion but through the *de* (power/virtue) that his personal cultivation radiates — transforming society the way the pole star orders the heavens without moving.

## Mencius and Xunzi — the great debate

### Mencius (Mengzi, 372–289 BCE)
Developed Confucianism in an optimistic direction: human nature is originally good (*xing shan*). The four sprouts of virtue (compassion, shame, deference, moral judgment) are innate. Moral failure comes from neglect of cultivation, not from original depravity. Political implication: a ruler who does not govern benevolently loses the Mandate of Heaven and may legitimately be overthrown — even killed (tyrannicide is justified).

### Xunzi (c. 310–235 BCE)
Developed Confucianism in a realist direction: human nature is originally *bad* (*xing e*) — not evil but chaotic, driven by desire without moral direction. Virtue must be acquired through education, ritual, and social formation — it is not innate. *Li* (ritual) is not an expression of pre-existing virtue but the mechanism by which virtue is constructed.

The Mencius-Xunzi debate — human nature as originally good vs. originally in need of correction — structures much of later Confucian thought.

## Neo-Confucianism (Song dynasty, 960–1279 CE onward)

### Why it arose
Buddhism and Daoism had dominated Chinese intellectual and spiritual life for centuries. Neo-Confucian thinkers responded by constructing a Confucian metaphysics — an account of ultimate reality, human nature, and cosmic order — that could answer the questions Buddhism had raised about mind, reality, and liberation.

### Key figures
**Cheng Yi and Cheng Hao** (11th century): Introduced *li* (principle/pattern — different character from ritual *li*) as the cosmic ordering principle inherent in all things and in human nature.

**Zhu Xi** (1130–1200): The greatest synthesizer of Neo-Confucianism. His system: *li* (principle) and *qi* (material force) together constitute all things. Human nature is originally good because it embodies *li*; moral failure comes from the distortions of *qi*. Moral cultivation: *gewu* (investigation of things) + *zhizhi* (extension of knowledge) — careful study of patterns in the world and in the classics, together with inner cultivation (*jingzuo*, quiet sitting). Zhu Xi's commentary on the *Four Books* became the standard curriculum for the imperial civil examinations until 1905.

**Wang Yangming** (1472–1529): Major alternative to Zhu Xi. *Liangzhi* (innate moral knowledge) — every person already knows what is right; the problem is not knowledge but action-knowledge unity. "To know and not to act is not yet to know." His method: extension of innate knowledge through inner cultivation rather than external investigation.

## Confucianism and the state

### The examination system
The imperial civil examination system (*keju*, fully developed Tang dynasty onward, formally ending 1905) required mastery of the Confucian classics (*Four Books*, *Five Classics*) and the ability to compose essays on them. This was the primary path to official office for a thousand years. It created a scholar-official class (*shi*) whose shared formation was Confucian — making Confucianism the de facto state ideology and elite educational framework for most of Chinese history.

### The temple-shrine system
Confucian temples (*Wenmiao*) were established in every administrative city. Regular sacrifices were offered to Confucius and to major Confucian sages. This is not "merely symbolic" — it is a ritual system with cosmic significance, understood as maintaining the correct order of the realm.

## The Four Books and Five Classics

### Four Books (Zhu Xi's canonical core)
1. *Analects* (*Lunyu*) — Confucius
2. *Mencius* (*Mengzi*)
3. *Great Learning* (*Daxue*)
4. *Doctrine of the Mean* (*Zhongyong*)

### Five Classics (older canon)
1. *Book of Changes* (*I Ching / Yijing*)
2. *Book of Documents* (*Shujing*)
3. *Book of Songs* (*Shijing*)
4. *Record of Rites* (*Liji*)
5. *Spring and Autumn Annals* (*Chunqiu*)

## Confucianism today
Confucian influence persists through:
- East Asian family ethics and ancestor veneration
- Educational culture in China, Japan, Korea, Vietnam
- The "Confucian capitalism" debate (whether Confucian values explain East Asian economic development)
- Contemporary New Confucianism (*Xiandai Rujia*) — a philosophical movement attempting to synthesize Confucianism with modernity and democracy
- State Confucianism in contemporary China (Xi Jinping has drawn explicitly on Confucian imagery for "harmonious society" ideology)

## Research notes
- Confucianism should not be read as pure philosophy detached from ritual and practice — that reading produces a partial and distorted picture
- The relationship between Confucianism and Chinese popular religion is complex: Confucianism provided the elite framework; popular religion operated at a different register, often blending Confucian, Buddhist, and Daoist elements
- Tu Weiming is the most important contemporary English-language interpreter of Neo-Confucianism and spiritual Confucianism
