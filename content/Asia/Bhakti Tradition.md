# Bhakti Tradition

## Purpose
Map the bhakti (devotional) tradition in Hinduism: its theological foundations, the poet-saint movements, the major Vaishnava and Shaiva streams, and how modern global Vaishnavism (ISKCON) relates to historical bhakti.

---

## What bhakti is

**Bhakti** (Sanskrit: "devotion," "participation," "sharing") refers to the path of loving devotion to a personal deity as a means of liberation (moksha) and/or blissful union (bhava). It is one of the three classical Hindu paths to liberation:
- **Jnana marga** — the path of knowledge (Advaita, philosophical inquiry)
- **Karma marga** — the path of right action (duty, ritual)
- **Bhakti marga** — the path of devotion

Bhakti is not simply emotional religion as opposed to intellectual religion. The major bhakti theologians (Ramanuja, Madhva, Chaitanya) developed sophisticated metaphysical systems that justify the devotional path philosophically.

The defining feature: **the divine is personal**. God (Vishnu/Krishna, Shiva, the Goddess) is not a formless absolute but a being who loves and can be loved; relationship (seva, service; bhava, emotional state; puja, worship) is the medium of liberation.

## Origins and the Bhakti Sutras

Devotional elements are present in the Vedic tradition (Rigveda hymns addressing Varuna with personal love), but bhakti as a self-conscious path emerges clearly in:

- **The Bhagavad Gita** (c. 200 BCE–200 CE) — Krishna reveals himself as the supreme personal God; Chapter 12 presents bhakti as the highest path
- **The Narada Bhakti Sutras** — attributed to the divine sage Narada; defines bhakti as pure love (para prema) for God; distinguishes different flavors of devotional relationship
- **The Sandilya Bhakti Sutras** — another early systematization

The Bhagavatam (Srimad Bhagavatam / Bhagavata Purana, c. 9th–10th century CE) becomes the central text of Vaishnava bhakti, especially its 10th book narrating Krishna's life in Vrindavan and Mathura.

## The Tamil Alvars and Nayanmars

The earliest major bhakti poet-saints emerge in Tamil Nadu, South India, from roughly the 6th to 9th centuries CE.

**Alvars** (literally "those immersed in God") — 12 Vaishnava poet-saints. Their devotional Tamil poems are collected as the **Nalayira Divya Prabandham** (Four Thousand Divine Verses), sometimes called the "Tamil Veda" — a provocative claim that vernacular devotional poetry carries the authority of Sanskrit scripture.

Notable Alvars:
- **Andal** — the only female Alvar; her Tiruppavai and Nachiyar Tirumozhi are still sung today; she is said to have married Vishnu himself
- **Nammalvar** — considered the greatest Alvar; his Tiruvaymozhi is the crown of the collection

**Nayanmars** — 63 Shaiva Tamil poet-saints. Their Tamil hymns collected as the **Tevaram**. Key figures: Appar, Sambandar, Sundarar, and Manikkavasagar (Tiruvasagam).

What these movements share:
- rejection of caste hierarchy in the space of devotion ("I am lower than a pariah's dog" — direct devotion makes caste irrelevant)
- vernacular Tamil as a sacred language alongside Sanskrit
- pilgrimage to temple sites; the temple as the sacred meeting point with the divine
- intense personal love relationship with the deity, using erotic poetry (bridal mysticism) in many compositions

## Ramanuja and Vishishtadvaita

**Ramanuja** (1017–1137) — the greatest theologian of the Vaishnava bhakti tradition. His philosophical position: **Vishishtadvaita** (qualified non-dualism).

Against Shankara's Advaita (world as maya, Brahman as formless): Ramanuja insists:
- Brahman (= Vishnu/Narayana) is fully real and personal
- Individual souls (jiva) and the world (jagat) are real — they are the body of Brahman, not illusions
- Liberation (moksha) is eternal service and loving communion with Vishnu in the highest realm (Vaikuntham), not dissolution into formless oneness
- Bhakti is the highest path — not inferior to jnana but its fulfillment

Ramanuja's commentary on the Brahma Sutras (Sri Bhashya) is the philosophical foundation of Sri Vaishnavism. The Sri Vaishnava tradition (Shrirangam temple complex is its heart) remains one of the most influential Hindu traditions in South India.

---

## Madhva and Dvaita

**Madhvacharya** (1238–1317) — even more radically theistic. Position: **Dvaita** (dualism).

- God (Vishnu) and souls/world are permanently distinct — they never merge or become one
- Liberation is eternal blissful service to Vishnu in Vaikuntha, but the souls remain distinct beings
- Vishnu's grace is necessary — human effort alone cannot achieve liberation
- More explicit predestination: souls are divided into those destined for liberation, those for eternal service in a lower realm, and those for eternal damnation (a position unusual in Hindu philosophy)

Madhva's tradition continues in the **Udupi tradition** (Karnataka).

## North Indian bhakti: the Sant tradition

From the 12th–17th centuries, an explosion of bhakti devotion sweeps North India, partly as a response to the disruption of the Delhi Sultanate and the energy of popular religious renewal.

**Sant tradition** — poet-saints who often transcend sectarian boundaries:
- **Kabir** (c. 1440–1518) — weaver-saint; attacks both brahminical Hinduism and Muslim orthodoxy; insists God (Rama/Rahim) is one and found in the inner heart, not in temples or mosques; his verses are in the Guru Granth Sahib and the Bijak; deeply influential on Sikhism
- **Mirabai** (c. 1498–1546) — Rajput princess-saint; devoted to Krishna as her divine husband; renounced her human marriage; her bhajans are among the most beloved in Hinduism
- **Tukaram** (c. 1608–1649) — Maharashtrian bhakti; Vithoba (a form of Vishnu at Pandharpur) as his deity; Varkari pilgrimage tradition
- **Surdas** — blind poet-saint; compositions on Krishna's Vrindavan life
- **Tulsidas** (1532–1623) — composed the **Ramcharitmanas** (Hindi version of the Ramayana); defines Rama devotion in North India to the present day

**The Warkari movement** (Maharashtra) — annual pilgrimage to Pandharpur on foot; Tukaram and Dnyaneshwar as patron saints; major living tradition.

## Chaitanya and Gaudiya Vaishnavism

**Chaitanya Mahaprabhu** (1486–1534) — the pivotal figure for understanding where ISKCON comes from. Bengali Vaishnava mystic; said to be the joint incarnation of Radha and Krishna in one body.

His distinctive teaching: **Achintya Bhedabheda** (inconceivable difference-in-nondifference) — Brahman and the world are simultaneously identical and different; neither pure non-dualism nor pure dualism; the paradox is held as unresolvable.

More importantly, Chaitanya's practice: **nama sankirtana** — congregational chanting of divine names (the Hare Krishna mantra: Hare Krishna, Hare Krishna, Krishna Krishna, Hare Hare / Hare Rama, Hare Rama, Rama Rama, Hare Hare). He is said to have danced through the streets of Navadvipa chanting, drawing crowds.

**The six Gosvamis** — Chaitanya's principal disciples who systematized his theology at Vrindavan: Rupa Gosvami, Sanatana Gosvami, Jiva Gosvami, Gopala Bhatta Gosvami, Raghunatha Bhatta Gosvami, Raghunatha Dasa Gosvami.

**Rasa theory** — Rupa Gosvami elaborated a system of devotional aesthetic states (rasa / bhava) based on different relationships with Krishna:
- **Shanta** — peaceful, calm
- **Dasya** — servitude (Hanuman archetype)
- **Sakhya** — friendship (Krishna's cowherd friends)
- **Vatsalya** — parental love (Yashoda and Krishna)
- **Madhurya/Shringara** — romantic love, the highest; Radha and the gopis as the paradigm

The love of Radha for Krishna is the theological summit — erotic devotion as the highest form of divine relationship, more intense and intimate than any other. This is not metaphor: Gaudiya theology treats Radha's love for Krishna as the actual nature of ultimate reality.

## ISKCON (International Society for Krishna Consciousness)

**A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada** (1896–1977) founded ISKCON in New York in 1966. He came from the Gaudiya Vaishnava lineage traceable to Chaitanya through the Gosvamis and Bhaktisiddhanta Sarasvati.

ISKCON's innovations and decisions:
- brought Gaudiya Vaishnavism to the West intact — not a simplified or "universal" version
- insisted on strict rules: four regulative principles (no meat, no illicit sex, no intoxication, no gambling), 16 rounds of japa (mantra beads) daily
- street chanting (sankirtana) as both practice and proselytism — highly visible in Western cities in the 1960s–70s
- communal ashram living as the primary structure for Western converts
- translated and commented on vast amounts of Sanskrit text (Bhagavad Gita As It Is, multi-volume Srimad Bhagavatam)

**Controversies**: child abuse at ISKCON gurukulas (schools) came to light in the 1990s; post-Prabhupada succession conflicts, cult-of-personality dynamics around some initiating gurus. These are serious institutional failures separate from the theological tradition.

ISKCON today operates temples globally; prasadam distribution (Hare Krishna Food for Life) is one of the world's largest vegetarian food relief programs.

**The distinction**: Prabhupada took one specific lineage of Gaudiya Vaishnavism and exported it wholesale. ISKCON is Gaudiya Vaishnavism, but Gaudiya Vaishnavism is not only ISKCON — and bhakti is not only Gaudiya Vaishnavism.

## Bhakti's theological and social tensions

**Against caste**: the bhakti saints consistently insisted devotion is open to all regardless of birth. Raidas (cobbler-saint), Kabir (weaver), Tukaram (shudra) — the tradition valorizes devotion over purity status. In practice this was always contested; temple access and ritual authority remained caste-stratified.

**Against gender restriction**: female poet-saints (Andal, Mirabai, Janabai, Akka Mahadevi) are central to the tradition. Their devotion is often expressed through the bridal/erotic relationship with the divine, which simultaneously empowers and constrains — they transcend ordinary gender norms through the devotee-divine relationship.

**Bhakti and vernacular literature**: the bhakti movements are the engine of classical vernacular literary traditions in Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Marathi, Hindi, Bengali — Bhakti literature created/elevated these languages as vehicles for deep human expression.
