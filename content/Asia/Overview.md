# Asia Overview

## Scope
This section covers the major religious and philosophical traditions of South Asia, East Asia, Southeast Asia, Inner Asia, and the zones where these traditions overlap, compete, absorb one another, and reshape political order.

## Why Asia matters religiously
Asia is not just one region with many religions. It is one of the main civilizational engines of global religion.
This is where you get:
- the Vedic-to-Hindu civilizational complex
- Buddhism in multiple philosophical, monastic, devotional, and tantric forms
- Chinese religious synthesis involving Daoism, Confucian traditions, Buddhism, and local cults
- Shinto in Japan
- Sikhism in Punjab
- large-scale interaction among statecraft, ritual, cosmology, meditation, law, and sacred geography

The category "Asian religion" is therefore useful only as a library shelf, not as a single interpretive system.

## Major regional clusters
### South Asia
- Vedic and post-Vedic religious development
- Hindu traditions in their plural forms
- Buddhism's origin zone
- Jain and Sikh worlds (with Sikhism currently covered here first)
- major traditions of ritual, asceticism, sacred text, pilgrimage, and philosophical schools

### East Asia
- Chinese religious worlds: Daoism, Confucian lineages, Buddhism, local cults, ancestor practice, spirit and temple systems
- Japan: Shinto, Buddhism, shrine-temple interaction, imperial myth, and modern state restructuring
- Korea and Vietnam would later warrant dedicated notes if the shelf expands further

### Inner and Southeast Asia
- Buddhist adaptation in Theravada and Vajrayana zones
- local spirit religions, kingship cults, and layered religious coexistence
- important cases of syncretism, sacred kingship, and state-temple relations

## Core themes worth tracking
- rebirth, karma, liberation, and awakening
- ritual purity and sacred order
- meditation, contemplation, and inward discipline
- temple networks and monastic institutions
- sacred text plus oral and ritual transmission
- divine plurality, divine presence, and non-theistic paths existing in the same civilizational space
- state-religion interaction, especially through kingship, imperial ideology, and monastic authority

## Research lenses that help here
- do not force all Asian traditions into a "religion as belief" model
- distinguish philosophy, temple cult, ethics, meditation, law, and folk practice
- watch how traditions coexist without merging cleanly
- pay attention to the relation between monastic orders and household religion
- treat state restructuring seriously, especially in Japan, China, and South Asian empires

## Good working summary
Asia is one of the deepest religious macro-regions on earth. It contains traditions organized around gods, ancestors, meditation, ritual, law, cosmology, awakening, sacred kingship, and disciplined practice. The main analytical mistake is to expect one religious template to fit all of it.
